Introduction

There are several structural design philosophies, including working stress and limit state design. Limit states has performance evaluated against a set of limiting performance conditions. This is a probabilistic approach. 3 three limit states are ULS for strength and stability, SLS for deflection and vibration, and FLS forr performance under cyclic loading.

ULS is commonly calculated with ϕR≥∑αiSi\phi{R}\geq{\sum{\alpha_iS_i}}ϕR≥∑αi​Si​. For SLS, live loads are calculated with Δ=L360\Delta=\frac{L}{360}Δ=360L​, total loads with Δ=L180\Delta=\frac{L}{180}Δ=180L​, and vibration such that f is greater than 8 Hertz.

Permanent dead loads come from self-weight and superimposed dead load from finishes or suspended allowances. Transient loads include occupancy, snow, wind, earth pressure, hydrostatic pressure, earthquake, or rain.

The value ϕ\phiϕ is based on uncertainty of the material and establishes a uniform reliability level for design.

Wind loads are laminar across the roof, creating lift, but apply pressure to the side of a building. Thus, forces are negative on the top but positive on the sides. In bridges, auroelastic responses are analysed.

  • Seismic problems develop due to the inertia of the building. Thus, this is NOT A LOAD.
  • Rain-ponding creates roof failures
  • Temperature-induced failures are major considerations for bridges, which naturally shrink and swell
  • Loads that are not in the code, like kids climbing a sculture, must be considered

The codes provide the worst, sensible case for "standard reliability". These are based on research from Carl Terpstra, the first Ph.D student at Waterloo. The worst-case of these sensible cases are used for design. See ULS and SLS load combinations, respectively, with D=dead, L=live, S=snow, W=wind, and E=earthquake.

Sometimes, dead load is reversed if the dead load is acting in the opposite direction of other loads. Certain loads are not factored. Earthquake isn't factored because it is predicted anyway, and thus it is inherently factored. Serviceability load combinations aren't factored because they do not put life at risk. The only actual SLS code requirement is thatwind lateral drift is less than H500\frac{H}{500}500H​. However, SLS consideration must be made on the basis of the intended use of the building/member and to limit damage to the structure itself.

The importance factor is based on the use and occupancy of the building. Buildings of low importance have low direct or indirect hazard to human life, such as agricultural buildings. High importance includes post-disaster shelters (schools, community centres) and buildings that store hazardous materials. Post-disaster includes buildings essential to providing services following a disaster event, such as hospitals, police, fire, EMS stations, and critical infrastructure. Properties of wood

There are 350 M hectares of wood in Canada, 10% of global wood. Forestry not really a problem; in 2016, 760000 harvested, 15M damaged by insects, 3.4 by firest fire (2017). It also contributed $24.6B to the GDP and 210k jobs. Half of Canada's forests are certified sustainably managed, and 94% is crown land.

Wood burns and rots. When built to code, timber buildings must have the same reliability as buildings constructed with other systems and materials. In comparison, steel rusts and conrete sufferes from freeze-thaw and rebar rust

Wood is also lightweight, strong, durable, insulating, relatively inexpensive, and plentiful, beautiful, the ONLY renewable construction material, and biophilic. It performs well in caustic, seismic, and fire. Timber has the lowest long-term impact on resources in the industry.

The wood layers form rings. Early wood (spring growth) is greater that latewood (winter growth), which is harder and denser. The earlywood is specifically bigger in softwoods.

Wood is orthotropic: it is not isotropic, and has different properties in different principal directions

Wood is hygroscopic: changes dimension depending upon the amount of moisture in the cells. This impacts the strength and stiffness, and has a great impact on serviceability considerations due to volumetric Δ\DeltaΔ. Drier wood means stronger and stiffer. In a kiln, it is tried to 19deg⁡19\deg19deg, or S-Dry. At 22%, wood rots. Some cracks can form hwile drying because wood shrinks more tangentially than radially, and barely shrinks longitudinally.

Moisture starts by filling the cell walls. Once 28% moisture, or the FSP has been reached, it fills the straws. Thus, volume changes only happen in the first 28%. The house moisture content is typically 8-13% and changes seasonally. Connections must accommodate shrinking and swelling of wood. Additionally, if formwork is stuck in concrete, it will swell and break it.

  • radial shrinkage: 3-6%
  • tangential shrinkage: 5-12%
  • longitudinal shrinkage: 0.1-0.3%

Wood is either quarter-sawn or flat sawn. Quarter sawn is stronger because it is more stiff, and thus doesn't bend as much. However, it's more expensive than flat sawn. See below:

3 directions: Longitudinal, or along the grain (L); radial, or perpendicular to growth and to annual growth rings (R); tangential, or perpendicular to grain and tangential to the growth rings (T). For engineering purposes, group R and T together.

Timber sizings are smaller than actual because they are dressed. See below for sizings, found in WDM-V2-9 (page 917)

Wood is graded visually for knots, cracks, wane, and grain-direction (NLGA) and is also machine stress rated (MSR) or machine evaluated (MEL).

Glulam: graded in two ways: stress and visual. Stress graded based on bending/compression usage. Often, knots are removed from the bottom (in tension), somewhat removed from the top (in compression), and not really removed in middle. This is because wood fails in tension more than compression.

Plywood: dimensionally stable due to perpendicular alternating grain directions, which resist shrinkage

Parallel Strand Lumber (PSL): proprietary add more

Laminated Veneer Lumber (LVL): add

Laminated Strand Lumber: basically structural OSB. Expensive replacement for light-framing

I-Joist: reliable replacement for sawn lumber, has commercial applications

Cross laminated timber: dimensionally stable

Sawn timber: only available in S-Grn

Member strength is classified by the following:

  1. Lumber or heavy timber
  2. Species
  3. Grade
  4. Strength

Sawn timber fails in longitudinal shear, or shear parallel to grain. This happens at the bond between the "straws". Timber also buckles according to euler buckling Pe=π2EIL2P_e=\frac{\pi^2EI}{L^2}Pe​=L2π2EI​. For buckling, E05E^{05}E05, or the bottom 5th percentile E, is used. Service Condition factor KSK_SKS​: Found in Table 6.10. It is based on the moisture impact on timber strength. This is impacted by:

  • material (glulam, sawn lumber)
  • size of sawn lumber
  • characteristic being considered (shear or bending)
  • exposure to wet conditions (eg. deck, footbridge)
  • for big timber, not only is it already high moisture, but the middle doesn't raining. This gives it higher strength parallel to grain. However, compression perpendicular to grain is the same for lumber and timber because same wet surface
  • Longitudinal shear for bending, fracture shear considers notches

Treatment factor KTK_TKT​: Found in Table 6.11. Preservative treatment doesn't impact strength (except fire retardant), but incising does. Typically 4x and bigger are incised and 2x or lower are not

System factor KHK_HKH​: Found in Table 6.12. Since not all members are the worst piece of wood, some strength is given back.

  • allows for increased capacity when considering 3+ members no more than 610mm apart (share load)
  • accounts for sheathing and fastener composite action
  • applies to the joist system, stud wall, and built-up beam

Size factor KzK_zKz​: Found in Table 6.13 (not for glulam). Larger volume of a member will tend to reduce the apparent strength - a phenomenon that exists to some extent in all materials but is more prounounced in timber. This is because there is a greater chance of a "fatal flaw".

  • depends upon the type of member and the characteristic property being considered.

KLK_LKL​ is the lateral stability factor. This accounts for lateral, torsional buckling. Timber is moreso analogous to concrete beams than to steel considering LTB. LTB is only considered for bending about the strong axis. LTB is only a problem if certain width-depth ratios are not met, or side rotation is not restrained. This factor is taken as 1 when there is full lateral support at bearing points and that the max depth-width ratio doesn't exceed:

  • 4:1 - held in line at supports
  • 5:1 - member held in line by purlins or tie rods
  • 6.5:1 - compressive edge is held in line by direct connection of decling or joists spaced not more than 610mm apart o/c
  • 7.5:1 - compressive edge is held in line by direct connection of decking or joists spaced not more than 610 mm apart and adequate bridging or blocking is installed at intervals not exceeding eight times the depth of the member
  • 9:1 - both edges held in line

Connections

Connection: an interface between 2 individual members where forces are transferred from one member to the other

Different types include:

  • Bearing
  • Connectors, such as tension or shear
  • Glue

Connections in wood design frequently control the size of the members. Connections increase the cost, as well as weaken the structure.

Wood also changes dimension depending upon the amount of moisture in the cells.   1. Determine forces and load effects due to the lateral (wind or seismic) loads
