Need for rehabilitation

Reasons for rehabilitation are as follows:

  • Consequences of structure failures can be very high
  • Infrastructure os deteriorating gradually for various reasons
  • Damage can happen in extreme loading events, like overload, impact, fire, floods, and climate change
  • Construction errors leading to major issues
  • Accidents happen (like the Burlington Skyway)
  • Building use changes over time! Like the Lang Tanning Company converted to teh offices in Waterloo

However, our budgets don't allow it. See below:

Rehabilitation is more than just strengthening. It could also mean providing alternative load paths, replacing damaged elements, stabilizing, softening, post-tensioning, additions, repurposing, posting load limits, reducing dead load, corrosion protection, surrface treatment, damping, and straightening.

Process of effective repair (concrete example):

  1. Seeing an effect (cracking, leakage, settlement, spalling)
  2. Determining a cause
    • Defect in: design, materials, construction
    • Damage from: overoad, vehicle impact, chemical spill, earthquake, fire
    • Deterioration from: freeze-thaw cycles, alkali-aggregate reaction, reinforcing bar corrosion
  3. Decide whether or not the problems need to be repaired
    • Cost
    • Public Safety
    • Loss of function
  4. Condition survey: performed to determine the extent and nature of damage. Steps include:
    1. Visual inspection
    2. Review of engineering data
    3. Detailed condition survey (mapping of various deficiencies, monitoring, non-destructive testing)
    4. Final evaluation
    5. Report
  5. Repair Analysis (Quantify the problem). Considers the owner (cost, urgency, and life-cycle) and the engineer (structural considerations, repair environment, safety, function). Looks at method of repair, material selection, and other elements like temporary support
  6. Selecting a repair method to address the cause of observed damage, and accomplish repair

Limit States Design

An engineer designing a new type of structure should follow the following:

  1. Select a structural system for the particular purpose.
  2. Identify all modes of failure or ways in which the structure might fail to fulfil its intended purpose.
  3. Determine mathematical relationships between the loads and each of the potential modes of failure.
  4. Determine the properties of the materials and loads which are required in these relationships.
  5. Select a reasonable level of safety.
  6. Analyze an idealized model of the structure.
  7. Interpret the results of the structural analysis in terms of the actual structure.
  8. Proportion the structural members.

Limit states design is a design process that involves:

  1. Identification of all potential modes of failure (limit states).
  2. Determination of acceptable levels of safety against occurrence of each limit state.
  3. Consideration by the designer of the significant limit states.

Limit states include:

  1. Ultimate Limit States-related to a structural collapse of part or all of the structure. Such a limit state should have a very low probability of occurrence since it may lead to loss of life and major financial losses.
  2. Damage Limit States-related to damage of the structure such as cracking or spalling of the concrete. Since there is less danger of loss of life, a higher probability of occurrence can be tolerated than in the case of the ultimate limit states.
  3. Serviceability or Functional Limit States-related to disruption of the functional use of the structure

The three primary reasons for safety factors include:

  1. Strengths of materials may be less than expected
    • Variability
    • Speed of Testing
    • In situ Strengths vs. Specimen Strengths
    • Shrinkage stress or residual stress
    • Fabrication errors
    • Simplified assumptions and equations
    • Discrete sizes vs actual sizes
  2. Overloads
    • Variations in size of members, densities and material properties, and structural and non-structural alterations
    • Uncertainties in load effects
  3. Consequence of failure
    • Cost of replacing building
    • Loss of life
    • Cost to society in lost time, lost revenue, or loss of property

Shortcomings of load and resistance factors

  1. Procedure is as good as the data used in the solution
  2. The procedure assumes that specific probabilities of failure can be evaluated
  3. Failure of the structure is assumed to occur when one cross section or element reaches its capacity. For a weakest-link structure such as a truss. failure will occur when the weakest of many elements is overloaded. For a ductile indeterminate structure, loads will be distributed from section to section before the entire structure fails. Our solution will tend to overestimate the safety of the first case and underestimate the second
  4. Only failure by known overloads has been considered. such causes of failure as gross errors, fire, explosion, etc. have not been considered

Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo Simulation (MCS): statistical simulation modelling applied to determine probability of failure PfP_fPf​

There are 4 steps to MCS (example):

  1. Generate random trial values for the input parameters (like material strength, live load etc.)
  2. Check if G(Zˉ)<0G(\bar{Z})<0G(Zˉ)<0 (failure)
  3. Repeat steps 1 and 2 N times (N is number of trials)
  4. Probability of failure is equal to the number of failures nfn_fnf​ divided by the total number of trials N pf=nfNp_f=\frac{n_f}{N}pf​=Nnf​​

The required number of trials depends on how small pfp_fpf​ is and the level of confidence desired in the results. Various researchers have proposed formulas, such as:

  2. The recovery of the structure on completion of the load test is assessed and is acceptable

Bridge Inspection

Damage identification/evaluation observes the scientific method

  1. Observe damage
  2. Form hypothesis
  3. Test hypothesis
  4. Determine most likely causes

Inspection is done to document the type of repair and extent of deterioration, as well as to investigate the cause of deterioration and provode guidance on the appropriate remedial action. Buildings are less frequently inspected than public works (bridges every 2 years) due to their building envelope protection. The condition survey below is followed:

Frequency of inspection depends on:

  1. Class of structure
    • Class 1: failure of the structure would have catastrophic consequences or the structure is of vital importance
    • Class 2: failure might cost lives or structure is of considerable importance
    • Class 3: failure is unlikely to be fatal and a period of time with the structure out of service could be tolerated
  2. Environment and loading
    • Very severe: environment is aggressive and there are cyclic or fatigue loads
    • Severe: environment is aggressive with static loading, or environment is normal with cyclic or fatigue loading
    • Normal: environment is normal with static loading
  3. Structural appraisal: Does the present condition mean that safety is impaired? What are the causes for the structure’s present state?

Rebar Corrosion

The most common cathodic reactions result in hydroxyl ion production. High concentrations of this is known as alkalinity. Additionally, iron ions may react with water to create hydrogen ions (acidity). The potential difference (voltage) is the driving force for electrochemical reaction; a more positive potential results in a higher oxidation state and more release of electrons. The potential difference, or reaction driving force, can be due to:

  • nonuniformity of metal concentration
  • nonuniformity of electrolyte
  • variations in physical condition
  • damage in protective film
  • areas of unequal stress or deformation
  • flow of water

The concrete pore solution may contain ions such as sodium, potassium, calcium, hydroxyl, and sulphate in pores and larger concrete defects. Concrete also generally has a high pH value (alkalinity). The most stable products of reaction between steel, water, and oxygen are insoluble oxides; a film of a metal-oxide layer covers the steel and presents a barrier to further corrosion.

For corrosion to happen, the following are required:

  1. Galvanic couple: cathode and anode
  2. Electrical circuit: metallic and electrolytic continuity between anode and cathode
  3. Moisture
  4. Oxygen

The reaction order is as follows:

  1. Cathodic reaction (reduction of oxygen) 0.5O2+H2O+2e−→2OH−0.5O_2+H_2O+2e^{-}\to{2OH^{-}}0.5O2​+H2​O+2e−→2OH−
    • The cathodic process cannot occur until sufficient supply of oxygen and water are available at the steel interface
  2. Anodic reaction (oxidation of iron) Fe→Fe+2+2e−Fe\to{Fe^{+2}}+2e^{-}Fe→Fe+2+2e−
    • The anodic process cannot occur until the passive iron oxide film is either removed due to acidity or made permeable by chloride ions
  3. Ferrous oxide Fe+2+2OH−→Fe(OH)2Fe^{+2}+2OH^{-}\to{Fe(OH)_2}Fe+2+2OH−→Fe(OH)2​
  4. Ferric hydroxide 4Fe(OH)2+O2+2H2O→4Fe(OH)3…2Fe(OH)3→Fe2O3H2O+2H2O4Fe(OH)_2+O_2+2H_2O\to{4Fe(OH)_3}\ldots{2Fe(OH)_3}\to{Fe_2O_3H_2O+2H_2O}4Fe(OH)2​+O2​+2H2​O→4Fe(OH)3​…2Fe(OH)3​→Fe2​O3​H2​O+2H2​O

Carbonation, or reduction in pH, reduces passivity of rebar. Chloride ingress, in which sufficient chlorides are present to destroy the passive layer around the steel, uses chloride ions as a catalyst to speed up reactions.

Carbonation procedure:

  1. CO2CO_2CO2​ gas dissolves in the pore water to form carbonic acid, which in turn reacts with calcium hydroxide (alkaline). CO2+H2O+Ca(OH)2→CaCO3+H2OCO_2+H_2O+Ca(OH)_2\to{CaCO_3}+H_2OCO2​+H2​O+Ca(OH)2​→CaCO3​+H2​O
  2. The alkalinity of concrete (pH) is reduced below 10, and the passive layer on the steel is thus destroyed
  3. When the steel is depassivated, corrosion begins, if moisture and oxygen gain access into the concrete.

Carbonation is common when concrete cover is small. Wet and dry cycling can accelerate carbonation by allowing CO2CO_2CO2​ in during the dry phase, and allowing water to dissolve it in the wet phase. CO2CO_2CO2​ is promoted in industrial atmospheres. Carbonation is also promoted in older buildings with concrete of low cement content. Quality concrete has slow carbonation. Since carbonation requires contant change in humidity from damp to dry, carbonation will not occur if constantly submerged under water. Carbonation can be assessed with the phenolphtalein test to check pH (purple if pH > 9)

The corrosion process initiated at time t when carbonation fromt reaches steel (passive layer becomes unstable). Rate of carbonation is below, with k being carbonation rate (depends on concrete/environmental combination):

Carbonation Depth (x)=kT\textrm{Carbonation Depth (x)}=k\sqrt{T} Carbonation Depth (x)=kT​

In chloride-induced corrosion, Cl ions cause steel to corrode (with sufficient oxygen and moisture conditions) - it is believed that Cl incorporate into the passive film, replace some of the oxygen, and increase solubility, permeability, and ionic conductance, such that passive film looses protective character. Chloride ions don't occur uniformly; there will be random imperfections in the passive film. Thus, pitting corrosion is the problem. Additionally, Cl presence near the rebar can increase the anodic electrode reaction by as much as 1000 times. Amount of Cl required for corrosion depends on pH of concrete. Corrosion causes tensile forces; when these exist the concrete cracks and delaminates, which then promotes access of salt, oxygen, and moisture further to that location. Chlorides are found in:

  • Internal sources: admixtures, sea water in mix, and contaminated aggregate
  • External sources: sea water wetting and de-icing salts

Corrosion leads to discoloration, spalling/cracking, and loss of steel. Cracking and spalling depends on the cover, tensile strength, bond between rebar and concrete, diameter of bar, and degree of corrosion. Ways to minimize corrosion:

  1. Insulate concrete surface from corrosive environment
  2. Remove Cl or corrosive elements of concrete
  3. Control electron flow within the environment so that no metal is lost from the surface
  4. Confine the concrete with ties or stirrups (improves resistance to tensile stresses caused by corrosion and keeps cracks closed)

Also:

  • Cover quality (low w/c ratio, curing) and depth
  • Membranes and sealers for concrete surface
  • Corrosion inhibitors
  • Non-corrosive reinforcement
  • Non-ferrous reinforcement
  • Coating on rebar
  • Cathodic protection

Half cell potential mapping

A half cell electrode placed on the surface of concrete shows the difference in electrical potential between anode and cathode. The following procedure is followed:

  1. A sound electrical connection ism ade to reinforcement
  2. An external reference electrode is placed in a wet sponde on the concrete surface
  3. Potential readings are taken with high impedance voltmeter (positive terminal of voltmeter attached to the reinforcement, negative to half cell)
  4. If bar is corroding, the excess electrons in the bar will flow from the bar to the half cell. This is indicated by a negative voltage

The half cell is typically a copper bar imersed in copper sulphate solution. Half cell makes electrical contact with concrete by means of porous plug and sponge moistened with a wetting solution

While half cell mapping provides information on corrosion extent, it doesn't about corrosion rate. It's information should be used in conjection with other data like chloride content, depth of carbonation, and exposure conditions. Generally, it is done in grids so that the corrosion can be plotted. The following indicates risk of corrosion:

Fatigue

There are three stages of fatigue failure:

  1. Crack initiation: local slip at stress concentrations
  2. Crack propagation: crack growth per cycle
  3. Final failure: rapid failure once crack reaches critical size

Hot-Spot Stress Method

The hot-spot stress method is useful in situations where the detail category approach is unapplicable. It is similar to SN approach; a hot-spot stress is calculated. This is then compared to a design hot-spot S-N curve. The hot-spot stress σhs\sigma_{hs}σhs​ is the stress at the crack location, or "hot-spot". It includes stress raising effects of the detail, but excludes stress concentrations due to the local weld profile itself.

In welds, there are a variety of unique discontinuities. Planar discontinuities are caused by partial penetration (inadequate weld depth) and lack of fusion (between base and weld metal, or between multiple welds). Volumetric discontinuities can be caused by entrapped gases within the solidifying weld metal and inclusions of impurities in the metal. In addition, geometric discontinuities are caused by undercutting, misalignment, distortion, and excess slag. Hot spot stress required.

Evaluation and Inspection of steel structures

Heat straightening can be used to correct impact damage to steel. This involves applicatin of heat and restraint to straighten or realign steel members after impact damage. This effects flow stress (average of yield and ultimate stress) and modulus of elasticity as shown below:

General guidelines include a maximum strain ratio of 100 times yield strain; jacking, residual, and thermal stresses should not exceed half of room temperature yield strength; and temperatures should be less than 590 degrees Celcius

In inspecting steel structures, OSIM describes the following defects as the most common:

  • Corrosion
  • Permanent deformation
  • Cracking
  • Connection deficiencies

There are many non-destructive evaluation techniques, including Dye Penetrant Testing. Steps include:

  1. Clean surface
  2. Apply penetrant (bright coloured oil) and wait about half an hour
  3. Remove excess penetrant
  4. Apply developer agent (white chalky coating)
  5. View cracks, which will appear as bright lines due to developer drawing penetrant to the surface

Magnetic Particle Inspection is also done. When ferromagnetic materials are subjected to a magnetic field, they generate flux lines. Defects at right angles to the flux lines create distortions; some of the magnetic flux will leak out of the steel at the defect. The fine magnetic particles on the surface are attracted by the leakage field and form an outline of the defect.

Ultrasonic testing involves high frequency sound waves passing through material. These are reflected at interfaces such as flaws like cracks and places of porosity. Travel time of the returning pulses indicates distance to the interface. Defect orientaiton, size, shape and nature can all be determined in this method. it can detect discontinuities larger than half of hte signal wave length

Radiographic testing: based on x-rays or gamma rays penetrading solid materials. Radiation is absorbed when bassing through sound/thick metal, but it readily passes through cracks, defects and thin material

Acoustic emission techniques: active growing cracks cause a sound under traffic load; this can be received and monitored for growth over time. This is complicated and still experimental, but used mostly to monitor crack propagation of known cracks

Concrete deterioration

Plastic shrinkage can happen due to concrete drying too fast due to evaporation. It happens in roads and slabs due to the fact that they are very long in one direction and takes place between 30 min to 6 hours after pour.

Plastic settlement happens as a result of concrete bleeding creating voids at the top, which leads to cracks.

Corrosion cracks occur typically after 2 years; the presence of iron doesn't always mean that the crack was created by corrosion

Alkali aggregate reaction (AAR) cracks happen after more than 5 years. These mainly form in damp locations with reactive aggregate and high alkali cement

Some specific cracks include:

  • Crazing: network of fine cracks (occur between 1-7 days) due to inadequate curing; does not impact structural capacity
  • Early Thermal: happen over 1 day to 2-3 weeks

There are other symptoms of undesirable behaviour.

  • Scaling is local flaking or peeling away of the near surface concrete
  • Spalling is deeper surface imperfection extending to the top layers of reinforcing steel
  • *Delamination is separation along a plane parallel to a surface caused by corrosion of reinforcing steel or freeze/thaw, and is similar to scaling except that it applies to large area
  • Erosion is progressive disintegration of concrete caused by fluids or solids in motion
  • Seepage is movement of water through pores and cracks
  • Pop out is the breaking away of small portionso f surface due to localized internal pressure

Deterioration can be caused in three ways:

  1. Physical: shrinkage, thermal, freeze-thaw, erosion, fire, construction error
  2. Chemical: AAR, sulphate attack, acid attach
  3. Electrochemical: corrosion of steel, carbonation of concrete

Freeze-thaw occurs when the concrete is critically saturated. or 90% of pores are filled with water. Water to ice transformation causes a 9% volumetric expansion; thus, at that point, there will be no more room to expand and stresses may be introduced in concrete. There are two types of freeze-thaw deterioration:

  • Capillary-based freeze-thaw: fractured surface consists of broken "paste" and some undisturbed aggregate faces
  • Aggregate-based freeze-thaw: spalled section contains broken aggregate at the bottom of the fracture surface

See figure below:

Freeze-thaw is commonly seen through delamination, scaling, pop outs, and durability cracking (D-cracking). D-cracks are a series of cracks in concrete near and roughly parallel to joints, edges, and structural cracks. They are visually apparent, especially in pavement.

Freeze-thaw is directly proportional to porosity, moisture saturation, and # of freeze-thaw cycles, and inversely proportional to air entrainment. The rate increases if horizontal surface trap standing water or there is aggregate with small capillary structure and high absorption

To avoid freeze thaw:

  1. Use air entrainment
  2. Use high quality aggregate
  3. Use low w/c ratio
