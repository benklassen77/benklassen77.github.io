Introduction

Structural Concrete has been used throughout history. Old arches were always in compression, and thus made of concrete. In history, the pantheon was made of concrete. More recent history is:

  • 1824: portland cement invented
  • 1860: reinforced concrete invented
  • 1930: Robert Maillant Salgmatobel Bridge

Cement accounts for 5% of man-made CO2CO_2CO2​ emissions. 50% of the emissions are process emissions during clinker production. Another 40% come from the burning of fuels to heat the cement kiln, and 10% from electricity use and transportation. However, 80% of building CO2CO_2CO2​ is from electric utilities of the building over its life cycle.

There are two types of concrete. Structural is used to carry loads. This is almost always reinforced with steel or FPP. Such reinforcement can be non-prestressed, partially prestressed, or prestressed. Prestressed includes pretensioned, post-tensioned, the latter of which is then subdivided into bonded and unbonded. Non-structural is used for curbs, patios, architectural, and landscaping.

Concrete has architectural and structural advantages because it can be formed into various shapes. It has high rigidity. The stiffness and mass result in less vibration. There is also reusable in some situations.

However, concrete requires formwork and shoring, it has low strength per unit weight (10% strength of steel, density 30% of steel), and time dependent volume changes like creep and shrinkage. To be structurally compatible, steel and concrete need to be bonded together to transfer stresses/forces. Steel bars have surface deformations for this bond. Fortunately, the therman expansion (CTE) is similar between them (CTEconc=10x10−6/(°C)≈CTEsteel=12x10−6/(°C)CTE_{conc}=10x10^{-6}/(\degree{C})\approx{CTE_{steel}}=12x10^{-6}/(\degree{C})CTEconc​=10x10−6/(°C)≈CTEsteel​=12x10−6/(°C)). Additionally, since portland cement concrete has a high pH, steel will not corrode.

Flexure

There are several basic assumptions that ignore shear deformation used for design:

  1. Plane sections remain plane during bending
  2. Steel is bonded to concrete εs=εc\varepsilon_s=\varepsilon_cεs​=εc​
  3. Concrete stress-strain curve is non-linear
  4. Once section is cracked, tensile stresses are neglected for flexure
  5. Steel stress-strain curve is elastic-perfectly plastic

Concrete failures wih several modes: under-reinforced, over-reinforced, and balanced.

  • Under-reinforced: steel reinforcement yields before concrete reaches maximum strain εcu=0.0035\varepsilon_{cu}=0.0035εcu​=0.0035 Ductile failure at which εs>εy\varepsilon_s>\varepsilon_yεs​>εy​
  • Over-reinforced: concrete crushes (εcu=0.0035\varepsilon_{cu}=0.0035εcu​=0.0035) before steel yields. Brittle failure at which εs<εy\varepsilon_s<\varepsilon_yεs​<εy​
  • Balanced failure: strain in steel reaches εy\varepsilon_yεy​

Assumptions of flexural design

  1. Sections perpendicular to the axis of bending, which are plane before benging, remain plane after bending
    • Invalid for deep beams
  2. The strain in the reinforcement is equal to the strain in the concrete at hte same level
    • Necessary because they are bonded
  3. Stresses in the concrete and reinforcement can be computed from the strains using stress-strain curves for concrete and steel
  4. Tensile stress of concrete neglected in flexural strength calculations
  5. Concrete fails when compressive strain reaches limiting value 0.0035

Rectangular section with Compression Reinforcement

Compression reinforcement is used for several reasons:

  1. Reduced sustained load deflections: creep in the compression zone transfers load from the concrete to the compression steel, reducing concrete stress and reducing creep
  2. Change of mode of failure from compression to tension: prevents concrete from crushing and allows yield failure in steel to occur
