Introduction

Engineers design to ULS, SLS, and FLS. Ulimate limit state is concerned with safety. The 3 areas of concern are:

  • Strength: mostly concerned with components, such as members
  • Overturning: mostly concerned with the structural system and foundation
  • Sliding: foundation and the structure

Serviceability limit state is concerned with satisfactory, normal operation. The 3 areas of concern are:

  • Excessive deflection: most common; has safety issues
  • Excessive vibration
  • Excessive permanent deflection

Fatigue limit state is concerned with safety due to crack propagations. See my workterm report to learn more.

The load effect and the element resistance cannot be completely separate. Safety of the structure is a function of the size of the region of overlap between them. See below:

To lower the overlap, the resistance and the loads are factored such that ϕR≥αiSi\phi{R}\geq{\alpha_iS_i}ϕR≥αi​Si​, with ϕ\phiϕ being the resistance factor, R being the nominal resistance, α\alphaα being the load factor, and SiS_iSi​ being the load effect. Different load combinations are a result of:

  1. Material properties
  2. Cross-sectional shape
  3. Workmanship
  4. Analysis method - is it assumed rigid or pinned, since it is truly neither
  5. Load magnitude - variability is common
  6. Location of applied loads - eg. air duct location

Serviceability defines the allowable deflection (Cl 6.3.1). Vertical deflection is limited by L/240 or L/360 for LL and SL, with L being the span length. Lateral deflection is limited by H/400 or H/500 for wind load, with H being the building height. The P−ΔP-\DeltaP−Δ effect impacts lateral deflection. Vibration is currently considered with ASD (WSD in Canada) under Floor Vibration Annex E. It is particularly important for lightweight framing or long spans.

Stability is also considered based on the type of construction. For stability, several effects need to be considered, such as the following:

  • Deformation: structure, member, and connection
  • Geometric nonlinearity: second-order or P−ΔP-\DeltaP−Δ or p−δp-\deltap−δ
  • Geometric imperfection: Δ0\Delta_0Δ0​
  • Stiffness reduction due to inelasticity: residual stress
  • Uncertainty: strength and stiffness

Tension Members

Tension members are often rolled, built-up, or welded shapes. Rods, bars, and cables are often designed to be tension only. In a truss system. the elements facing toward the centre are in tension.

There are several failure modes of steel. In the below, the plot shows premature failure due to local fracture, failure if holes are not significant, and failure if the gross section is yielding.

Excessive elongation has large spread of yielding and deformation and gives warning. Rupture in net area can failure elastically, elastic-plastic, or have crack propagation. If there is a effective net area, or a failure plane, failure can happen in other ways. Shear forces are present parallel to the applied forec, and normal forces perpendicular to the applied force. Welds

In arc welding, two parts are fused together through the melting of metal at the joint between two parts. This is either a direct connection or through a filler metal, known as a consumable welding electrode. 4 common weld types are: complete penetration groove weld, partial penetration groove weld, fillet weld, and plug weld. Complete penetration welds bring members back to their original strength, in contrast to partial, because they restore the full cross-sectional area.

Fillet weld strength and ductility are dependent upon the angle of inclination θ\thetaθ. While strength increases with θ\thetaθ, so does the likehood of brittle failure. The ductile, longitudinal weld is the most advantageous. Factored resistance is shown below:

Bolts

Bolts are subject to shear, bending, tension, and compression. Stronger bolts take more shear stress, but can have less deformation before fracture. It is assumed that all bolts take equal portation of the load; however, tests show that the last row of bolts carries the largest portion of the load.

If pretensioning is not required: holes are aligned, bolts inserted, and nuts are turned until all plies in a joint are in firm contact. This is the snug-tight method. It is accomplished with a spud wrench, or an air-driven wrenh corresponding to the first impact of the wrench. If pretensioning is required: minimum fastener tension is equal to 70% (TBC) of the specified minimum tensile strength of the bolts. Pretension induced as nut is turned against the gripping material and bolt elongates. The procedure for this turn-of-the-nut method is as follows: bolts are brought to snug-tight condition and then given the following additional turns:

  • 12\frac{1}{2}21​ turn if grip length is between 4-8 bolt diamaters and less than 200mm
  • 13\frac{1}{3}31​ turn if "short-grip bolt" grip length is below 4 bolt diameters
  • 23\frac{2}{3}32​ turn if "long-grip bolt" grip length more than 8 bolt diameters

To simplify the turn-of-the-nut process, some bolts have a head that breaks off automatically at 70% strength. Thus makes it easy to determine exactly when the nut pretension requirement is satisfied.

Load Transfer Mechanisms

Bolts in a single lap connection are subjected to shearing across the single surface - the plates pull up against the fasteners. As the plates pull up against the fasteners, failure can occur in 1 of 4 ways:

  1. Plate tears through the net section with combination of shear and tension (Block shear)
  2. Bolt can shear at the interface of the two plates
  3. Plate can shear out between the end fastener and the end of the plate (Can govern bolt capacity.. Block shear with net tension area zero and gross shear area equal to twice the end distance times plate thickness)
  4. Plate immediately adjacent to a bolt can pile up and/or yield as a result of the contact force between the plate and the bolt (Plate bearing failure.. governs capacity of thin plates. Happens when non-pretensioned bolts are used or frictional resistance from pretensioned bolts has been overcome)

Slip-critical connections are pretensioned bolts, with which the frictional resistance from the pretensioning is what holds the plates together. Once this has been exceeded, the plates "slip" and it becomes a bearing connection. Appropriate situations for slip-critical connections include connections under repetitive or impact loading (bridges, not buildings).

  • If bearing-type connection, must be designed at factored load levels
  • If slip-critical connection, must be designed at specified load levels (against slip) and factored load levels (as a bearing-type)

There are 3 typically-assumed moment-resisting systems:

  1. Non-sway (braced frame): lateral load-resisting system is bracing and shearwall
  2. Rigid beam-to-column connection: no lateral displacement at joints. Beam also protices rotational restrain at column ends
  3. Tension-only bracing: can only restrain lateral movement

The majority of columns aren't perfectly straight and elastic, with joint translation and rotation as either free or fixed. Boundaries have partial fixities. Additionally, not all columns reach their critical load simultaneuosly (can be a good assuption for efficiently designed structures and gravity loads for the critical case of checkerboard type loads).

  • If the beams at the end are connected through shear connections, the beams act like bracing and don't provide any rotational restraint. PINNED END
  • If the beams are connected to column with rigid connection, beam provides rotational rigidity to column end. Amount of rigidity depends on the relative stiffness of the beams and the columns at the ends.

For combined compression and bending, strength of member is limited by either:

  1. Local buckling
  2. Yield failure
  3. General buckling (flexural or LT)

Connections can cause problems. Simple connections can induce bending by an eccentricity. Axial action dominates with minor interaction with bending. Generally, if the flange is not connected to the column, it is a pin connection; the # of bolts doesn't make it fixed. Rigid connections have significant combined interaction between axial compression and bending moment. Cross-sectional strength established by attaining:

  • Plastic moment capacity + capacity for inelastic rotation (Class 1)
  • Plastic moment capacity (Class 2)
  • Yield moment capacity (Class 3)
  • Elastic buckling capacity (Class 4)
