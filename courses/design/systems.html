Introduction

Structure: is “a device for channeling loads that result from the use or presence of the building in relation to the ground.”

System: is a collection of interrelated and interacting components that work together in an organized manner to fulfill a specific purpose or function

Structural System: is an assembly of interrelated and interacting structural components or elements that work together to fulfill structural functions

System Design: represents a systematic process for defining the problem, establishing alternative concepts and developing design solutions

Here are the phases of design:

  1. Conceptual Design
    1. Functional requirements
    2. Serviceability/performance requirements
    3. Construction requirements
    4. Load Evaluation
  2. Preliminary Design
    1. Structural systems and preliminary design
  3. Final Design
    1. Structural Analysis
    2. Proportioning of members and elements
    3. Specifications, structural drawings and contracts
  4. Construction and O&M
    1. Fabrication
    2. Construction
    3. Inspection and operation

Architectural challenges are to create building forms that fulfill the functional requirements, yet are aesthetically and economically successful. Structural challenges are to create structural forms that serve the functional requirements while supporting the imposed loads efficiently.

The following is a list of structural subsystems:

Subsystem Purpose Components
Horizontal Distribution distribute vertical (gravity) loads to vertical support system Roof and floor systems
Lateral Distribution distribute lateral loads to lateral support system Roof and floor systems
Vertical Support transfer vertical (gravity) loads from horizontal distribution system to foundation Columns, walls, etc.
Lateral Support transfer lateral loads from lateral distribution system to foundation Columns, walls, braces,etc.
Foundation transfer all vertical and lateral loads to ground

Lateral loads are transferred by either a rigid frame (joint stiffness), a braced frame (joints braced), or shear walls (walls infilled). The horizontal element both provides stiffness and transferred the load to the vertical element.

A braced frame is generally more efficient. For structures in seismic zones, the requirement to resist large lateral forces while undergoing large lateral deformation suggests use of ductile moment-resisting frame. Very tall buildings with large wind loading are more efficient with shear walls and tubular systems.

Dynamic forces vary w/ time, magnitude, direction, and position while static forces only have gradual variation (no inertial force). See breakdown below:

As a building height increases, so does the wind loading. Shorter buildings weight are dominated by the horizontal planes, but taller buildings weight are dominated by the vertical elements, as they must be larger to accomodate the wind loading.

The code also specifies some conservative point loads. The roof maintenance point load is applicable for a worker with tools. The floor with vehicle point load represents a vehicle jack taking the car's weight.

Live loadings

Specified UDL and concentrated loads are in the code. Roof surface specified concentrated LL is like a work man w/ tools; a vehicle area load is like a vehicle resting on a jack.

There are also allowances for live load reductions. Note that no LL reduction should be applied to wooden or sheet metal decks, precast units, or one-way slabs due to uncertainty of the lateral distribution

Wind load

Wind generates pressure on wall elements and suction on roof elements. Wind impacted by wind attributes (such as magnitude of sustained velocities, duration of high-level velocities, mass density of air, presence of gust effects, swirling etc), the structural attributes (size, aerodynamic shape, stiffness), and the surrounding environment (sheltering and funnelling). The standardized static procedure assumes the following:

  • Boxy or bulky buildings result in a typical aerodynamic response
  • Closed, fairly smooth surfaces on buildings
  • Buildings fit snuggly to ground (negates drag)
  • Stiff structures limit natural period variation

Wind load is a combination of lateral pressure and uplift. See below:

End-zone width z is the lesser of 10% of the least horizontal dimension and 40% of height, H, but not less than 4% of the least horizontal dimension or 1 m

3 approaches for wind load determination:

  • Static Procedure (simplified equivalent static load): used for low and medium height structures and cladding
  • Dynamic Procedure: used for tall buildings and slender structures. Differs from static procedure in the determation of gust factor, exposure factor, as well as detailed looks at wind turbulence and building vibration/damping
  • Experimental Procedure: used for special structures as it is costly

Seismic

Seismic design has 3 limit states:

  1. SLS: minor earthquakes occurring frequenrlt during the structure's life cycle without damage. Resonse should be elastic. Control of lateral displacements will govern design
  2. DCLS: moderate earthquakes which have a low probability of occurrence during life of structure. Damage control also referred to as continuity of operations limit state. Some minor plastic deformation allowed, but damage is primarily elastic. Damage shouldn't threaten the integrity of the structure but may be for aesthetic/functional reasons etc. Limit state marks the boundary between damage which can be repaired economically and damage that annot be repaired economically or at all
  3. CPLS: collapse prevention applies for the largest earthquake expected for a particular location. Plastic deformation, lots of damage, but no loss of life ideally

The seismic force resisting system (SFRS) must be designed for 100% of the earthquake load and effects. Therefore, all structural framing elements that are not part of the SFRS must be shown to behave elastically or to have sufficient non-linear capacity to support their gravity loads while undergoing earthquake-induced deformations

Stiff elements that are not considered part of the SFRS, like concrete, masonry, brick or precast walls or panels, shall be either separated from all structural elemts such that no interaction takes place as the building undergoes seismic deflection, or added to the SFRS.

  • Stiffness imparted to the structure from elements not part of the SFRS, other than those mentioned previously, shall be accounted for as follows:
    • In calculating the period of the structure for determining forces if the added stiffness decreases the fundamental lateral period by more than 15%
    • In determining the irregularity of the structure
    • In designing the SFRS if it has an adverse effect on the SFRS

Structural modelling shall be representative of the magnitude and spatial distribution of the mass of the building and of the stiffness of all elements of the SFRS. It shall account for:

  • cracked sections in reinforced concrete and masonry elements
  • sway effects arising from the interaction of gravity load with the displaced configuration of the structure
  • Other effects that influence the lateral stiffness

The higher mode factor (MvM_vMv​) transforms the minimal lateral force from a SDOF to MDOF problem, accounting for participation of higher modes in dynamic response.

Where the SFRS contains more than one type of system, the lowest product of RdRo must be used. Values of Rd recognize:

  • ability to absorb energy without excessive deformation or failure
  • presence of multiple or alternate load paths (redundancy)
  • ability of members and connections to undergo inelastic, cyclic deformations
  • contribution of non-structural components to damping

Values of Ro recognize:

  • difference between nominal and factored resistances
  • ratio of actual yield strength to minimum specified yield strength
  • effect of strain hardening
  • effect of mobilizing the full capacity of the structural system by the formation of a collapse mechanism

General Types of Lateral Systems

There are braced frames, rigid moment frames, and shear walls. To increase bending moment resistance, the greatest amount of material must be located as far as possible from the neutral axis. If a center core is used, stiff outrigger trusses connect the central core with external peripheral columns to increase moment resistance. The diagrid also places much material as far as possible from the neutral axis to provide high internal moment resistance. This has diagonal members connected by horizontal floor beams. These members carry gravity and lateral loads. The diagrid is connected to an internal core for more stiffness; however, a less rigid internal core is required with this system. Good at resisting shear deformation since it takes axial loads

In a rigid diaphragm, all lateral force-resisting elements are equally displaced. If the vertical elements all have the same lateral stiffness, the lateral force is distributed to them equally. If they have the same properties with the exception of length, the lateral force will be distributed according to the ratios of the length of each element to the total length. Stiffness influenced by:

  1. Construction material: plywood decks are quite flexible, and the deflection should be investigated when spans are large or span-to-depth ratios are high. Stiffness of metal decks is generally comparable with those of plywood decks. Metal decks with cast-in-place concrete fill tend to produce rigid diaphragms. Cast-in-place concrete decks provide the strongest and stiffest diaphragm. Precast units must be adequately attached to each other and support members for diaphragm action to develop. The attachments that are designed only for gravity loads are usually not adequate for lateral forces. In situations where the roof decking does not provide sufficient lateral support, such as some metal roof systems for low-rise buildings, the roof purlins have to be braced
  2. Continuity of the spanning diaphragm over number of supports
  3. Span-to-depth ratio of the diaphragm
  4. Effect of various special conditions, such as chord length changes, type and yielding of connections, and influence of large openings

Torsion arises due to asymmetrical building geometry, mass and stiffness, as well as eccentric forces. Torsion is created when the centre of mass of the floor doesn't coincide with the centre of rigidity (seismic loads) or when the resultant of the lateral loads does not pass through the centre of rigidity (wind loads). Torsion is best resisted by placing lateral force-resisting elements as far away from the centre of rigidity as possible, or using closed tubular systems.

The distribution of forces among vertical elements is essential. The following assumptions are made in the code analysis:

  1. Rigid diaphragms; they go through rigid body displacement
  2. Bending stiffness of planar vertical structure about the weak axis is neglected
  3. Torsional resistance of individual planar vertical elements is neglected
  4. Intersecting planar units are treated as separate; there is no shear flow around corners
  5. Shear flow along shear wallss is constant (based on average shear stresses)
  6. Hinged connections between the floor diaphragms and lateral force-resisting vertical planes are assumed. ∴\therefore∴ interaction of floor systems and shear walls is ignored

The first assumption is poor in the following circumstances:

  • Closely spaced shear walls in relatively narrow buildings are stiffer in comparison to the floor diaphragms
  • For low-rise buildings, the floor or roof diaphragms are often more flexible than the supporting shear walls (as in light wood-framed construction); therefore, for deep diaphragms (where shear deformations control) the diaphragms may be treated as flexible and supported by rigid walls
  • Floor diaphragms in long, narrow buildings with deep beam proportions of greater than 3:1 that span large distances across the building.
  • Floor diaphragms that are weakened by cut-outs and openings for atriums, escalators, stairs, etc., unless they are braced.
  • Wood and metal deck (without concrete fill) roofs as well as prefabricated floor systems without cast-in-place topping are to be treated as flexible, unless the floor or roof framing is braced to allow the planes to act as horizontal or inclined trusses

Composite Beam Construction

Composite implies that concrete and steel act together to resist imposed loading and deformation. For this to happen, longitudinal shear stresses are transferred between the concrete and steel components. If slip occurs (non-composite), each section develops a linear strain distributin having a neutral axis at the centroid of each section. If slip is prevented, fully composite action occurs and a single linear strain distribution is developed across the depto of the composite section. In order to develop composite action, shear forces must be transferred between the materials/sections. Stud/channel/angle/spiral connectors are all used.

The floor slab may has a constant thickness (solid slab). The slab is cast with temporary plywood or metal forms. However, it is more common for there to be stay-in-place steel formwork, referred to as steel form or steel deck. The form is different from the deck because it has only modest corrugations, and the deck has larger ribs (greater than 40% of overall slab thickness).
