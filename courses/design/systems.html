Introduction

Structure: is “a device for channeling loads that result from the use or presence of the building in relation to the ground.”

System: is a collection of interrelated and interacting components that work together in an organized manner to fulfill a specific purpose or function

Structural System: is an assembly of interrelated and interacting structural components or elements that work together to fulfill structural functions

System Design: represents a systematic process for defining the problem, establishing alternative concepts and developing design solutions

Here are the phases of design:

  1. Conceptual Design
    1. Functional requirements
    2. Serviceability/performance requirements
    3. Construction requirements
    4. Load Evaluation
  2. Preliminary Design
    1. Structural systems and preliminary design
  3. Final Design
    1. Structural Analysis
    2. Proportioning of members and elements
    3. Specifications, structural drawings and contracts
  4. Construction and O&M
    1. Fabrication
    2. Construction
    3. Inspection and operation

Architectural challenges are to create building forms that fulfill the functional requirements, yet are aesthetically and economically successful. Structural challenges are to create structural forms that serve the functional requirements while supporting the imposed loads efficiently.

The following is a list of structural subsystems:

Subsystem Purpose Components
Horizontal Distribution distribute vertical (gravity) loads to vertical support system Roof and floor systems
Lateral Distribution distribute lateral loads to lateral support system Roof and floor systems
Vertical Support transfer vertical (gravity) loads from horizontal distribution system to foundation Columns, walls, etc.
Lateral Support transfer lateral loads from lateral distribution system to foundation Columns, walls, braces,etc.
Foundation transfer all vertical and lateral loads to ground

Lateral loads are transferred by either a rigid frame (joint stiffness), a braced frame (joints braced), or shear walls (walls infilled). The horizontal element both provides stiffness and transferred the load to the vertical element.

A braced frame is generally more efficient. For structures in seismic zones, the requirement to resist large lateral forces while undergoing large lateral deformation suggests use of ductile moment-resisting frame. Very tall buildings with large wind loading are more efficient with shear walls and tubular systems.

Dynamic forces vary w/ time, magnitude, direction, and position while static forces only have gradual variation (no inertial force). See breakdown below:

As a building height increases, so does the wind loading. Shorter buildings weight are dominated by the horizontal planes, but taller buildings weight are dominated by the vertical elements, as they must be larger to accomodate the wind loading.

The code also specifies some conservative point loads. The roof maintenance point load is applicable for a worker with tools. The floor with vehicle point load represents a vehicle jack taking the car's weight.

Loadings

Live loadings

Specified UDL and concentrated loads are in the code. Roof surface specified concentrated LL is like a work man w/ tools; a vehicle area load is like a vehicle resting on a jack.

There are also allowances for live load reductions. Note that no LL reduction should be applied to wooden or sheet metal decks, precast units, or one-way slabs due to uncertainty of the lateral distribution

Wind load

Wind generates pressure on wall elements and suction on roof elements. Wind impacted by wind attributes (such as magnitude of sustained velocities, duration of high-level velocities, mass density of air, presence of gust effects, swirling etc), the structural attributes (size, aerodynamic shape, stiffness), and the surrounding environment (sheltering and funnelling). The standardized static procedure assumes the following:

  • Boxy or bulky buildings result in a typical aerodynamic response
  • Closed, fairly smooth surfaces on buildings
  • Buildings fit snuggly to ground (negates drag)
  • Stiff structures limit natural period variation

Wind load is a combination of lateral pressure and uplift. See below:

End-zone width z is the lesser of 10% of the least horizontal dimension and 40% of height, H, but not less than 4% of the least horizontal dimension or 1 m

3 approaches for wind load determination:

  • Static Procedure (simplified equivalent static load): used for low and medium height structures and cladding
  • Dynamic Procedure: used for tall buildings and slender structures. Differs from static procedure in the determation of gust factor, exposure factor, as well as detailed looks at wind turbulence and building vibration/damping
  • Experimental Procedure: used for special structures as it is costly

Pressure Coefficient CpC_pCp​ and CpiC_{pi}Cpi​ modifies reference velocity pressure to reflect the external or internal pressure on specific surfaces of the structure.

  • Category 1 −0.15≤Cpi≤0-0.15\leq{C_{pi}}\leq{0}−0.15≤Cpi​≤0: Building with no “large” openings but having small ones uniformly distributed amounting to less than 0.1% of the total surface area. The value of Cpi should be -0.15 except where that alleviates an external load, when Cpi = 0 should be used. Buildings in this category would include most buildings that are nominally sealed and ventilated mechanically, and other buildings such as windowless warehouses with door systems not prone to storm damage
  • Category 2 −0.45≤Cpi≤0.3-0.45\leq{C_{pi}}\leq{0.3}−0.45≤Cpi​≤0.3: Buildings in this category have large openings that can be relied on to be closed during storms and small openings uniformly distributed. Examples would include buildings which, although nominally sealed, may experience a significant imbalance due to the air leakage around doors and windows or through other small openings. Most high-rise buildings with operable windows and balcony doors would fall into this category
  • Category 3 −0.7≤Cpi≤0.7-0.7\leq{C_{pi}}\leq{0.7}−0.7≤Cpi​≤0.7: Buildings with “large” openings through which gusts are transmitted to the interior. Such buildings would include, sheds with one open side, and building with shipping doors, ventilators or the like which have a high probability of being open during a period of high wind. Structures required in post-disaster service should be capable of withstanding the consequences of failure of glass windows and thus should be included in Category 3
