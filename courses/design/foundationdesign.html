Course Overview

CIVE 354 covers the analysis, design, and construction aspects of foundations (particularly shallow foundations), slopes, and retaining structures. The main objective of CIVE 354 is to enable students to select the best foundation solution for different types of civil engineering structures. After completing the course, students should be able to design shallow foundations, slopes, and retaining structures for routine projects and supervise their constructions.

Geotechnical engineering is a combination of differenc sciences. Soil mechanics, rock mechanics, and engineering geology are fundamental sciences that contribute to design. Applications are made for foundation design, slope design, and retaining structures. Other applicaitions include landfill design, waste containment, and subsurface remediation and cleanup.

Geotechnical engineering: concerned with the use of earth materials for improving and defending society and life. It involves systematic application of scientific principles and techniques for constructions on, on, or with geomaterials.

Foundation engineering: process of solving problems that involve safe and economical transfer of structural loads to the ground

Foundations are transition elements between the superstructure and the ground that transmit loads. Of these, shallow foundations, such as footings or rafts, are built near the ground surface. Deep foundations, such as piles or caissons, penetrate deep into the ground. Shallow foundations are often created using reinforced concrete. Piles are slender column-like structures, made of reinforced concrete (cylindrical or square), steel pipes, or H sections. Single piles under each column or multiple piles are considered a pile group, which have a rigid slab on top, known as a pile cap. Shallow foundations transfer the load in horizontal directions, while deep foundations transfer it vertically. For deep foundations, transportation and manufacturing loads must be considered.

Retaining structures or retaining walls are built to create space - temporary ot permanent - for a definite purpose. A retaining wall is any constructed wall that holds back soil, liquid, or other materials where there is an abrupt change in elevation.

  • Gravity retaining walls: massive walls that work on the principle of gravity for stability
  • Embedded walls: slender walls that are embedded in ground for stability
    • Pre-formed walls: made of thin steel, timber, or concrete sheets that are driven into the ground. Also called sheet pile walls
    • In situ walls: constructed by excavating deep trehcnes or auger holes, placing reinforcement and filling with concrete - diaphragm walls and bored pile walls are popular examples
  • Composite or Hybrid Walls: composite construction using a variety of materials with a variety of support systems. They use both gravity and embedded walls

Wall construction of a gravity wall follows these steps: excavation, preparing the site, constructing the wall, and putting in backfill soil. For embedded walls, you could either drive a wall or dig a trench or auger hole and pour concrete and reinforcement. You could put props or anchors as necessary

There are two types of slopes: natural and artificial. On basis of extent, infinite and finite. Mountain and hill slopes are natural and infinite, while hill side cuttings for road construciton; embankments for roads, railways, and canels, and earth dams and mines are all artificial and finite

The selection is based on feasibility, constructability, availability of construction equipment, project-specific factors (eg. deadline), and environmental factors.

In general, design aims at stopping failures. Limit states are identified and prevented from being breached. A limit state is an unacceptable condition with respect to performance. If it is successful, a limit state is not reached or breached. Limit states separate desired states of a structure from undesired states.

Serviceability Limit States are a set of unacceptable conditions related to performance (excessive settlement, differential settlement or tilt). Excessive settlement leads to malfunctioning service lines, and excessive uneven settlement leads to building cracks

Ultimate Limit States are a set of unacceptable conditions related to Safety/Danger (collapse or breakage). Bearing capacity failure leads to plunging of foundation, and excessive settlement leads to complete collapse

A successful structure should pass strength, serviceability, constructability, economy, deformation, connection, utility, drainage, access, and aesthetic requirements add. To eurocode, specific requirements include serviceability, safety, fire, and robustness.

Working/Allowable Stress Design (WSD/ASD), or factor of safety design; Limit State Design (LSD), or load and resistance factor design (LRFD); reliability based design (RBD), or probabilistic estimation of safety against potential failures, to account for different uncertainties. Shear Strength - Actual Source

Cohesion is a fictitious quantity - no glue forces exist between dry soil particles

  • There is no cohesion in sands
  • There is no cohesion in clays; particles are kept together by surface tension (suction). There is a small amount of true cohesion in clays from the clay-water chemical interaction, but it is negligible practically
  • The apparent cohesion from surface tension and true cohesion from clay-water chemistry are prodominant at very low confining stresses (not significant to practical problems)

Shear resistance in soil originates from friction and dilatancy. Sometimes, resistance comes only from friction (pure friction) and sometimes from both friction and dilatancy. Particle sliding, interlocking, rolling, generates these two aspects of shear resistance. These resistances are quantified using different friction angles (multiple friction angles). Thus, the c−ϕc-\phic−ϕ method is old, but wrong

The magnitude of frictional resistance depends on the coefficient of friction, which is a property of the interface along which sliding occurs. By Amonton's Law of Friction, F=μNF=\mu{N}F=μN, with N being the normal force and F being the frictional force. Pure friction exists when particles slide or roll. FOr soils, sliding occurs along internal planes of failure within the soil element (like c−ϕc-\phic−ϕ curve). The friction coefficient is a property of the soil and it depends on the grain shape, size, size distribution, and surface roughness. This is an intrinsic property and is a constant for a given soil.

  • At the critical state, shear strength of soil originates from pure friction. Thus, there is no dilatancy. Therefore, the friction angle of soil at the critical state is unique (intrinsic property), and cohesion is "0" on the c−ϕc-\phic−ϕ graph

Dilation means size/volume increase. When particles are densely packed, they dilate (increase in volume) during shearing. This is true for dense sands and overconsolidated (also stiff or dense) clays. When particles are loosely packed, they contract (decrease in volume) during shearing. This is true for loose sands and normally consolidated clays See below:

Dilation occurs because densely packed particles have to climb over each other before they can shear. For loosely packed particles, such climbing is not necessary because of which they do not exhibit dilatancy. Thus, for dense soils, or dilatant soils, additional energy is required by the soil for the climbing action. Thus additional required energy has to come from the externally applied loads

  • Extra force or stress is required for failure or shearing to take place. Thus, soil exhibits additional resistance to shearing over and above its frictional resistance

Total stress analysis is applicable for CD triaxial tests (no pore pressure). Because if is very expensive to find pore pressure for clay, undrained analysis or total stress analysis are used to determine shear stress from σ1\sigma_1σ1​ and σ3\sigma_3σ3​ without calculating u.

Pore pressure is generated during shearing. In dense sands or overconsolidated clays, there is negative pore pressure during shearing, while in loose sands or normally consolidated clays, there is positive pore pressure. This is because the pore pressure resists the general movement of the grains. Thus, effective stress is less than total stress in contractive soils, but greater in dilative soils

Slobe Stability

There are different types of slope movements:

  • Falls: a mass of any size is detached from a steep slope or cliff - descends mostly through the air by free fall, leaping, bouncing, or rolling
  • Topples: one or more units of mass rotate forward about some pivot point, below or low in the unit - it is tilted without collapse
  • Slides: movement consists of shear strain and displacement along one or several surfaces that are visible or may reasonable be inferred
  • Spreads: the dominant form of movementis lateral extension. Movements may involve fracturing and extension of coherent material, either bedrock or soil, owing to liquefaction or plastic flow
  • Flows: slope movements may appear in several different forms

Slope movement based on:

  • Age
    • Contemporary movements are generally active and are relatively easily recognizable by their configuration. The surface forms produced by the mass movements are expressive
    • Dormant movements are usually covered by vegetation or are disturbed by erosion so that the traces of their last movements are not easily discernible
    • Fossil movements are generally developed in the Pleistocene or earlier periods, under different morphological and climate conditions, and cannot repeat themselves at present
  • Stage
    • Initial Stage is where you see the first signs of disturbance of equilibrium appear and cracks in the upper part of the slope develop
    • Advanced stage: the loosened mass is propelled into motion and slides downslope
    • Exhausted stage: the accumulation of slide mass creates temporary equilibrium equations

There are also different slopes

  • natural slopes and artificial slopes
  • infinite slopes and finite slopes

Fundemental requirement of stability: shear strength of the soil must be greater than the shear stress required for equilibrium. Therefore, instability often caused if the shear strength of soil is less than the required shear strength. Reached in two ways:

  • Decreasing shear strength of soil
  • Increasing shear stress applied to soil

These are caused by earthquake; rainfall; improperly applied loads on the slope; seepage through the slope; and change in soil properties from weathering, swelling, cracking, leaching, strain softening, creep, cyclic loading, seasonal changes in water table etc.

Site Investigation/Exploration

Goals of the investigation are to get a picture of the ground. With this, you establish soil stratigraphy and establish water table with piezometers

Factors controlling site investigation are decided by structure to be supported (size and importance, depth of influence of foundations), type and method of construction, nature of soil (weak vs. strong, permeable vs. impermeable), and geology

There are several types of site investigation methods. Borings, or cylindrical vertical holes, are made in the ground. Also, pits, trenches, in situ testing (CPT, SPT), and geophysical tests

The number of borings depends on the importance of the project. Minimum is 3 - for a rectangular plot, 5 are recommended as a minimum. You want to get a profile of the plot. More are required for sites with highly variable profiles; experience is important to know how many.

Borings should be extended to a depth at which stresses have diminished to ≈10%\approx{10\%}≈10% of the stresses applied at the base of the foundation. A few borings must extend to bedrock or to the competent (hard) layer

Boreholes are made by drilling (with drill bits or continuous flight auger) or with wash boring

Types of in situ tests include standard penetration test (SPT), cone penetration test (CPT), and vane shear test, as well as pressuremeter test, dilatometer test.

The standard penetration test (SPT) is the most widely used in situ test. It is crude - hammer a sampler into the ground and record the resistance. Done intermittently with drilling of bore holes. Sampler is called split-spoon-sampler

The number of blows is very important indicator of capacity and strength of soil. These are recorded at first 15 cm, 2nd 15 cm, and 3rd 15 cm. These are added together for N blows in first 45 cm

SPT Procedure:

  • Drilling of bore hole is interrupted at regular intervals
  • Split-spoon sampler is introduced into the bore hole
  • Sampler is drivin into the ground by dropping a hammer on top of the rods
  • Sampler is driven into the soil for an overall depth of 45 cm in intervals of 15 cm
  • Operator counts the number of blows required for each 15 cm penetration of the sampler
  • Sampler is then retrived back to the ground surface (soil samples collected in the smapler is stored for subsequent transportation to the lab)
  • Drilling of bore hole is resumed until the next SPT testing depth

SPT should be stopped at 50 blows required for any 15cm interval, or 100 for a 30cm interval. Finally, 10 successive blows with no advancement into the ground

Limitations of SPT:

  • lack of standardization
  • different hammers produce different driving energy
  • operator dependent (quality of operation, method of hammer release affects N)
  • conditions of rope, anvil, sampler, cathead affect N
  • Sampler type also affects N
  • Total string length, tightness of rod connections affect N
  • Borehole diameter affects N

Benefits:

  • quick
  • cheap

Tolerable Foundation Movement - SLS

Impairment of the function of the structure is subjective. Definition is dependent on balance between maintenance cost and cost of construction. Settlement based SLS is most commen, such that the expected/calculated settlement is less than or equal to the tolerable settlement

Settlement can disrupt functionality of the structure (SLS) or distort the structure so that some part of it may yield/collapse (ULS)

There are 3 types of settlement: uniform, non-uniform, and tilting (tall-structure)

Uniform Settlement is not the most common, and has minimal structural damage. Damage can come to utility/service lines, cause access problems and drainage problems.

Non-uniform Settlement causes structural damage through the cracking of beams/columns/slabs (crack width is an indicator of how dangerous the settlement is). Architectural damage results with the cracking of panel joints, door/window jamming, uneven floors. Damage happens at the interface of utility lines, access and drainage issues, and excessive tilting

Cracks are indicators of ULS and SLS, and are often formed in response to foundation movement. Their patterns give information about foundation movement. Characteristics of cracks caused by foundation movement:

  • these cracks may have any direction depending on the direction of tensile strain (cracks are perpendicular to tensile strain)
  • at a specific location in a wall, beam, column, cracks have the same direction. Different from web pattern observed in cracks caused by shrinkage, drying of plasters)
  • cracks appear on both sides of the wall, beams, columns and other elements
  • cracks will have similar patterns in all floors with intensity of cracking decreasing at higher floors

Shallow foundations

Foundation elements that transfer loads from structures to relatively shallow depths into the ground. Typically, they spread the load into the ground.

Shallow foundations advantages:

  • more economical than deep foundations
  • No specialized construction equipment required
  • Visual inspection of the soil at foundation base possible to make sure design assumptions match reality

Shallow foundations disadvantages:

  • excavations required for constructing shallow foundations may need additional support (increase cost of construction)
  • dewatering may be necessary if the water table is close to the ground surface
  • Not suitable in soft compressive soils (like peat), expansive clays, or unconcrolled fill materials
  • It is better not to construct shallow foundations close to property lines and obstructions; potential future disputes in case of damages caused by future constructions in adjacent plots

Isolated footings can be made from unreinforced concrete or masonry blocks, which can only take axial. Also made with pure concrete base - can take horizontal, vertical loads, and moments. 99% of these foundations will be reinforced. Square footings are preferred, or rectangular (if space constraints present), or sometimes circular in shape. However, "pedestals" are not reinforced

Combined footings: when applied, loads are relatively heavier so that individual footings would overlap to become one larger footing beneath both the columns. Trapezoidal footings are used if one of the two vertical loads are not equal or if moments act such that the resultant load acts through the centroid of the footing in plan. Reinforced concrete is used for construction

  • single footing typically supports two columns
  • The centroid of the footing and the centroid of the line of action of the load must coincide
  • must be triangular or rectangular
  • if an isolated footing would cross property line, or overlap with another, combined is used

Strap footing: when one isolated footing close to the property line becomes eccentrically loaded because of lack of space, then a tie beam is used to connect this footing to an adjacent footing to counter the resultant moment. Reinforced concrete is used for construction. It is basically two isolated footings connected by a beam. Preferred over combined footing if the distance between the two columns is relatively large

Strip footing: very long in one direction, placed beneath a series of columns or a wall. Plane strain condition exist - 1D is so large that a cross section can be used as a representative for analysis and design. Reinforced concrete is usually used in modern constructions. For greater load/column spacing ratios, isolated footings may be too close to each other or may overlap, and then single long footing supporting all columns is more economical. Also used to support load-bearing walls. Strip footings are reinforced. The L/B ratio must be greater than or equal to 10!!

Raft/Mat foundation: when there are multiple column loads such that individual footings would overlap, one big slab is used beneath all the column loads. Flexibility is much greater than isolated footings; reinforced concrete is used for construction. Rectangular, square, circular, L-shaped or of any other shape as required. Modular constructions are sometimes made with space for car parking, etc. In this case, structural-geotechnical combined design is done

Construction Methods include:

  • excavation is the first step
  • manual excavation or with equipment
  • easier for clays; for sands, dewatering may be necessary
  • excavation must be inspected to ensure properties match design assumptions
  • clean excavation (no loose materials)
  • light compaction in case of sands
  • a layer of lean concrete is often placed as a good working platform

Construction Specs are required:

  • minimum depth of footing base (>0.75m)
    • erosion, ground loss, ground freezing. Want to ensure foundation on stable soil
  • minimum distances from utilities, cavities, and other foundation elements
  • sequence of construction of individual footings
    • for footings at two different elevations, the lower footing should be built at first so that there is never a lack of support for the higher footing
  • before excavation, ensure there is some sort of support (like a retaining wall)

Construction Inspection

  • seven necessary checks
    • foundation base elevation
    • nature and type of soil at the base of excavation
    • base cleaning (including compaction if necessary) before concrete placement
    • dimensions and cross sections of footings, grade beams, reinforcements etc
    • concrete placement, quality
    • time for concrete placement
    • footing integrity after concrete placement

Sump pumping is done for by pumping water accumulating in a sump. Alternatively, some diameter wells are made in a series which takes water out of the excavation with a pump.
