Course Overview

CIVE 354 covers the analysis, design, and construction aspects of foundations (particularly shallow foundations), slopes, and retaining structures. The main objective of CIVE 354 is to enable students to select the best foundation solution for different types of civil engineering structures. After completing the course, students should be able to design shallow foundations, slopes, and retaining structures for routine projects and supervise their constructions.

Introduction

Wide base gives more stability to structures than thin bases. All buildings are relient on the base.

Geotechnical engineering is a combination of differenc sciences. Soil mechanics, rock mechanics, and engineering geology are fundamental sciences that contribute to design. Applications are made for foundation design, slope design, and retaining structures. Other applicaitions include landfill design, waste containment, and subsurface remediation and cleanup.

Geotechnical engineering: concerned with the use of earth materials for improving and defending society and life. It involves systematic application of scientific principles and techniques for constructions on, on, or with geomaterials.

Foundation engineering: process of solving problems that involve safe and economical transfer of structural loads to the ground

Foundations are transition elements between the superstructure and the ground that transmit loads. Of these, shallow foundations, such as footings or rafts, are built near the ground surface. Deep foundations, such as piles or caissons, penetrate deep into the ground. Shallow foundations are often created using reinforced concrete. Piles are slender column-like structures, made of reinforced concrete (cylindrical or square), steel pipes, or H sections. Single piles under each column or multiple piles are considered a pile group, which have a rigid slab on top, known as a pile cap. Shallow foundations transfer the load in horizontal directions, while deep foundations transfer it vertically. For deep foundations, transportation and manufacturing loads must be considered.

Retaining structures or retaining walls are built to create space - temporary ot permanent - for a definite purpose. A retaining wall is any constructed wall that holds back soil, liquid, or other materials where there is an abrupt change in elevation.

  • Gravity retaining walls: massive walls that work on the principle of gravity for stability
  • Embedded walls: slender walls that are embedded in ground for stability
    • Pre-formed walls: made of thin steel, timber, or concrete sheets that are driven into the ground. Also called sheet pile walls
    • In situ walls: constructed by excavating deep trehcnes or auger holes, placing reinforcement and filling with concrete - diaphragm walls and bored pile walls are popular examples
  • Composite or Hybrid Walls: composite construction using a variety of materials with a variety of support systems. They use both gravity and embedded walls

Wall construction of a gravity wall follows these steps: excavation, preparing the site, constructing the wall, and putting in backfill soil. For embedded walls, you could either drive a wall or dig a trench or auger hole and pour concrete and reinforcement. You could put props or anchors as necessary

There are two types of slopes: natural and artificial. On basis of extent, infinite and finite. Mountain and hill slopes are natural and infinite, while hill side cuttings for road construciton; embankments for roads, railways, and canels, and earth dams and mines are all artificial and finite

Sequence of activities

  1. Owner/Developer aquires site
  2. Owner hires architects/consultants
  3. Consultant hires specialty contractors/consultants
  4. Planning, analysis, design, construction, testing
  5. Monitoring/Performance evaluation

To determine information on the existing foundations for reuse, load-tests or vibration tests are used.

From this process, choices need to be made on the foundation/retaining structure type, design parameters and methods, construction and testing methods, and monitoring methods. The selection is based on feasibility, constructability, availability of construction equipment, project-specific factors (eg. deadline), and environmental factors.

In general, design aims at stopping failures. Limit states are identified and prevented from being breached. A limit state is an unacceptable condition with respect to performance. If it is successful, a limit state is not reached or breached. Limit states separate desired states of a structure from undesired states.

Serviceability Limit States are a set of unacceptable conditions related to performance (excessive settlement, differential settlement or tilt). Excessive settlement leads to malfunctioning service lines, and excessive uneven settlement leads to building cracks

Ultimate Limit States are a set of unacceptable conditions related to Safety/Danger (collapse or breakage). Bearing capacity failure leads to plunging of foundation, and excessive settlement leads to complete collapse

A successful structure should pass strength, serviceability, constructability, economy, deformation, connection, utility, drainage, access, and aesthetic requirements add. To eurocode, specific requirements include serviceability, safety, fire, and robustness.

There are three foundation design approaches. Working/Allowable Stress Design (WSD/ASD), or factor of safety design; Limit State Design (LSD), or load and resistance factor design (LRFD); reliability based design (RBD), or probabilistic estimation of safety against potential failures, to account for different uncertainties. Probabilistic approach used for nuclear or difficult facilities.

Shear Strength - Actual Source

Cohesion is a fictitious quantity - no glue forces exist between dry soil particles

  • There is no cohesion in sands
  • There is no cohesion in clays; particles are kept together by surface tension (suction). There is a small amount of true cohesion in clays from the clay-water chemical interaction, but it is negligible practically
  • The apparent cohesion from surface tension and true cohesion from clay-water chemistry are prodominant at very low confining stresses (not significant to practical problems)

Shear resistance in soil originates from friction and dilatancy. Sometimes, resistance comes only from friction (pure friction) and sometimes from both friction and dilatancy. Particle sliding, interlocking, rolling, generates these two aspects of shear resistance. These resistances are quantified using different friction angles (multiple friction angles). Thus, the c−ϕc-\phic−ϕ method is old, but wrong

The magnitude of frictional resistance depends on the coefficient of friction, which is a property of the interface along which sliding occurs. By Amonton's Law of Friction, F=μNF=\mu{N}F=μN, with N being the normal force and F being the frictional force. Pure friction exists when particles slide or roll. FOr soils, sliding occurs along internal planes of failure within the soil element (like c−ϕc-\phic−ϕ curve). The friction coefficient is a property of the soil and it depends on the grain shape, size, size distribution, and surface roughness. This is an intrinsic property and is a constant for a given soil.

  • At the critical state, shear strength of soil originates from pure friction. Thus, there is no dilatancy. Therefore, the friction angle of soil at the critical state is unique (intrinsic property), and cohesion is "0" on the c−ϕc-\phic−ϕ graph

Dilation means size/volume increase. When particles are densely packed, they dilate (increase in volume) during shearing. This is true for dense sands and overconsolidated (also stiff or dense) clays. When particles are loosely packed, they contract (decrease in volume) during shearing. This is true for loose sands and normally consolidated clays See below:

Dilation occurs because densely packed particles have to climb over each other before they can shear. For loosely packed particles, such climbing is not necessary because of which they do not exhibit dilatancy. Thus, for dense soils, or dilatant soils, additional energy is required by the soil for the climbing action. Thus additional required energy has to come from the externally applied loads

  • Extra force or stress is required for failure or shearing to take place. Thus, soil exhibits additional resistance to shearing over and above its frictional resistance

Total Stress Analysis

Total stress analysis is applicable for CD triaxial tests (no pore pressure). In CU and UU tests, pore pressures generated during shearing have to be subtracted from the total applied stresses to calculate effective stesses. Because if is very expensive to find pore pressure for clay, undrained analysis or total stress analysis are used to determine shear stress from σ1\sigma_1σ1​ and σ3\sigma_3σ3​ without calculating u.

Pore pressure is generated during shearing. In dense sands or overconsolidated clays, there is negative pore pressure during shearing, while in loose sands or normally consolidated clays, there is positive pore pressure. This is because the pore pressure resists the general movement of the grains. Thus, effective stress is less than total stress in contractive soils, but greater in dilative soils

Shear stress is not affected by pore pressure (water doesn't generate shear stress). However, the normal stress changes.

Slobe Stability

There are different types of slope movements:

  • Falls: a mass of any size is detached from a steep slope or cliff - descends mostly through the air by free fall, leaping, bouncing, or rolling
  • Topples: one or more units of mass rotate forward about some pivot point, below or low in the unit - it is tilted without collapse
  • Slides: movement consists of shear strain and displacement along one or several surfaces that are visible or may reasonable be inferred
  • Spreads: the dominant form of movementis lateral extension. Movements may involve fracturing and extension of coherent material, either bedrock or soil, owing to liquefaction or plastic flow
  • Flows: slope movements may appear in several different forms

Slope movement based on:

  • Age
    • Contemporary movements are generally active and are relatively easily recognizable by their configuration. The surface forms produced by the mass movements are expressive
    • Dormant movements are usually covered by vegetation or are disturbed by erosion so that the traces of their last movements are not easily discernible
    • Fossil movements are generally developed in the Pleistocene or earlier periods, under different morphological and climate conditions, and cannot repeat themselves at present
  • Stage
    • Initial Stage is where you see the first signs of disturbance of equilibrium appear and cracks in the upper part of the slope develop
    • Advanced stage: the loosened mass is propelled into motion and slides downslope
    • Exhausted stage: the accumulation of slide mass creates temporary equilibrium equations

There are also different slopes

  • natural slopes and artificial slopes
  • infinite slopes and finite slopes

Fundemental requirement of stability: shear strength of the soil must be greater than the shear stress required for equilibrium. Therefore, instability often caused if the shear strength of soil is less than the required shear strength. Reached in two ways:

  • Decreasing shear strength of soil
  • Increasing shear stress applied to soil

These are caused by earthquake; rainfall; improperly applied loads on the slope; seepage through the slope; and change in soil properties from weathering, swelling, cracking, leaching, strain softening, creep, cyclic loading, seasonal changes in water table etc.

Rigid Mass

Along the sliding plane, the properties are plastic. The strength properties along the sliding place are done in the calculations

  • Calculations are done considering the incipient failure condition - the sliding and stationary masses are at static equilibrium
  • Multiple possible slip surfaces are considered and factors of safety are calculated for all such surfaces using static equilibrium. However, the critical slip surface corresponds to the minimum FS represent the most likely sliding surface. The minimum factor of safety is unique for a given problem and should be calculated as part of any analysis of stability. FS is global FS, although there are many local factors of safety
  • If statically determinate, there are three unknowns - FS, magnitude of N, and point of application of N. Shear resistance force T is known once FS is determined

Although failure surface shape can be irregular, dependong on if the composition is homogeneous or heterogeneous, there are 3 typical failure surfaces:

  • Non-circular, planar: made of a number of planes, but may occur if there are weak layers or seams that start and end at or near the slope surface
  • Composite: combination of circulatar and plane failure surface
