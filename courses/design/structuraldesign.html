Introduction

Structural components include columns, beams, and connections. For beam-column connections, beams transfer shear force from end of beam to the column because the fringe of the beam is connected. This connection also transfers the moment from the beam to the column. Often, moment is simplified as being resolved by tension and compression forces, since tension = compression by equilibrium.

In the below, moment is resisted by fringe, and shear by web connections.

Chords can also be considered components. These have top chords, bottom chords, and web members. Bracing members provide stability and transfer loads.

Structural system: assembly of interrelated and interacting structural components or elements that work together to fulfill a structural function

System design: a systematic process for defining the problem, establishing alternative concepts and developing design solutions

To design structural systems, functional, serviceability, and construction requirements are all considerations. Loads are typically found in the buildings code or design standards. Pre-2000, building code was prescriptive based. Now, it is objective based and doesn't need to be as prescriptive.

Engineers need to have an intuitive feeling for using structural materials and systems, and their behaviour. Approximate analysis is a strong design skill.

Design starts with creativity. Selection of structural system, materials, methods, initial layout and dimensions are required. Next is verification. Calculations such as force, moments, and proportioning of members and connections. Finally, codes and standards need to be checked. NBCC, CSA S16, CSA A23.3. This is an iterative process. Structural System Requirements

Functional requirements: relate to the fundamental purpose for which the structure exists

Spatial function: may also be referred to as the architectural function or purpose of a structure. Defined in terms of building shape, clear span, clear height, and limitation on structural member size

Architectural functions are esthetic and economical, while structural are about efficiently supporting loading.

Load-Carrying function: the form of the structures defines the load path: the path that loads take from the point of application to the supports

The structural system must be able to support the applied loads without failure to fulfill its load-carrying function. Strenth is a function of material properties and material arrangement. Strength may also be affected by local and member buckling or instability. Modes of failure for flexural and axial members are illustrated below:

Buckling failure comes without any warning, and is hard to predict. Lateral buckling, flexural buckling, or torsion buckling.

The structural system selected must be stable to ensure static equiliobrium under applied loads. The system and its members must be carefully designed against a sudden geometric change, such as buckling (dealt with bracing) or frame side-sway (must be geometrically stable). The structural system must provide lateral stability for the effect of lateral load on the structure. Lateral stability may be provided by bracing, rigid (moment resisting) joints, shear walls or combinations. These are the three forms of protection, and work to maintain lateral stability and resist lateral loads. The bracing, moment frame, and shear walls are called vertical diaphragm because they can maintain stability of structure by transferring loads to ground. The horizontal diaphragm transfers wind loads to locations of a shear wall, which transfers it to the ground. See Figure below and Examples:

In a unique case of a pin along a beam, the angle α→0\alpha\to{0}α→0 would mean nearly infinite force. This is called instantaneously unstable. See Example.

Shear wall, bending moment, and axial bracing systems can be combined. However, this is difficult because the distribution of lateral load is difficult to predict. Additionally, seismic load resistance is difficult to evaluate. Larger stiffnesses have more seismic forces; moment frame would have the least and shear walls would have the most.

Fire Protection

Fire protection to:

  1. Protect people (occupants and responders)
  2. Protect structure (property and adjacent property)

Building codes stipulate fire protection according to building's occupancy type and type of construction (noncombustible and combustible material). Fire resistive ratings are expressed in hours and refer to the length of time a structural member can be subjected to a standard fire test without failing. Connections have traditionally not been subjected to these tests, but they should be. Failure may come from three failure modes.

Transmission failure: rise of temperature of, or passage of hot gases to, the side opposite the fire. This type of failure may be because of conduction or of holes or cracks forming in the member

Structural failure at high temperature: the element must retain sufficient strength to support the building and its contents during the fire. Failure may be one of actual collapse of the element or of excessive deformation resulting in failure of other parts of the structure

Failure when subjected to a stream of water: the structure should retain its strength when subjected to a hose stream during or after the fire

Passive protection can be made with spray, gypsum, or masonry.

In the Cardington Project, fire was treated as a type of building loading. Columns had fire protection, but beams were found to not need such. Under CSA S16 Design of Steel Structures Annex K, fire is treated as a special load case. Structural Fire Engineering is an emerging discipline

Serviceability Requirements

Serviceability requirements relate to the expected performance of the structure in service. They are detailed design criteria that specify the standards that a structural system must meet in fulfilling its basic purpose. It includes flow-vibration, deflection,

Excessive structural deflections create: damage to construction and finishes, function problems of non-structural components (doors, windows, machinery), user comfort (visually unacceptable), second order PδP\deltaPδ or PΔP\DeltaPΔ effects, and ponding of water on roof (possible collapse). The first 3 are serviceability issue and bottom 2 are safety issue. Ponding isn't a huge issue in Canada because roofs are designed for snow loads.

Recommended limits for deflection are provided by the design codes. Deflection limits may also be established based on experience and common practice

Structural vibrations may result from a number of factors, and may be continuous or transient in nature. Vibrations may be annoying or discomforting to occupants, and may affect the operation or equipment. Transient vibrations may be caused by impact, wind, people walking, equipment moving, and vehicles. Continuous vibrations may be caused by machinery operation and human activities such as aerobics and dancing.

Vibration is reduced by proper design of structural systems and members, or by introducing passive or active control damping or isolation. Human-induced vibration is an issue in cold-formed steel flooring systems.

The durability is the expected service life of the structure, as well as the associated maintenance costs,should be considered in selection of structural system. Durability is the ability to resist weathered action, chemical attack, abrasion, insects, or other deterioration processes over time. Eg. corrosion, fatigue, termites. Durable structures retain original form, quality, and serviceability when exposed to the environment over intended service life. Buildings need to last 50 years and bridges 70.

Steel, concrete, and timber structures see serviceability durability issue: cracking, spalling, staining, increased deflections. Strength and safety issue: loss of x-section, reinforcement, degradation of bond, and spalling

Thee structural material, form, details, and environment are all durability considerations. The building must be within the building envelope to ensure safety. Cold-formed steel for residential structure is galvanized, as the zinc sacrifices itself. Distance from shoreline is considered in this. The type of board ised is also an important consideration. MGO boards, lightweight in-filled thermal insolation, and others have chlorides, and thus cannot be used with steel. Welded connections are pinned. Tributary Area

TAs are used to determine the loads on beams and columns due to uniform floor and roof loads. Thay are defined by lines of support of the member and lines of zero shear (V=0). Tributary area is the area surrounding the column by the floor centrelines, For accuracy, beam end reactions can also be used for column loads. Sometimes, the centrelines may not be the exact location of zero shear, but fairly close. This depends on boundary condition (continuous beam or simply supported). For beams, it depends on one-way or two-way bending. If one-way, it is bounded by the floor centreline. If two-way, it is bounded by triangle created by two diagonals with angle α=45\alpha=45α=45. Two-way bending slabs are statically indetermine, while the one-way slab is determinate. As such, the area is derived by the bending of a plastic plate.

Design Issues

Spatial requirements are defined by the need for interior spatial definition, subdivision and separation, and the need for unobstructed interior space. Essentially, functional dimensions lead to clear span dimensions. These are described using 2 orthogonal critical dimensions to be accommodated by the structural system: a1a_1a1​ and a2a_2a2​. See below:

To choose a structural system, 1-on-1 fit for larger dims, like a1a_1a1​ or a2a_2a2​, is used and known as a tight fit. If the dimensions are used repeatedly corresponding to a1a_1a1​ and a2a_2a2​, tight fit.

If selected span has multiple a1a_1a1​ or a2a_2a2​ (eg. a2a_2a2​ span increases), this is called loose-fit. If two-way system is preferred, 1:1 or 1:1.5 systens are most efficient.

From steel and concrete span tables, one can check if the span length is practical. One should also check the wall supports vs. column supports and foundation conditions (distribution of forces).

To design:

  1. Choose basic functional modes
  2. Choose wall system
  3. Choose one-way or two-way spanning system

Although all systems work, systems are selected on economics, constructibility, and foundations. When critical distances are fairly large (15'-30'), tight fits are preferable. If smaller (<15'), loose fit area desirable for structural steel or concrete system (except for lightweight framing, as cold-formed steel or timber are acceptable with loosefit). In general, loosefit is more flexible for what building can be used for future, as larger opening created.

Directionality: functionality inferenced by the direction of the people movement.

  • In a linear one-direction movement (apartment, hotel), suited to one-way system and bearing walls. This is because there is no movement in the other direction, and thus walls can be used.
  • Two-way spaces, such as offices or retail, have two-way systems with interior columns. Also could use concrete column with cast-in-place slab.
  • Neutral spaces have no limitation for flow of movement. This may require long-spans, which are vulnerable to lateral torsional buckling and require bracing

When one-way system selected, the engineer must decide which way to orient the elements. While there is no "best solution", it is preferable to place collector elements (girder) in the shorter direction to reduce the span length. This orientation reduces the required section depth.

Lateral load systems for building

There are two functions of lateral load systems:

  1. Maintain lateral stability of a structure
  2. Transfer lateral loads to the ground

Lateral load systems for buildings consist of: lateral distribution (horizontal elements) and lateral support subsystems (vertical elements). The lateral distribution system is a roof or floor diaphragm. The lateral support system is provided by frames (beams or columns w/ possible bracing), or shear walls. See below to see the lateral load path:

If exterior wall has wall studs, load is transferred to floor level and to the foundation. With a bracing frame, all horizontal loads are taken by brace. Know that braces are typically tension only, but can be designed for compression also. With moment frame (rigid beam-column connection), loads transferred along frame.

There are 3 lateral load categories: braced frame, rigid frame, and shear walls. Efficiency is compared with cost and stiffness. Shear walls have the largest stiffness, followed by the bracing frame, and the least stiff is moment frame. Combinations should be avoided because of the analysis complexity. It is hard to determine how much is taken by the different types of resistance methods. Although computers are available for this, conceptual design will be tricky. If a braced frame is placed above a mement frame (vertical combination), they will have different stiffnesses. This is difficult for seismic design. If a moment frame is next to a braced frame (horizontal combination), it's hard to tell how much load is taken by both types. See examples below:

Moment frame has the advantage of providing large openings in walls. Bracing frames are more efficient than moment frames for resisting wind load (in the direction opposite of the greatest wind area). See below:

Lateral load resistance is very important for tall buildings. Since rigid frame is the least efficient means of resisting lateral loads, it is not effective as building heights exceed 10 storeys. Most high-rises are tall and slender, and thus behave like a vertical cantilever under lateral load. It is idealized to have moment from wind or earthquake, Q, and resistance from foundation. The self-weight and applied loads form a resultant load, R. R is preferably inside the building to reduce the overturning moment. Thus, gravity load is increased to increase the self-weight component. See below:

If aspect ratio (height:width building) is small, smaller lever arm. This requires an increase in the pile foundation depth. Cold-formed steel isn't viable for high buildings because it isn't too light. If aspect ratio increased, larger lever arm. This reduces the resisting moment and foundation depths do not need to be so deep.

Exterior columns carry axial forces from lateral loads (and some gravity), while interior columns only carry gravity loads.

Sears Tower is an example of a bundled tube structure. This uses close column spacing and stiff spandrel beams to form a "bundled tube".The John Hancock building is an example of a trussed frame with tubes. This has external, visible bracing without corrosion protection (weathered steel). Weathered steel forms a small layer of rust, which ironically acts as rust protection. The West Coast Transmission Tower is an example of a suspended structure (concrete core, truss on top, all floors hanging from top). Although the cables are a very large load path, which is traditionally considered bad, it accomodates for earthquake by allowing the building to sway. Since 911, these buildings are avoided because they are vulnerable at the core.
