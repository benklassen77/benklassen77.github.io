Introduction

Structural components include columns, beams, and connections. For beam-column connections, beams transfer shear force from end of beam to the column because the fringe of the beam is connected. This connection also transfers the moment from the beam to the column. Often, moment is simplified as being resolved by tension and compression forces, since tension = compression by equilibrium.

In the below, moment is resisted by fringe, and shear by web connections.

Chords can also be considered components. These have top chords, bottom chords, and web members. Bracing members provide stability and transfer loads.

Structural system: assembly of interrelated and interacting structural components or elements that work together to fulfill a structural function

System design: a systematic process for defining the problem, establishing alternative concepts and developing design solutions

To design structural systems, functional, serviceability, and construction requirements are all considerations. Loads are typically found in the buildings code or design standards. Pre-2000, building code was prescriptive based. Now, it is objective based and doesn't need to be as prescriptive.

Engineers need to have an intuitive feeling for using structural materials and systems, and their behaviour. Approximate analysis is a strong design skill.

Design starts with creativity. Selection of structural system, materials, methods, initial layout and dimensions are required. Next is verification. Calculations such as force, moments, and proportioning of members and connections. Finally, codes and standards need to be checked. NBCC, CSA S16, CSA A23.3. This is an iterative process. Structural System Requirements

Functional requirements: relate to the fundamental purpose for which the structure exists

Spatial function: may also be referred to as the architectural function or purpose of a structure. Defined in terms of building shape, clear span, clear height, and limitation on structural member size

Architectural functions are esthetic and economical, while structural are about efficiently supporting loading.

Load-Carrying function: the form of the structures defines the load path: the path that loads take from the point of application to the supports

The structural system must be able to support the applied loads without failure to fulfill its load-carrying function. Strenth is a function of material properties and material arrangement. Strength may also be affected by local and member buckling or instability. Modes of failure for flexural and axial members are illustrated below:

Buckling failure comes without any warning, and is hard to predict. Lateral buckling, flexural buckling, or torsion buckling.

The structural system selected must be stable to ensure static equiliobrium under applied loads. The system and its members must be carefully designed against a sudden geometric change, such as buckling (dealt with bracing) or frame side-sway (must be geometrically stable). The structural system must provide lateral stability for the effect of lateral load on the structure. Lateral stability may be provided by bracing, rigid (moment resisting) joints, shear walls or combinations. These are the three forms of protection, and work to maintain lateral stability and resist lateral loads. The bracing, moment frame, and shear walls are called vertical diaphragm because they can maintain stability of structure by transferring loads to ground. The horizontal diaphragm transfers wind loads to locations of a shear wall, which transfers it to the ground. See Figure below and Examples:

In a unique case of a pin along a beam, the angle α→0\alpha\to{0}α→0 would mean nearly infinite force. This is called instantaneously unstable. See Example.

Shear wall, bending moment, and axial bracing systems can be combined. However, this is difficult because the distribution of lateral load is difficult to predict. Additionally, seismic load resistance is difficult to evaluate. Larger stiffnesses have more seismic forces; moment frame would have the least and shear walls would have the most.

Fire Protection

Fire protection to:

  1. Protect people (occupants and responders)
  2. Protect structure (property and adjacent property)

Building codes stipulate fire protection according to building's occupancy type and type of construction (noncombustible and combustible material). Fire resistive ratings are expressed in hours and refer to the length of time a structural member can be subjected to a standard fire test without failing. Connections have traditionally not been subjected to these tests, but they should be. Failure may come from three failure modes.

Transmission failure: rise of temperature of, or passage of hot gases to, the side opposite the fire. This type of failure may be because of conduction or of holes or cracks forming in the member

Structural failure at high temperature: the element must retain sufficient strength to support the building and its contents during the fire. Failure may be one of actual collapse of the element or of excessive deformation resulting in failure of other parts of the structure

Failure when subjected to a stream of water: the structure should retain its strength when subjected to a hose stream during or after the fire

Passive protection can be made with spray, gypsum, or masonry.

In the Cardington Project, fire was treated as a type of building loading. Columns had fire protection, but beams were found to not need such. Under CSA S16 Design of Steel Structures Annex K, fire is treated as a special load case. Structural Fire Engineering is an emerging discipline

Serviceability Requirements

Serviceability requirements relate to the expected performance of the structure in service. They are detailed design criteria that specify the standards that a structural system must meet in fulfilling its basic purpose. It includes flow-vibration, deflection,

Excessive structural deflections create: damage to construction and finishes, function problems of non-structural components (doors, windows, machinery), user comfort (visually unacceptable), second order PδP\deltaPδ or PΔP\DeltaPΔ effects, and ponding of water on roof (possible collapse). The first 3 are serviceability issue and bottom 2 are safety issue. Ponding isn't a huge issue in Canada because roofs are designed for snow loads.

Structural vibrations may result from a number of factors, and may be continuous or transient in nature. Vibrations may be annoying or discomforting to occupants, and may affect the operation or equipment. Transient vibrations may be caused by impact, wind, people walking, equipment moving, and vehicles. Continuous vibrations may be caused by machinery operation and human activities such as aerobics and dancing.

The durability is the expected service life of the structure, as well as the associated maintenance costs,should be considered in selection of structural system. Durability is the ability to resist weathered action, chemical attack, abrasion, insects, or other deterioration processes over time. Eg. corrosion, fatigue, termites. Durable structures retain original form, quality, and serviceability when exposed to the environment over intended service life. Buildings need to last 50 years and bridges 70.

Steel, concrete, and timber structures see serviceability durability issue: cracking, spalling, staining, increased deflections. Strength and safety issue: loss of x-section, reinforcement, degradation of bond, and spalling

Tributary Area

TAs are used to determine the loads on beams and columns due to uniform floor and roof loads. Thay are defined by lines of support of the member and lines of zero shear (V=0). Tributary area is the area surrounding the column by the floor centrelines, For accuracy, beam end reactions can also be used for column loads. Sometimes, the centrelines may not be the exact location of zero shear, but fairly close. This depends on boundary condition (continuous beam or simply supported). For beams, it depends on one-way or two-way bending. If one-way, it is bounded by the floor centreline. If two-way, it is bounded by triangle created by two diagonals with angle α=45\alpha=45α=45. Two-way bending slabs are statically indetermine, while the one-way slab is determinate. As such, the area is derived by the bending of a plastic plate.

Design Issues

Spatial requirements are defined by the need for interior spatial definition, subdivision and separation, and the need for unobstructed interior space. Essentially, functional dimensions lead to clear span dimensions. These are described using 2 orthogonal critical dimensions to be accommodated by the structural system: a1a_1a1​ and a2a_2a2​. See below:

To choose a structural system, 1-on-1 fit for larger dims, like a1a_1a1​ or a2a_2a2​, is used and known as a tight fit. If the dimensions are used repeatedly corresponding to a1a_1a1​ and a2a_2a2​, tight fit.

If selected span has multiple a1a_1a1​ or a2a_2a2​ (eg. a2a_2a2​ span increases), this is called loose-fit. If two-way system is preferred, 1:1 or 1:1.5 systens are most efficient.

From steel and concrete span tables, one can check if the span length is practical. One should also check the wall supports vs. column supports and foundation conditions (distribution of forces).

To design:

  1. Choose basic functional modes
  2. Choose wall system
  3. Choose one-way or two-way spanning system

Although all systems work, systems are selected on economics, constructibility, and foundations. When critical distances are fairly large (15'-30'), tight fits are preferable. If smaller (<15'), loose fit area desirable for structural steel or concrete system (except for lightweight framing, as cold-formed steel or timber are acceptable with loosefit). In general, loosefit is more flexible for what building can be used for future, as larger opening created.

Directionality: functionality inferenced by the direction of the people movement.

  • In a linear one-direction movement (apartment, hotel), suited to one-way system and bearing walls. This is because there is no movement in the other direction, and thus walls can be used.
  • Two-way spaces, such as offices or retail, have two-way systems with interior columns. Also could use concrete column with cast-in-place slab.
  • Neutral spaces have no limitation for flow of movement. This may require long-spans, which are vulnerable to lateral torsional buckling and require bracing

When one-way system selected, the engineer must decide which way to orient the elements. While there is no "best solution", it is preferable to place collector elements (girder) in the shorter direction to reduce the span length. This orientation reduces the required section depth.

Lateral load systems for building

There are two functions of lateral load systems:

  1. Maintain lateral stability of a structure
  2. Transfer lateral loads to the ground

Lateral load systems for buildings consist of: lateral distribution (horizontal elements) and lateral support subsystems (vertical elements). The lateral distribution system is a roof or floor diaphragm. The lateral support system is provided by frames (beams or columns w/ possible bracing), or shear walls. See below to see the lateral load path:

If exterior wall has wall studs, load is transferred to floor level and to the foundation. With a bracing frame, all horizontal loads are taken by brace. Know that braces are typically tension only, but can be designed for compression also. With moment frame (rigid beam-column connection), loads transferred along frame.

There are 3 lateral load categories: braced frame, rigid frame, and shear walls. Efficiency is compared with cost and stiffness. Shear walls have the largest stiffness, followed by the bracing frame, and the least stiff is moment frame. Combinations should be avoided because of the analysis complexity. It is hard to determine how much is taken by the different types of resistance methods. Although computers are available for this, conceptual design will be tricky. If a braced frame is placed above a mement frame (vertical combination), they will have different stiffnesses. This is difficult for seismic design. If a moment frame is next to a braced frame (horizontal combination), it's hard to tell how much load is taken by both types. See examples below:

Moment frame has the advantage of providing large openings in walls. Bracing frames are more efficient than moment frames for resisting wind load (in the direction opposite of the greatest wind area). See below:

Lateral load resistance is very important for tall buildings. Since rigid frame is the least efficient means of resisting lateral loads, it is not effective as building heights exceed 10 storeys. Most high-rises are tall and slender, and thus behave like a vertical cantilever under lateral load. It is idealized to have moment from wind or earthquake, Q, and resistance from foundation. The self-weight and applied loads form a resultant load, R. R is preferably inside the building to reduce the overturning moment. Thus, gravity load is increased to increase the self-weight component. See below:

If aspect ratio (height:width building) is small, smaller lever arm. This requires an increase in the pile foundation depth. Cold-formed steel isn't viable for high buildings because it isn't too light. If aspect ratio increased, larger lever arm. This reduces the resisting moment and foundation depths do not need to be so deep.

Exterior columns carry axial forces from lateral loads (and some gravity), while interior columns only carry gravity loads.

Horizontal Diaphragm

Roof and floor systems normally function as a horizontal diaphgram; they distribute lateral forces horizontally to the vertical elements of the lateral force resisting system. In multi-bay structures, where stiff lateral load-resisting elements are located near the ends of the structure, the diaphragm is needed to efficiently distribute lateral forces. There is two purposes of horizontal diaphragm:

  1. Ensure stability
  2. Distribute forces

Diaphragms are called rigid or flexible depending on their stiffness. These are based on the stiffness ratio between the horiziontal and vertical diaphragm. Note: if the stiffness of a horizontal diaphragm is 2-2.5 times greater than that of a vertical one, the horizontal diaphgram is rigid (NBC). Rigid horizontal diaphragms could be concrete slab and flexible could be OSB or steel deck.

Stiffness and deformation

The in-plane stiffness of the diaphragm influences the distribition of lateral forces to the vertical elements, and influences the deformation of the diaphragm. In a lateral force system with symmetric, parallel laterial force-resisting elements, the amount of lateral force distributed to each element depends on the diaphragm stiffness.

  • if diaphragm rigid, all lateral force-resisting elements are displaced equally, and the force distribution is based on the lateral stiffness of vertical elements.
    • Case A: if all vertical elements have the same lateral stiffness, the lateral force is distributed to them equally
    • Case B: if the vertical elements have the same properties with the exception of length, the lateral force will be distributed according to the ratios of the length of each element to the total length (relative lateral stiffness assuming identical material, thickness, and height)
  • Case C: if diaphragm is flexible, lateral force is distributed based on tributary area of the lateral force-resisting elements
  • Case D: if diaphragm is semi-rigid, indeterminate and structural analysis required

Snip of cases

If you are not sure if rigid or flexible, use rigid values on outside walls and flexible on interior walls. This brings conservative, maximum envelope value.

Stiffness and deformation of horizontal diaphragm influenced by:

  • construction material
  • continuity of the spanning diaphragm over a number of supports. Continuous beam more stiff than simply supported
  • span-to-depth ratio of the diaphragm (related to flexural and shear deformation)
  • effect of special conditions, like chord length changes, connection yielding, and large openings

For material, plywood deck very flexible and deflection investigated with large spans or span-to-depth ratio. Metal decks are comparable to plywood decks, with acceptable shear capacities provided by manufacturer. Metal decks with cast-in-place concrete produce rigid diaphragms, and cast-in-place concrete decks are the strongest and stiffest diaphragm. Precast units must be adequately attached to each other and support members for diaphragm action to develop. The attachments that are designed only for gravity loads are usually not adequate for lateral forces.

Distribution of lateral forces to vertical elements

Translation of lateral forces to vertical element is key information. The distribution is impacted by the geometry or arrangement of vertical elements, lateral stiffness of vertical elements, diaphragm stiffness, and location of applied forces. The layout of vertical elements in lateral force-resisting system are classified as symmetrical or asymmetrical system.

In symmetrical systems, building geometry, mass, rigidity are symmetrically arranged. The geometric centre of the building coincides with the centre of mass and centre of rigidity. CR=CG

  • The center of gravity, CG, is determined on the distribution of building/floor mass, and generally assumed with uniform mass distirbution. Center of rigidity, CR, is determined based on the distribution of vertical diaphragms. It is the point where the application of lateral forces will not produce a torsional moment or rotation about a vertical axis
  • Resultant of the applied lateral loads passes through the centre of rigidity, or centre of lateral resistance. Thus, the building deflection is mainly due to translation
  • Many building design codes still specify buildings to be designed with a minimum eccentricity against seismic loads (minimum torsion) even with symmetric design. This is known as accidental eccentricity. However, torsional effects are relatively small in symmetric systems and are ignored for preliminary design.
  • It is assumed that out-of-plane lateral stiffness is negligible.

See examples below:

In asymmetrical systems, CR does not equal CG. Asymmetry in a building lateral force-resisting system can be caused by geometry, stiffness, or mass distribution. Since the resultant of the lateral applied loads does not act through the centre of rigidity, there will be a torsional effect as well as direct force effect.

  • This torsional effect bay become significant if the horizontal diaphragm is stiff. In this case, the rigid horizontal idaphragm not only transfers loads, but also rotates in the direction of lateral action. To resist in torsional moments most effectly, the lateral force-resisting system should be located nera the periphery of the building with some distance from the rigidity centre (larger lever arm) or be in a centre, symmetric closed shape to form a couple.

Torsional moments result from asymmetry (building geometry, mass, and stiffness) and eccentric forces (non-uniform wind pressure and torsional ground motion). Torsion is generated when the centre of mass of the floor doesn't coincide with the centre of rigidity (seismic loads), or when the resultant of the lateral loads does not pass through the centre of rigidity. In regular structures, little torsion develops. Irregular structures have mass, stiffness, and damping ratio fluxiations which contribute to torsion.

  • Seismic joints (aka expansion or separation joint) are used to separate the structure, aligning center of gravity with center of rigidity.
  • Lateral force-resisting elements need to be as far away from the centre of rigidity as possible (large lever arm)
  • Closed tubular systems very efficient due to their torsional stiffness
  • Ensure that the torsional resistance system does not have concurrent forces

Statically Determinate Systems

The requirements are as follows: the lateral force-resisting system consists of 3 vertical planar elements and is laterally stable

Force distribution can be determined using the equilibrium conditions with or without torsion. With this approach, the CR doesn't need to be found and the stiffness of resisting elements is neglected.

  • Both translational and torsional force effects can be considered.
  • 3 vertical elements can be arranged in any matter such that lines of action aren't concurrent. Torsional resistance is improved by placing the vertical elements such that their moment arm about the centre of rigidity is maximized

J-Hancock Tower

This Boston building is 60 stories and was considered the plywood palace for years. In 1973, the glasses at upper level fell down due to the winds. Myths for this included:

  • Tower swayed too much in the wild. In actuality, sway wasn't harming any windows. Two 300-ton weights sit at opposite ends at the 58th floor of the tower. These were designed by William LeMessurier, are known as tuned mass dampener
  • The sharp angles of the tower's rhomboid shape were creating "hot spots" where high wind velocities would damage or suck out windows. In actuality, hot spots didn't create window failure
  • The tower was settling on its foundations, which stressed the windows. In actuality, this did not stress the windows. The foundation problems were created by the excavation. These impacted the nearby Trinity Church.

The actual problem was defects in the manufacturing/assembly of the window curtain wall units. Samples of the window panels were being tested in wind tunnel tests. To cut the glare and heat of the sun, a coat of reflective chromium was placed on the inside surface of the outside pane of glass. The window frame was bonded to the chrome with a lead solder. The lead solder bonding too well with the chrome, and was too rigid such that the joint couldn't absorb any movement. But window glass expands and contracts with changes in temperature, and it vibrates with the wind. Window replacement costed $7M

There were also concerns for the overall stability of the building under certain rare wind loading conditions. Bruno Thurlimann, famous engineer, found that it might fall over on its narrow edge. This is because in earthquake loading, it could move a bit at the foundation which would make it more flexible. Building was stiffened with diagonals for around $5M

Structural Loads

Building codes and design standards are different classifications:

  • General building codes: NBCC, OBC. These have specified service loads, structural design strength, fire, accessibility, HVAC
  • Technical design standards: CSA S16. CSA A23.3. CSA O86. These discuss how to design with steel, concrete, and timber.

These codes have:

  • Legal ordinance enacted by governmental bodies and regulatory agencies, establishing regulations governing building design and construction
  • In the technical standards, there are 3 categories of representatives: public interest (regulatory agencies, professors), industry (manufacturers, fabricators), users (consulting engineers). Each of these groups represents one third of the representatives
  • They specify minimum design loads to protect public, but it is up to the engineer to consider if the minimum loads are adequate or not
  • They specify loads that are generally applicable to routine or regular structures. Non-typical or special structures may be subject to different load types and magnitudes
  • Structural engineers must determine the specific loads to be used for a particular structure that may be more or less than the code minimum
  • NBC is not a legal document, but a model code for provincial and municipal codes. CSA S16 (Steel) and CSA A23 (Concrete) are referenced by NBC and adopted by OBC. These are updated every 5 and 10 years for steel and concrete, respectively.

If CSA Standard S16-14 is created in 2014, it is then referenced by NBC in 2015, which is referenced by OBC in 2016. Thus, there is a 2 year difference for it to be a legal document.

There are two categories of loads: static and dynamic.

Static loads: can vary in magnitude and location over time, but these variations are assumed to be gradual and do not induce an inertial response. Includes live load (occupants, snow [not live load since 2000]), dead (selfweight, [finishes and fixtures/non-moving equipment if known well, else live loads]), earth pressure, and restraint actions (settlement, thermal strains, creep, expansion/swelling, shrinkage)

Dynamic loads: vary with time in terms in magnitude, direction or position. These induce an inertial response in the structure. Effects may be simplified for design by treating as equivalent static loads

  • Continuous dynamic loads are caused by human activity (walking, running, dancing), vehicles and moving equipment, fixed equipment (oscillating), wind, and earthquakes. Wind and earthquakes can be treated as equivalent static loads depending on location, shape, and aspect ratio
  • Discrete or impact loads are caused by blasts, vehicles

Dead Loads

Dead loads: include the weight of the structure and any permanent attachments such as fixed partitions, ceiling, floor and roof coverings, and permanent equipment.

  • Self weight of structure
  • Superimposed dead load
  • May include loads from earth, plants, and trees. Eg. green building
  • Construction dead loads may also be present Eg. gypsum board piled up

Live Loads

Live load: any non-permanent load on the structure produced by building occupants, furniture, storage items, minor equipment, or movable partitions. Defined according to the "occupancy and use" of the building

  • short term and possibly variable in magnitude and position. Thus, not easy to predict or quantify
  • load factors for live loads are normally higher than for dead loads
  • Variability in magnitude: To deal with uncertainty of live load magnitude, building codes have enacted conservative values for specified minimum live loads. Use office building surveys conducted in US over past 50 years. Typically use 5% probability of exceedance and engineers judgement
    • Loads have decreased over time (less books etc). Can be problem for vibration
  • Variability in position: because live loads are transient, pattern loading must be considered. Maximum force effects in structural members may be produced by patterns of live loading rather than live loads applied on all spans. Influence line used. Snow loads can be partially distributed

NBC 2015 defines uniformly distributed loads in Table 4.1.5.3 and concentrated loads in Table 4.1.5.9. Where occupancy may vary at different times, the greatest value must be used.

Pattern or partial loading must also be considered:

  • consider patterns of live load on any portion of the loaded area such that the critical force effect is produced (influence line)
  • check live load applied uniformly over the entire area

For use/occupancy not specified in Table 4.1.5.3, live load is determined from an analysis of the weight from the probability of: assembly of persons, accumulation of equipemnt/furnishings, and storage of materials.

Live load may be reduced to 80% of the specified value if the building is considered to be in the low importance category.

Other Loads

Snow and Rain Loads: independent of the building occupancy, dependent on location

  • Ground snow load is provided in the NBC. However, since most buildings are hidden in the winter, a coefficient of 0.8 is applied to obtain roof snow load
  • Drift snow load happens due to different roof elevations, thus the snow density is not uniform. Snow on adjacent building could slide onto its roof
  • Unbalanced snow load can result from wind blowing snow off of roof. This could bring a member typically in tension into compression
  • Rain on snow effect creates ponding. Must be considered

Wind Load: independing of building occupancy, but highly dependent on height, shape, opening, surrounding environment, and location of buildings

  • dynamic in nature. Internal/external pressure and suction, uplift, overturning, and vibration
  • Treated as static load
  • Wind load is primarily related to wind speed: mean wind speed, peak wind speed (gust)
  • Higher wind load at corners of building than in the middle as the wind passes through there

Earthquake Load: rare, random, and dynamic in nature

  • Primary effect: violent ground motions generate lateral and vertical inertia forces resulting building damage or collapse
  • Highly dependent on location, self-weight, soil condition (certain conditions amplify loading) and structural system of buildings (unique from other loadings)
  • Earthquake load is primarily related to ground motion velocity and acceleration, and fundamental period of structure.
  • Equivalent static method; response spectra analysis, and nonlinear time history analysis
  • More stiff buildings create large forces because of shorter fundamental period (1st mode of vibration). Flexible systems have longer fundamental period, smaller forces will result. If the structure is too flexible, there is a large P−ΔP-\DeltaP−Δ effect.

Importance factors are used for snow and wind loads. AT ULS, post-disaster is designed for 1 in 100 years and low, normal, and high at 1 in 50 years. SLS designed for 1 in 30 years

Load Estimation

For first-order, linear-elastic structural behaviour, concrete beams and slabs can be designed to patterned live loading. This can be done with:

  • rules for arrangement of live load for continuous beams and slabs
  • approximate analysis
  • beam tables (CDH Part 1 White Pages)
  • structural analysis program

For continuous beams and other indeterminate structures, pattern live loading must be considered to find maximum force and moment effects. Governing live load patterns are determined using:

  • influence lines
  • brute force - check all possible LL combinations with software
  • CSA rules for continuous beams
  • Approximate frame analysis

For continuous beams and one-way slabs, the arrangements of LLs and DLs may be limited to combinations of:

  1. Factored DL of the structure and factored permanent superimposed DL on all spans, with factored partition load and factored live load on two adjacent spans. This creates max positive moment at the support
  2. Factored DL of the structure and factored permanent superimposed DL on all spans, with factored partition load and factored LL on alternate spans. This creates max positive moment within span
  3. Factored DL and factored LL on all spans

Loading Concepts

Beams in normal use are called "in-service". In-service beams have self-weight and load due to use and occupancy

Since the above resistance is greater than the applied load effect, it is safe. Factor of safety demonstrates how safe

  • In limit state design, designed based on probability of failure

To ensure that R≥LR\geq{L}R≥L, follow the following:

  1. Estimate load effects
    • Code specified design loads
    • Structural analysis
  2. Estimate resistance
  3. Account for variability in load effects
    • Loads can be difficult to estimate, and may vary in intensity and location
    • Structural analysis involves assumptions and approximations. Linear elastic analysis and pinned or fixed connections/ends
  4. Account for variability in resistance
    • Variability in concrete and steel strength
    • Differences between designed and constructed dimensions
    • Construction errors
    • Assumptions and approximations in estimating resistance
  5. Define an acceptable level of safety
    • Potential loss of life
    • Cost of failure: clean up, lost revenue
    • Warning before failure: brittle vs ductile
    • Function of building
    • Acceptable level. Based on probability of failures
