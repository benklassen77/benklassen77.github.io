General

Structural Design is governed by codes and standards and based on limit states design principles (ultimate and serviceability limit state). In Ontario, the ontario building code (obc) is used. For wood: CSA 086, steel: CSA 516, concrete: A23.3

Ultimate Limit State looks at strength and stability, and includes fatigue (sometimes treated as a separate limit state)

ϕR≥∑αiSi(ULS)\phi{R}\ge\sum{\alpha_i{S_i}}\tag{ULS} ϕR≥∑αi​Si​(ULS)

Serviceability Limit State looks at deflections and vibrations. Subjective criteria based on service loads/conditions

Permanent (dead) loads include the self weight of structure and superimposed dead loads. Transient (live) loads include occupancy (residential 1.9 kPa, assembly 4.8, library 7.2, parking 2.4), snow, wind, earth or water pressure, earthquake, rain, temperature, and strain-induced

Given information includes applied loads, imposed deformations, material properties (yield strength, rupture strength, E, G), geometry (span, length, x-section), and boundary conditions (support conditions, joints) Engineers need to know: internal forces/stresses, external reactions, and deflections/deformations/rotations

This course makes the following assumptions: static, linear-elastic behaviour/relatinship between stress and strain is known, small deformations/small rotations relative to member size/span, first order analysis, compatibility of strain

If fixed but not completely fixed (rotation could happen), pin more conservative

Idealized joint conditions

  • Pinned: allows relative rotation between members
  • Fixed: fixed angle is maintained between members

Stability and Determinacy

Hibbeler Method

When all the forces in a structure can be found w/ equilibrium equations, it is statically determinate. If there are more unknowns than can be found, it is indeterminate. Additional equations are required if indeterminate. These are known as combatibility equations, and exist in equal number to the degree of indeterminacy. With r force and moment reaction components (both reaction and bar) and n parts, the below are used to calculate determinacy (Example)

r>3n(Statically Indeterminate)r>3n\tag{Statically Indeterminate} r>3n(Statically Indeterminate)

r<3n(Unstable)r<3n\tag{Unstable} r<3n(Unstable)

  • also unstable if concurrent, parallel, or collapsible mechanism

External stability: three conditions must all be met to achieve external stability for 2D structures (Example): there must be 3 reactions, which cannot all be concurrent or all be parallel.

Internal stability: a structure must have sufficient internal constraints; it must not form a mechanism

Force Method

Procedure for analysis (Example)

  1. Determine the degree of static indeterminacy (how many redundants)
  2. Select the n redundant forces to be solved by the analysis and identify the corresponding compatibility conditions (eg. known displacements, rotations)
  3. Remove the redundant forces to produce the primary structure (has to be stable)
  4. Analyze the primary structure. Perform a displacement analysis under the applied loads where displacements at the locations and in the directions of the redundant forces are determined
  5. Remove the applied loads and sequentially apply a unit load at the location and in the direction of the redundant forces determined in step 2. The resulting deflections doe to these unit loads rae the flexibility coefficients, fijf_{ij}fij​, with i being the location of interest and j being the location of the unit force applied.

Influence Lines

An influence line is used to determine a reaction, internal force or displacement at a specific point in a structure. They are constructed to reflect the effects, at a single point on a structure, of a moving unit load acting anywhere on a structure. They are then used to determine the effects of actual moving loads, or of transient loads

To find shear influence line:

  1. By statics: draw shear force diagram with the unit load applied at the point of interest. From this, there are two options.
    1. a) Connect corners of shear-force diagram with ends of beam
    2. b) Take mid-way cuts to determine which side is negative and positive
  2. If complicated, start at point of interest and then do endpoints, followed by midpoints. By breslau: apply unit deflection of 1 to right of cut and -1 to left of cut. Slopes on the RS and LS of supports must be equal
  3. To find actual shear at point of interest, multiply the applied forces by the amount of the influence lines at each position and sum them together. If there is an area load, multiply magnitude by area of the influence line

To find moment influence line:

  1. By statics: apply load at point of interest, then endpoints and midpoints to create moment influence line. By breslau: apply a unit rotation θ=1\theta=1θ=1 at point of interest
  2. To find actual moment at point, multiply the applied forces by the amount of the moment lines at each position and sum them together. If there is an area load, multiply magnitude by area of the influence line

To find maximum moment (Example):

  1. Identify the location of the maximum moment (likely midspan of beam)
  2. Draw influence line for moment at that point
  3. Apply loads at positive sections, then at negative sections to check which is bigger

Muller-Breslau Principle: The influence line for any reaction or internal force (shear, moment) correstonds to the deflected shape of the structure produced by removing the capacity of the structure to carry that force and then introducing into the modified (or released) structure a unit deformation that corresponds to the restraint removed

  1. Inflection points are important
  2. The assumed shape must satisfy joint/member continuity and support conditions
  3. The sketched shape may violate equilibrium

Procedure of approximate analysis (Example):

  1. Determine how many degrees indeterminate system is to understand how many assumptions are required
  2. Draw assumed deflected shape and estimate locations of inflection points
    • Inflection points are influenced by relative lengths of members, relative stiffness, and load locations
  3. Make other assumptions to make beam determinate.
    • If there is a horizontal displacement induced by the force, one could assume that different cantilevers deflect an equal amount horizontally. Thus, their horizontal forces would be proportional to their stiffness and their lengths. By making them equal, one could solve for their forces.

General Displacement Method

Force (flexibility) Method considers static indeterminacy. The Displacement (stiffness) Method considers kinematic indeterminacy. This imagines the structure as a series of members connected to nodes at joints, supports, and member ends. Unknowns are the independent displacements and slopes needed to be solved in order to define the structure's deformed shape (only values at nodes considered as there are infinite values along the beam).

Degrees of freedom: number of displacements or slopes that are unknown. In two dimensions, each node can have at most two linear displacements and one rotational displacement. See below for four degree of freedom system:

Procedure for analysis (Example):

  1. Determine the degree of kinematic indeterminacy. Thus is the number of degrees of freedom
  2. Establish a kinematically determinate primary structure
  3. Determine fixed-end moments in beam from determine primary structure (counter-clockwise sense). The table is useful for this
  4. Apply a unit displacement or rotation at the node where it was made determinate. Positive sign convention is taken as counterclockwise. The benefits of this model are that it's easier to come up with combatibility equations and it doesn't require separate procedures for determinate/indeterminate structures.

The stiffness of the structure (K) is independent of load. If different load combinations need to be considered, the stiffness is not affected. Thus, load effects like FEMs and FEFs, as well as joint-applied forces, can be changed but the stiffness remains the same.

Matrix Method (Direct Stiffness Method)

Matrix method: more general form of the displacement (general stiffness) method. It standardizes the treatment of members, loads, and deflections. It considers all nodel degrees of freedom rather than just identified unknowns, is well suited to automation using computers, and is a simplified form of finite element analysis.
