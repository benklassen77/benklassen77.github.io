Introduction

Dynamic problems are dynamic because of the following

  1. Time-varying nature of load: Not a single solution, but solution at different times of interest
  2. Inertial effect of dynamic response: internal moment, shear, and deflection shape depend on load an inertial force opposing them. If inertial force significantly contributes to the structure response, dynamic investigation could be required.

Several forms of dynamic loads are machinery, earthquake, footfall, vehicle, and impact. There are 2 types:

  • Prescribed: exact representaiton of load is available. Deterministic analysis is used
  • Stochastic loading: inherently uncertain in magnitude and time variation. Non-deterministic analysis used

Problems are solved by taking a physical system and breaking it down into an analytical model, and then a mathematical model. Analytical models are either continuous or discrete. Continuous results in the exact representation of the displacement and have infinite DOF. Discrete parameter models are lumped mass models with finite DOF. Single degree of freedom (SDOF) models have mass lumped at a single point

Distributed parameter models lead to PDEs; for the cantilever problem, the governing PDF is ∂2∂x2[EI(x)∂2v(x,t)∂x2+m(x)∂2v∂t2]=P(x,t)\frac{\partial^2}{\partial{x^2}}[EI(x)\frac{\partial^2v(x,t)}{\partial{x^2}}+m(x)\frac{\partial^2v}{\partial{t^2}}]=P(x,t)∂x2∂2​[EI(x)∂x2∂2v(x,t)​+m(x)∂t2∂2v​]=P(x,t). Dampers are also included in models. A viscous damping element is a damper that dissipates mechanical energy. The 3 main sources of damping include:

  • Mechanical damping, such as microcracks in concrete and yielding of steel
  • Interfacial damping, such as friction between connectors
  • Aerodynamic damping, such as water and offshore structure
