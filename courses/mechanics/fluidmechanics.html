Lecture 1 - Introduction

Water exists in 3 states. Below is a comparison amongth these.

  • solids are tightly packed. Holds shape w/o container. The molecules are not mobile due to strong intermolecular forces, causing small molecular spacing
  • liquids are less packed. Takes shape of container. Molecules freely move even though there are strong intermolecular forces, causing small molecular spacing
  • gases are very free. Expand to fill closed container. Molecules move freely w/ interaction, and have large molecular spacing

Fluid: a substance that continually deforms under shear stress. Liquids and gases are both fluids

  • special materials exist where solids behave like liquids
  • fluids of interest are liquid water and gaseous air between temperature of 0º - 40º C
  • for air, N = 78.08%, O = 20.95%, Argon = 0.93%, carbon dioxide = 0.035%

Fluid Mechanics: study of fluids under all conditions of rest and motion. 3 categories:

  1. Statics (fluids at rest)
  2. Kinematics (fluids in motion w/o considering cause of motion)
  3. Dynamics (fluids in motion considering the causes of motion)

Hydraulics: science that deals w/ mechanical behaviour of water at rest or in motion

  • more empirical than fluid mechanics, occuring on a large scale

Lecture 3 - Surface Tension

Cohesion: the self attraction of liquid molecules, which allows a liquid to resist tensile forces

Adhesion: the attraction of liquid molecules to a solid, which allows it to adhere to or cover the solid

Surface Tension: property exhibited at the junction of a liquid and a gas, or two immiscible liquids. Molecules of the liquid exhibit cohesive forces, forming a thin membrane that covers the liquid

σ=FL(Surface Tension)\sigma = \frac{F}{L}\tag{Surface Tension} σ=LF​(Surface Tension)

  • F is tensile force
  • L is the length of the perimeter of the contact line. It would be 2d + 2l simply for a cylindrical rod floating on water

Another definition of surface tension could be as follows: energy required to increase the surface area of a liquid over the unit area.

σ=EA(Energy Surface Tension)\sigma = \frac{E}{A}\tag{Energy Surface Tension} σ=AE​(Energy Surface Tension)

When two immiscible fluids are in contact and also w/ a solid, the fluid with the higher adhesion tends to spread over or wet the solid.

Wetting fluid: a spreading fluid that makes a solid wet do to its adhesion. In the case of a water drop on glass, the water is the wetting fluid and the air is the non-wetting fluid

Hydrophilic surface: defines the surface when the angle of contact is less than 90º. This is because the adhesion forces between the liquid and solid surfaces is greater than the internal cohesion between liquid molecules.

Lecture 4 - Continuation

Capillarity/Capillary action: the tendency of a liquid to rise in narrow tubes or to be drawn into small openings

  • caused by liquid cohesion and adhesion to glass
  • responsible for water being drawn into narrow soil openings or into openings between fibres of a dry paper towel
  • when a capillary tube is placed into a container of water, water rises up the tube because of the adhesive force between the water and the glass pulls the water up the tube. The contact angle in a glass capillary tube for water is similar to the contact angle of water on a glass surface
  • when a fluid is non-wetting, like mercury on glass, the liquid will display capillary repulsion (analogous to the water droplet beading on the Teflon surface)

Vapour Pressure: the pressure exerted by a gas vapour on its corresponding liquid phase in equilibrium in a closed system

  • reported as an absolute pressure Pabs=Patm+PgaugeP_{abs} = P_{atm} + P_{gauge}Pabs​=Patm​+Pgauge​
  • marks the transition of a liquid to a gas
  • if the absolute pressure in a liquid is reduced to below its vapour pressure, the liquid will boil to become a vapour
  • one way to increase vapour pressure is increasing temperature of the liquid
  • another way to increase vapour pressure is by increasing atmospheric pressure (eg. climbing mount everest)

Hydrostatic Pressure

Pressure: force per unit area

  • F acts normal to the surface with area A
  • as the A shrinks, pressure can be considered as a point measurement
  • related to the weight of fluid

Pressure Scales: two types

  1. Absolute pressure is always positive (or zero)
    • always pushes on the area of interest
    • datum is zero
  2. Gauge pressure is a relative pressure that can be positive or negative
    • conceptually pushes (+) or pulls (-) on the area of interest
    • gauge datum is usually a reference of normal atmospheric pressure

Pabs=Patm+PgaugeP_{abs}=P_{atm}+P_{gauge} Pabs​=Patm​+Pgauge​

  • a and g represent the pressure units. psas = absolute psi, psig = gauge psi
  • always referenced as gauge pressure
  • standard atmospheric pressure at sea level: 14.7 psi, 101.3 kPa, 760 mmHg, 1 atm, 10.34 m water
    • thus, 20 psig = 34.7 psia

Local atmospheric pressure: the air pressure at a location of interest. Reported as an absolute pressure, often called barometric pressure

  • decreases with increasing elevation
  • water boils at lower temperatures at higher elevations due to the lower atmospheric pressure and hence vapour pressure
  • density of air is lower at high altitudes, thus nose bleeds are more common etc

Pascal's Law: pressure is independent of direction. ρx=ρs=ρz\rho_x = \rho_s = \rho_zρx​=ρs​=ρz​

  • pressure doesn't change horizontally, only vertically

Δρ=γΔh=−γΔz(Vertical Pressure)\Delta{\rho} = \gamma{\Delta{h}} = -\gamma{\Delta{z}}\tag{Vertical Pressure} Δρ=γΔh=−γΔz(Vertical Pressure)

Rules for stepping through fluid systems to determine pressures:

  1. Start at a point with known pressure or a defined pressure variable
  2. Move horizontally through a fluid dnc the pressure if the fluid is hydraulically continuous (fluid-fluid interface is not crossed
  3. Moving vertically downward increases pressure
  4. Moving vertically upward decreases pressure
  5. If system has multiple fluids, move from interface to interface until you reach the point of interest. The pressure at an interface is the same in both fluids
  6. Typically the pressure in a gas can be assumed to be constant in gas-liquid systems, even vertically, as gas density at low pressure is very small

Manometer: liquid filled tubes that are connected to one or two containers to measure pressure or pressure difference

  • since manometer has a large diameter, the capillary effects are negligible
  • Piezometer is a form of manometer that is a simple, vertical tube open at the top that measures moderate, positive gauge pressures
  • U-Tube Manometer normally contains gauge fluids with specific weight greater than water. Measure larger or negative pressures, and pressure differences
    • Open is connected to container and other end is open
    • Differential is connected to 2 points in one container or 2 containers to measure pressure difference

Procedure for manometer analysis:

  1. Start at a container end
  2. Move to the other end progressively
    • if going down vertically, add pressure. If going up, subtract pressure.
    • if moving vertically, dn change prssure
  3. Set equal to other pressure on the other end, whether that be pressure of air (0 gauge) or the pressure of the other container

Stability

Submerged objects

  • the CB and CG are both fixed on an object when it is submerged. However, their alignment relative to vertical can change. If the object is disturbed, CB and CG are not aligned, and the bouyancy force and weight will produce a moment. If CG is below CB, the object will be stable; otherwise, it will be unstable

Floating objects

The stability of floating objects is similar to submerged objects. The CG is fixed, but the CB changes based on the volume and shape of the liquid displaced. The metacentre mc is defined as the intersection between the CB line of action and the axis of symmetry through CG

  • the speed of travel V=dsdtV = \frac{ds}{dt}V=dtds​, which can change along the streamline

Limitations of the Bernoulli Equation

  • fluid is inviscid
  • flow field is steady-state
  • density is constant along/across streamlines
  • fluid is incompressible
  • uniform flow at sections of interest
  • no mechanical devices (pumps, turbines) between points of interest

A control volume CV is a closed prism used to balance mass, E, and momentum in fluid systems. A control surface CS is a side of the CV. A control surface has an area and orientation defined by a unit normal vector (usually pointing outside of the CV - outward normal). A CS can allow fluid flow or be a no-flow boundary

The fundamental parameter for fluid movement is velocity. Velocity must be defined normal or perpendicular to a CS in order to define flow rates. If the velocity is defined in vector form, the magnitude of velocity normal to a CS is given by:
