Intro to Water Resource Engineering

Water is essential for life. Fresh potable water is rare. Periods of too much and too little water can result from natural and anthropogenic causes. Naturally, availability varies widely. Humans interrupt water movement. Hydrology is the science that encompasses the distribution, movement, and properties of water on our planet

Precipitation: process where water particles fall from the atmosphere onto Earth's surface. Interception involved precipitation being caught by buildings and vegetation before reaching ground, stemflow involves precipitation running down the main stem of plants, and throughfall involves precipitation falling through spaces in vegetation canopies

Depression storage: water not stored in bodies of water or as snow, and in small depressions on land (like a puddle)

Infiltration: water can trickle into pores or cracks in the soil after falling. Percolation includes wate moving through the unsaturated zone to groundwater table, soil water includes water stored in the unsaturated zone (above water table), and groundwater includes water stored below the water table. Water moves through either overland flow or groundwater flow

Evapotranspiration: water being converted from liquid form on the surface to gaseous form in the atmosphere (combines both evaporation and transpiration). Evaporation is when liquid becomes vapour. Transpiration is when liquid from living tissue (leaves) becomes vapour

  • We define watersheds based on topography to help keep track of water on and under the ground
    • A watershed is the area enclosed by a topographic divide such that surface runoff drains by gravity

In Southern Ontario, watersheds are managed by conservation authorities. They are mandated to ensure the conservation, restoration, and responsible management of Ontario's water, land and natural habitats through programs that balance human, environmental, and economic needs

  2. 150 mm of rain fell on 10 January 2011

Rainfall is measured with tipping buckets, rain gauges, radar, and passive microwave (satellite). Rainfall varies in both intensity and duration (see IDF Curves). A hyetograph of rainfall graphs 4 intervals of precipitation per hour (precipitation per 15 minute). Measured using a tipping bucket. These demonstrate intensity and duration

Intensity duration pairs are graphed as a Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) Curve. This descripes the relationship between rainfall intensity, rainfall duration, and return period at a given location.

Procedure to construct an IDF curve (Example):

  1. For a given time interval, or duration, calculate the maximum amount of rain (Annual Maximum Rainfal AMR) that fell during that time interval during each year of record
  2. Order the AMR values from highest to lowest and calculate the return period (T) for each using the Weibull formula (to get the rank). T=n+1mT=\frac{n+1}{m}T=mn+1​. N is period of record (total number of years), T is return period, m is rank.
  3. Interpolate between calculated values of T to find the depth of rainfall at a desired value of T (at easy return periods)
  4. Repeat steps 1-2 for a range of durations
  5. Connect the depth of rainfall/duration pairs for a given T
  6. Divide each AMR value by the corresponding duration to calculate the average intensity

IDF curves generally have 2 year, 5 year, 10 year, 25 year, 50 year, and 100 year flood. An IDF curve is for one location. To understand how rainfall varies spatially, we can take use intensity-duration pairs from a bunch of different weather stations and draw contour lines to visualize the spatial patterns.

Frequency analysis is used to understand the probability that different types of events will occur within a certain period. This carries 2 key assumptions:

  1. The types of future rainfall events are the same as historical events
  2. The same probability distribution fits the entire data set and future data

If nature of rainfall events change, it's called a statistically nonstationary time series. Additionally, if rainfall is generated by different event types, frequency analysis is not statistically correct due to samples from different populations.

To plan for extreme events, we often use the concept of the regional storm. The regional storm is the storm event for a general geographical area which produced the largest flow on record. This event is used in watershed studies to define floodplain

Probable Maximum Precipitation PMP is theoretically the greatest depth of precipitation for a given duration that is physically possible over a given size storm area at a particular geographical location at a certain time of year. The PMP is estimated by

  • using generalized PMP maps based on meteorological analysis of the largest storms in a large region

Isochrones and time of concentration

All the surface runoff will eventually make its way to the outlet point for the watershed. However, the water that falls far from the outlet or that has a very torturous/rough path to the outlet will take longer to get there than the water that falls close to the outlet or has a very straight/smooth path to the outlet. Isochrones are plotted

A hydrograph is a plot of discharge Q over time. If a storm happened where water falls at a constant intensity uniformly over a watershed, the hydrograph would increase until it hit the time of concentration.

Time of concentration tct_ctc​ is the travel time for water to travel from the most distant point of the watershed to the outlets

  • once the full area of the watershed is contributing to flow at the outlet, the discharge will stay constant because we have achieved a balance where the volume rate of rainfall will exactly equal the volume rate of outflow

Runoff

Flow at any time can come from runoff, interflow, and groundwater flow

An abstraction is any rainfall that doesn't turn into runoff. Abstractions generally include infiltration, incerception, and depression storage. Interception is the process by which rainfall is abstracted prior to reaching the ground. However, throughfall or stemflow could move this rater to become infiltration. The displacement of tree branches is sometimes used to determine amount of snow on trees. A Depression storage refers to water that accumulates in suface depressions (eg. puddle). Overland flow has two types: Hortonian (excess rainfall leads to runoff, even though the bed remains insaturated below the wetting front) and Dunne (soil is saturated) and fill-and-spill (Canada). In Dunne runoff, runoff occurs at a rate equal to the hydraulic conductivity of the soil

Baseflow: water in the channel that comes from groundwater. Flow from the saturated regions of the ground reacts slowly to the new rainfall due to the time it takes for water to infiltrate and percolate down to the water table. Quickflow: anything above baseflow (direct precipitation + interflow + runoff)

There are several methods to split baseflow from quickflow:

  1. Constant slope method - draws line between 1st inflection point and 2nd inflection point. Assumes baseflow starts to increase at the same moment as the onset of surface runoff
  2. Concave method - follow slope of original line until peak of runoff. Assumes baseflow lags the runoff (baseflow only increases after Q peak)
  3. Constant-baseflow method - assumes baseflow doesn't respond to rainfall

Interflow: water comes to channel from unsaturated areas of ground

The rational method can estimate the peak discharge, QpQ_pQp​, using C as a runoff coefficient, i as the intensity, and A as the area. See below:

Qp=CiAQ_p=CiA Qp​=CiA

This assumes that the length of storm is ≥ the time of concentration, the rainfall is uniform in time and space for the duration of the event across the watershed area, and that abstractions such as infiltration, depression storage, and interception can be represented using a coefficient.

The hydrograph is influenced by the shape of the watershed. If the area increases along the watershed, it makes sense that there would be more runoff in the latter part, and thus the hydrograph would be right-skewed. Drainage density, or the length of channels/drainage area, also influences the hydrograph because water runs off faster in channels than overland. The more channels, the earlier the peak and the shorter the duration. Steep slopes also indicate quicker runoff. Impervious surfaces indicate rapid rise to runoff peak.

Additionally, larger basin size increases volume of runoff, and are apt for longer duration floods. Engineered ponds are thus made to slow down water, reduce the magnitude of the QpQ_pQp​ and to encourage both evaporation and infiltration.

Unit Hydrograph

To produce the unit hydrograph, the direct runoff from consecutive storms are recorded. The unit hydrograph considers the areas of the catchment and understands the difference in amount of runoff per hour since certain areas are unable to runoff due to being a distance away from the point of interest. A lag exists every hour due to the distance between areas and the point of interest. See example:

The unit hydrograph (UH) is defined as the outflow response from one unit of direct runoff over a fixed duration. In Canada, UHs are constructed for a DR unit of 1 cm over 1 hour. Each UH is unique to the watershed upstream of the location for which it was derived

Convolution is the process of predicting streamflow using unit hydrographs.

The hyetograph of DRs is called the direct runoff hyetograph (DRH)

Unit Hydrograph Assumptions

  1. Basin characteristics are reflected in the hydrograph shape
  2. Linearity: twice the excess rainfall doubles the hydrograph ordinates
  3. Time Base: rainfall excesses of equal durations produce hydrographs of equivalent time bases, regardless of intensity
  4. Superposition: multiple consecutive storms can be added to produce the composite hydrograph
  5. Independence of Antecedent Precipitation: the time distribution of direct runoff is independent of previous precipitation

To determine hydrograph (deconvolution);

  1. Remove baseflow from streamflow hydrograph (now starts from zero). This changes the hydrograph from a storm hydrograph to a (DRH).

River ecosystems

Engineering purposes often assume that rivers are graded. The sediment budget for a channel reach assumes that Qs = Qin. This assumption cannot be made at the network level, meander bend level, or the sediment grain level, so engineers work at the reach level.

A graded river is one that has been made so that sediment leaving is the same as sediment coming. The reach has had a slope adjusted over time so that the energy provided by the flow is just enough to transport the supplied sediment.

This concept of grade doesn't apply at very short time scales, nor can grade in rivers apply over long distances or times. The slope of the reach (grade) is assumed to remain somewhat constant for 10-50 years by engineers. In a graded river, the system has adjusted over time so that the needle in the middle points straight down (neither aggradation or degradation will occur). As t→∞t\to\inftyt→∞, rivers drag mountains to the sea. This means that assumption no longer holds true. See below for a visual of the balance.
