Introduction

Transportation - Purpose and objectives:

  1. Provide mobility: move people and goods at an acceptable level of service
  2. Provide accessibility: to economic and social activities, which are distributed over space and time. Mobility and accessibility go hand-in-hand
  3. Provide safety: mitigate traffic fatalities and severe injuries
    • 2000 people killed and 165000 injured using Canada's road transportation system
  4. Promote economic prosperity: bad transportation hurts the economy, while good transportation boosts the economy
  5. Promote environmental sustainability: encourage sustainable development of land use and reduce energy consumption and pollutant emissions
    • transportation sector contributed 34% to anthropogenic GHG emissions in Ontario in 2012
    • 7 Gt CO2 equivalent of direct GHG emissions in 2010
  6. Promote equity: essential accomplished by designing transportation system that are fair

Benefits of transportation

  • fulfill demand, move people and goods
  • emergency and defence benefits

Costs of transportation

  • time
  • energy, material and land use
  • noise
  • pollution
  • accidents

Balance between benefit and cost

  • plan, design, and operate a cost-effective system
  • maximize benefits while minimizing costs

Macroscopic Traffic Flow

Traffic Flow Theory

  • development of mathematical models or relationships between variables characterizing traffic flow
  • useful for description and quantification of traffic flow, for all aspects of planning, design, and operations
  • help build simulation models with artificial traffic behaving similar to reality

Analytical (deterministic) -> Simulation (stochastic)

  • stochastic accounts for randomness and creates a distribution while analytical shows one answer

Modelling Approaches

  • Macroscopic flow: deals with characteristics of the roadway. Each roadway segment has a flow, density, and speed
  • Microscopic flow: deals with characteristics of individual vehicles. Each vehicle has a position, speed, and acceleration
  • Mesoscopic (outside scope of course): takes stuff from both macroscopic and microscopic

The continuity equation states that ∂k∂t+∂q∂x=r−s\frac{\partial{k}}{\partial{t}}+\frac{\partial{q}}{\partial{x}}=r-s∂t∂k​+∂x∂q​=r−s, with r and s being on and off ramps. If there are no ramps, this is equal to zero. This is used to derive the following:

To do a complete macroscopic simulation:

  1. Use conservation equation to update density k
  2. Use fundamental diagrams to update speed u
  3. Use definitional relationship to update flow q

The q used is the minimum between the demand flow leaving section i and the supply flow that can be accommodated at i+1. The demand flow, qDq_DqD​ is kukuku if k<kok<k_ok<ko​, and is qmaxq_{max}qmax​ if k>kok>k_ok>ko​. This is because it is the demand flow, not the capability flow. Simulation captures some essential phenomena:

  • decreasing speed with increasing density
  • shock wave propagation
  • consistent with a number of microsimulation models at steady state

Here are some limitations of the simulation

  • instantaneous reaction of speed to density, implying infinite acceleration/deceleration rates
  • fails to reproduce congestion instability (stop and go traffic) due to neglected dynamics
  • flow downstream of congestion equals bottle neck capacity (not capturing start up delay)

To fix these, 2nd order models are created. These replace fundamental diagrams (Greenshield or Greenberg created for steady-state) with a dymanic mean-speed equation (Payne, 1979)

Shock Wave Analysis

Congestion occurs when:

  1. Demand exceeds normal capacity (recurrent congestion) (k>kok>k_ok>ko​)
  2. Capacity drops for a period of time due to an incident (non-recurrent congestion)

Congestion spreads in space over time until either demand subsides or capacity is restored (eg. construction finished). The extent and severity of congestion depends on the congestion wave speed, the clearing wave speed, and the duration of the incident.

The congestion shock wave results from the incident at the back of the shock wave. Here, there is faster traffic upstream. As obstruction remains, congestion wave gets larger. The clearing shock wave occurs at the front of the shock wave. Here, there is faster traffic downstream. In essence, a shockwave is a boundary between two different traffic states. There are two different zones with respective values of (q,k,u).

By conservation, the # of vehicles leaving one state is the same as the # of vehicles entering the other in a certain period of time. Thus, N1=N2N_1=N_2N1​=N2​, or q1t=q2tq_1t=q_2tq1​t=q2​t [(u1−wu_1-wu1​−w)k1t=(k_1t=(k1​t=(u_2-w)))k_2t$]

Traffic Control

Objectives

  1. Maximize safety
  2. Maximize efficiency
  3. Minimize side effects

To be effective, control must fulfill a need, command attention, convey a clear simple meaning, comply with reasonable expectations, and give adequate time for proper response. Effectiveness can be achieved through proper design, proper placement, proper operation and maintenance, and universal uniformity.

Actuated Signals

Pre-timed signals do not respond to demand. This can be a problem. Semi-actuated detectors exist on minor streets only, while fully-actuated streets are for all approaches. It is important to note that cycle length, phase length, and phase sequence may vary from cycle to cycle

The minumum green is the the minimum time that should allow pedestrians to cross safely. It should also allow vehicles between the detector and the stop line to clear the intersection. The maximum green is to avoid locking the green in a given direction. This is the same as it is in a pre-timed scenario. The Passage time/unit extension allows a vehicle to travel from the detector to the stopline (clear light).

In a Max Out, back-to-back activations continue until the upper limit. This means that the phase is terminated. In a Gap Out, passage time elapses without an activation. The phase is terminated if this happens. If the recall switch is on, light goes to red when phase ends. If not, it stays green until a detector activation happens in the other direction.

Demand: the desired level of usage (people willing to travel, not necessarily those who do). For a given state of economy, demand is a function of cost. The lower the cost, the higher the demand. Cost would be money, or other things like travel time. Worse economy decreases demand, so performance increases due to lack of demand. Change in land use can also increase demand

Supply: the infrastructure provided for transportation (eg. roads, trains)

Performance: relates cost to level of usage. Travel time is dependent on how many choose to travel. The higher the level of usage, the higher the cost. The link performance function models this relationship

Equilibruim: if everything stays the same, you will have the same level of usage, and same travel time

Latent Demand: if you improve the highway, more will come

User Equilibrium

It is assumed that rational behaviour implies selecting or switching to shorter routes if one exists. User Equilibrium is reached when no traveller can improve his or her travel time by switching routes. Wardrop's Principle indicates that at this state of equilibrium, all used routes have equal travel time and less than the travel time on any unused route (eg. Route 66)

The first way to figure out where the speeds are the same are by plotting them both on a graph and drawing a horizontal line when they are parallel. The second way is to rearrange the t functions of v to be v functions of t and then add them together, then rearranging back into a t function. This cab then be plotted where it intersects with the bottom line. The total volume is plotted on the combined function, and the volumes are horizontally found on both the other lines. Alternatively, both of the original lines are plotted in opposite directions to each other, with t being on the y and V being on the x. The point of intersection indicates how much volume is on each side. Finally, t1=t2t_1=t_2t1​=t2​. This means that it can be solved using equations.

System Optimal: UE is not necessarily optimal for the system because users are selfish. The SO means the total travel tine in the system is minimized. SO is interesting to the system operator. At this point, the marginal costs on all used routes are equal. This is the increase in total system cost (time) per unit increase in flow. SO isn't stable, as users could switch if they could.

Transportation systems are represented by a network of links (streets, lines) and nodes (intersections, stations, stops).

A path or route is a sequence of transportation network links between an origin and a destination.

The sets of internal and external zones are established based on study objectives. Zones are represented by a node which is usually termed a centroid. It is possible to have more or less zones than nodes. However, there is only one centroid per zone. Centroids are connected to nodes in zones.

Trip Generation

4-step model, or the classic transport model, is the common model for traffic distribution

Step 1: Trip generation. This is the determination of the total number of trips produced by each origin i and the total trips attracted by each zone j

Step 2: Trip distribution. This is determining how much travel along different zones considering totals. Total trips produced by zone i (origin) attracted to zone j (destination)

Step 3: Mode Split. This splits traffic into auto and transit

Step 4: Trip Assignment: considering how transit comes together (user equilibrium assumed)

Model Development Process

Models relate one or more dependent or endogeneous variables (Y) to one or more independent or exogenous or explanatory variables (X). Eg. y=f(X,β)y=f(X,\beta )y=f(X,β). Process:

  1. Specification. Choose the model structure (make assumptions), variables to include x, and the functional forms
  2. Model estimation and calibration. Using observed data to find the best model parameters.
    • Estimation: using statistical techniques
    • Calibration: by optimizing one or more goodness-of-fit measure
  3. Validation. comparing the model predictions with information not used during the process of model estimation
  4. Application

The bulk of the data to determine origin-destination pairs comes from household travel surveys

Trip generation models

These models predict the trip ends generated by a person, zone, or household on a daily or peak-period basis. Household makes sense because it demonstrates the relatinoship among the household. Not everybody is on the road

There are 2 parts of the trip, the production and the attraction. Home is always on the production end of the trip. For other departures (eg. shop to work), the place left is the production.

Variables affectiong trip productions include household income, auto ownership, household size, number of workers per household, residential density, and distance to the CBD (central business district, or urban core)

Vairables affecting trip attractions: zonal employment levels (by type), zonal floor space (by type), accessibility to workforce

2 types of commonly used models: cross-classification models and linear-regression models. Additionally, models are produced for different types of trips and on a person, household or zonal basis

Cross-Classification models: often used for trip-production

  • For trip production, group households are analyzed according to common socioeconomic characteristics (eg. household size, income). Average trip rates computed for each group
  • For trip attraction, analysis based on employment type (eg. manufacturing) and possible employment density

Problems with cross-classification models

  • can require more detailed data than typical regression models, both for model estimation, but also for application (predicting future trips).
  • no goodness-of-fit measure associated with calibration
    • can validate (predicted vs. observed)
  • Reliability of each trip rate varies (depends on number of observations)
  • no objective way to choose variables for classification or aggregation scheme

Regression models: often used for trip-attraction

  • ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is used to predict trip generation as a linear function of one or more of the previously listed variables
  • developed with a forward selection (adding variables one at a time to determine statistical significance), a stepwise selection (modification of forward selection that drops variables from the model if they lose their significance as other variables are added), or a backward selection (starts with fitting a model with all variables of interest), and then reduce model until all remaining variables are statistically significant. Alternatively, brute force method estimates all possible model specifications, and chooses model with best fit

Problems with linear regression models

  • correlation among explanatory variables
  • assumes explanatory variables have linear and additive impacts
  • parameter estimates may be unintuitive
  • zonal averages obscure zonal variations, which may lead to problematic results

Mode Choice

Mode choice is influenced by the following

  1. Characteristics of the trip maker (drivers license, income)
  2. Characteristics of the journey (trip purpose, time of day)
  3. Characteristics of transport facility (travel time, cost)

There are 2 principle assumptions in utility theory

  1. The degree of preference of an individual toward a choice option can be expressed using a number called utility. This utility is a function of the characteristics of the individual and the choice option
  2. Individual always chooses the option with the highest utility function value. Thus assumption is also called the utility maximization principle

Disadvantages
