Introduction

Geotechnical Engineering is the branch of civil engineering that studies the mechanical, hydraulic, and engineering properties of earth's materials, such as soils and rocks. Soil mechanics, rock mechanics, and engineering geology are all used to analyze, design, and construct all structures made on soil or rock

Soil: thin layer on the Earth's surface made of weathered rocks and organic material. Soil mechanics can be divided into 3 distinctions

  1. Soil characterization and classification: the study of physical and index properties of soils
  2. Flow of fluids through soil masses: when hydrostatic conditions are not accomplished into a soil mass, fluids will be flowing through it and seepage will occur. It must be known where the water table is
  3. Strength and deformability: soil is a very heterogeneous material with randomly distributed voids between particles. The stress distribution and compressibility potential for the soil mass must be known

Problems that are solved with soil mechanics includes bearing capacity for building foundations, improving building sites, slope stability for excavations, design of retaining walls and other earth-supported structures.

Origin of soil

Soil is a blend of minerals, organic matter, water and air. They are formed through weathering of rocks and minerals over thousands of years, involving breaking down rocks into smaller pieces. Physical weathering is the breaking down of bigger rocks into smaller pieces. Chemical weathering is when the smaller pieces have a different chemical composition than parent rock

There are 4 main soils: gravel, sand, silt, and clay. Naturally, soils are a mix of these. See below

It is also necessary to classify soil based on engineering behaviour. For soil nature, considerations are as follows: highly variable spatially (heterogeneous), nonlinear stress-strain response, inelastic, anisotropic (depositional history)s, and multiphase systems (soil, water, air).

Earth movements contribute to these factors. See below

Gradation: the process in which topography is changed by action of water, air, ice, and weathering factors. It arises from geomorphic properties

The rock cycle is another process over millions of years. It involves disintegration (mechanical action by wind, water, glaciation, ΔT\Delta{T}ΔT, Freeze thaw, splitting action of plant roots, and abrasive movements), and decomposition (chemical action by oxidation, hydration, carbonation, and vegetation). This is partly aided by weathering, both mechanical and chemical. Sometimes, chemical weathering can create sinkholes. See below

From this cycle, several types of rocks originate. See below:

Chemical and boilogic sedimentary rocks susceptible to deformation and low strength based on the way they are formed. Due to the pressure of ice, ice depositd soils are the strongest.

Soils are transportated by water and wind. Water transported soils have low bearing capacity, have high compressibility, and are good for agricultural purposes and dams. For water and wind-blown soils, the heaviest material is deposited first. Soils are susceptible to liquifaction.

Based on transportation and weathering, there are 7 soil types.

  1. Residual soils: formed when the weathered products stay at their place of origin
  2. Glacial soils: formed by transportation and deposition of soils
  3. Alluvial soils: formed when transported by running water and deposited along streams
  4. Aeolian soilsL transported and deposited by wind
  5. Marine soils: formed by deposition in the seas
  6. Lectustrine: formed by deposition in quiet lakes
  7. Colluvial soils: formed by movement of soil from its original place by gravity, such as during landslides

It is very important to know the soil. This is because engineers need to identify where to do investigation, and from the investigation what parameters to expect

Particulate Media

The strength of soil is affected by its shape. Round shape allows for greater void ratio. Soils with larger void spaces in their fabric are more susceptible to deformation than those with smaller void spaces. Sometimes, strength is lost w/ lab because soil pressure and shape is lost. Thus, field testing is important.

Sand and gravel have are course grained. This means that their behaviour is governed by particle size. They are non-plastic and do not hold water. The particle sizes of these range from 0.075 mm to 75 mm. Clay and silt are fine grained. This means that they behave plastically, are sticky, and have small grains that interact with water. If silt has particles 75 microns or larger, it will behave as a sand. If smaller, it will behave as a plastic material like a clay. In nature, never exactly 75 microns. If sticky, acts as clay. The particle sizes of these range from <0.002 mm to 0.075 mm (#200 Sieve).

Soil classification

Most common classification system is the Unified Soil Classification System USCS. 2 letters are assigned. First Letter: major component of soil. Course grained is G (gravel) or S (sand), fine grained is M (silt) or C (clay), organic soil is O and peat is Pt. Second letter: other component. Course grained is W (well-graded) or P (poorly-graded), or it could be M (contains silt) or C (contains clay). Fine grained soil could be H (high plasticity) or L (low plasticity).

One important application of particle size distribution is frost heave. This occurs when water is drawn from below towards the freezing front, forming lenses of ice. Whether or not water may be drawn to the freezing front is governed by the pore size, which is a function of the grain size distribution of the soil. Pore size may be small enough to allow capillary action, pulling pore water up to the freezing front, or large enough to have high permeability that allows water to migrate fast enough. Silts maximize ice lens production because of their high suction and permeability. Road base materials typically have mo more than 3% silt. Frost susceptibility occurs if >3% passes on 0.02 mm.

Fine soils

Coarse-grained: particulate soils, Fine-grained: identified on the basic of plasticity

The higher a specific surface of a clay, the more plastic it should behave. Specific surface increases as thickness and diameter decreases. Montmorillonite is very small, so it has a specific surface of 800.

Clay is negatively charged for 3 reasons. First, the centre atom is positively charged. This atom can be replaced by another atom of a lower valence. Secondly, the hydroxyl ions from water can create a negative charge when the hydrogen ions are released. Finally, there are broken bonds at the edges of particles. This attracts water hydrogen ions

Dry clays: cations are tightly held by negatively charged surface, cations neutralize negative surface charge. Cations in excess precipitate in salt. Clays are cohesive

Wet clays: precipitated salts go into solution. Adsorbed cations are at a high concentration. Diffusion takes place to equalize n, and diffusion is limited by the attraction of cations and repultion of anions. The diffuse double layer is present

In general, the diffuse double layer thickness ddd α\alphaα DTv2n0.5\frac{DT}{v^2n}^{0.5}v2nDT​0.5, with D being dielectric constant, T being temperature, v being ionic valence, and n being ionic concentration. The thicker the double layer, the higher the repulsive forces between particles (more coating, more repulsion), the higher the swelling pressure, the higher the liquid limit, and the lower the flocculation tendency (opposite to swelling).

Clays range from soft (high void ratio, high water content) to stiff (low void ratio, low water content). Soft clays have high compressibility, low strength, and low permeability, while stiff clays have low compressibility, high strength, and low permeability.

The Shrinkage Limit (SL or wsw_sws​) is the threshold water content exceeding which the clay shifts from behaving like a brittle solid to a semi-solid material. The degree of saturation is 1 at and above the SL. The Plastic Limit (PL or wpw_pwp​) is the threshold water content exceeding which the clay starts behaving like a plastic material. The Liquid Limit (LL or wLw_LwL​) is the threshold water content exceeding which the clay starts behaving like liquid. At the LL, strength is approximately 2.5 kPa. Thus, the plasticity index is the range of water content within which a soil exhibits plastic behaviour IP=wL−wPIP=w_L-w_PIP=wL​−wP​ and the liquidity index is the proximity of soil state to liquid and plastic limits IL=w−wPIPIL=\frac{w-w_P}{IP}IL=IPw−wP​​, with IL=1 being LL and IL=0 being PL.

Consolidation

Consolidation: any process which involves decrease in water content of a saturated soil without replacement of water by air. It is the settlement that occurs in fine-grained soils

Structures are designed to 3 properties: deformation, stability, and cost. The deformation is caused due to settlement.

As a rule of thumb, site investigation must go at least 2 times the width of the structure.

Settlement

There are 3 components of settlement:

  1. Immediate Settlement SiS_iSi​: caused by elastic deformation of soil without any change in moisture content. Based on theory of elasticity and occurs in sands and clays
  2. Primary Consolidation Settlement ScS_cSc​: direct result of the volume change in saturated cohesive soils because of the expulsion of water that occupies the void spaces. Primary consolidation is a time-dependent process that occurs in saturated fine-grained soil, which has a low coefficient of permeability. Happens for clays only
  3. Secondary Consolidation Settlement SsS_sSs​: is observed in saturated cohesive soils and is the result of the plastic adjustment of soil fabrics. It follows the primary consolidation settlement under a constant effective stress. Secondary consolidation is also time-dependent deformation. Happens for sands and clays, but important for clays only

Because of this difference, clays and sands settle differently. Clays settle in the middle most, while sands settle at the edges. The pressure distribution must be designed accordingly.

Soil Compression

Soils are assumed to not compress upon loading. Primary soil skeleton does compress ScS_cSc​. Here, particles rearrange themselves, reducing void ratio. Particles will bend and crush, and water and air in void space moves out

In sand, particles rearrange very quickly. Water drains quickly because of the high hydraulic conductivity. Thus, it occurs faster (by time construction complete). In clay, water drains very slowly due to the low hydraulic conductivity. Compression can take months or years, so the settlement is known as consolidation.
