Systems Theory

Systems fit in the civil engineering body of knowledge. Becoming and being recognized as a professional civil engineer requires both a basic technical education and a broader education acquired through experience and lifelong continuous learning

  • systems under technical knowledge

Examples of civil engineering systems include:

  • infrastructure
  • transportation
  • structural
  • water and wastewater

Less obvious:

  • construction project management systems
  • building systems
  • cloud-based systems
  • supply chains
  • design work flow management

Three objectives of a sustainable system: economic, environmental, social. Often have conflict. For example, water needs to meet people's demands, budget, and environmental standards. Thus, the purpose of a system is to serve the community in a sustainable fashion. Stella is an example system dynamics modelling tool

A system is something that transports inputs into outputs. It is a flow of material, energy, and information. Example: respiratory system, highway system, gear, ants in a colony, biodiversity in ecosystem; control system, information, mechanical systems, management systems; biological systems, banking systems, socio-economic systems, political systems

A system is an assemblage of parts united into an organized whole. It typically has a purpose, and is made of components, networks, subsystems, inputs, outputs, environmental forces, and a boundary. Anything within the boundry is part of whole system. Anything outside is environmental forces

  • Components: operating parts of a system consisting of input, process and output
  • Attributes: the properties or discernible manifestations of the components of a system. They characterize the system
  • Relationships: the links between components and attributes. They connect systems and subsystems

A subsystem is a a component in a given system that can be broken down into smaller components that work together in order to achieve a purpose. Systems are evaluated on effectiveness by their intended purpose and desired outputs

Classification of systems

  1. Natural and human-made systems
    • Natural are those naturally occurring
    • Human-made systems are those that humans have intervened with components, attributes, or relationships
  2. Physical and conceptual systems
    • Physical are those that manifest themselves in physical forms. They are constructed with physical components and occupy spaces
    • Conceptual are those in which symbols represent the attributes of components. Ideas, plans, concepts, computer programs and hypotheses
  3. Static and dynamic systems
    • Static are those having structure without activity
    • Dynamic are those that combine structural components with activity (eg. school)
  4. Closed and Open system
    • Closed: do not interact significantly with their environment. Output isolated from input
    • Open feedback system allow matters to cross in both directions

Composed of components, attributes and relationships

System design principles

  1. Systems design is an iterative process that evolves the design from a simple initial abstract representation to a detailed design that is implemented or communicated.
  2. Design starts with a need, from which the conflicting objectives, scope, constraints, and criteria evolve, as well as the quantitative definitions of the design problem.
  3. Many solutions should be generated and an intuitive and emotional leap to an immediate solution should be avoided.
  4. Solutions are analyzed for feasibility
  5. It is usually worth optimizing one or more solutions, and this will often require very formal and rigorous modeling and analysis

  • star-connected single hop planning, design
  • two-tiered hierarchal cluster hub and spoke system for air transportation
  • multi-hop mesh structural health sensors on a bridge

Complex systems are systems whose behaviour is intrinsically difficult to model due to the dependencies, competitions, relationships, or other types of interactions between their parts or between a given system and its environment. Systems that are complex have distinct properties that arise from these relationships. Eg. nonlinearity, emergence, spontaneous order, adaptation, feedback loops. Some complex systems are intelligent transportation systems, mega-projects, supply chains and networks, andcommodities (steel, cement etc.) price fluctuations and their impact on project and asset management risk. As a result of interdependance, mechanisms that are subjected to positive, reinforcing feedback loops, which cause fat tails. They prevent the working of the central limit theorum where extreme limits on the bell curve are fatter. Extreme events happen more often. In general, complex systems have a great degree of interdependence, both temporal (variable depends on its past changes), horizontal (variables depend on one another), and diagonal (variable A depends on the past history of variable B). Problems from complex systems include sudden paralyzing traffic jams w/ complex road system, radical price fluctuations w/ complex governments, andcollapsing markets w/ complex market

Decision Theory

People often make decisions based on immediate impressions. Kahneman argued in Thinking Fast and Slow that this is "substitution" and leads to bad decision making.

Tools available to engineers to make decisions include optimization, probability theory, decision theory, game theory, graph theory, mathematical modeling and simulation, and statistical analysis

Types of decisions

Engineering decisions are as objective as possible. This is different than real, human life.

Decisions under certainty have only one possible outcome for each action and this outcome is sure to occur. The decision is made to choose the action that will result in the most desirable outcome (cost or time)

Decisions under probabilistic uncertainty: there are several outcomes for each action depending on the state of nature. These states happen with known probabilities. In this category, the outcomes are uncertain but the probabilities associated with these outcomes are known precisely

Decisions under probabilistic imprecision: there are several outcomes for each action depending on the state of nature. These states happen with unknown or imprecise probabilities. In this category, the outcomes are uncertain and the probabilities associated with these outcomes are not known precisely

Decisions under information imperfection: there are several outcomes for each action depending on the state of nature. These states happen with imperfect possibilities. In this category, the outcomes are uncertain and the probabilities associated with these outcomes are not known precisely

Decisions under conflict and cooperation: there is more than a single decision maker. The objectives of different decision makers may differ and may not be known to other decision makers (game theory and negotiation and nash equilibrium)

Decisions under risk: each action results in one of the outcomes with a known probability. The probabilities are assumed to be known to decision makers. The impacts of the outcomes are also assumed to be known

Decision Matrix: put alternatives in left hand column, states of nature on top

Decision theory is based on 3 basic concepts: alternative, outcome, and state of nature. Alternatives can be open and new alternatives can be invented or discovered. However, they are assumed to be closed in decision theory. There's 2 types of closed sets: alternatives with voluntary closure (dicision maker closes set) and alternatives with involuntary closure (closure imposed on decision maker). Alternatives are mutually exclusive Outcomes are defined as an effect of choosing an alternative and the state of nature experienced. States of nature are defined as the various unknown factores that can affect decisions. For example, deciding to wear a raincoat is dependent on whether it rains or not.

Expected Utility

Expected Value is the sum of the product between the outcomes and their probabilities. The Expected utility brings the preferences and criteria other than monetary value into the problem. Thus, expected utility helps determine how people will make a decision and not always how they should make a decision.

Utility Function: visualizes expected utility to demonstrate how people will make decisions

Above, A depicts logical behaviour, B depicts someone careful about debt, C depicts a risk taker, D depicts declining marginal utility of money to the decision maker as more and more needs are met, and E illustrates typical human behaviour when making investment decisions.

Expected Utility Theory is commonly used when probabilities of the states of nature are known and well-defined. EU=(u1×p1)+(u2×p2)+…\textrm{EU}=(u_1\times{p_1})+(u_2\times{p_2})+\ldotsEU=(u1​×p1​)+(u2​×p2​)+…

  1. For each alternative, a weighted average of the utility values under different states of nature is assigned
  2. The probabilities of these states are used to weight these averages
  3. Then the expected utility theory selects an action that maximizes the expected value of the resulting utility

Game theory: a game is not a game of chance, but of strategy. There are multiple players that make decisions and at least one outcome (eg. win or loss). There are 3 elements to the game. Contract types

Lump-sum contract: contractor is competitively chosen based on the bid price. Risk assigned to contractoe

Unit price contract: labour, materials, and equipment prorated into units. Risk of misestimating transferred from contractor to owner

Reimbursable work contract: direct costs (labour) reimbursed at fixed rate. All risk to owner

Cost plus fixed percentage contract: used when a new technology is used or there is pressure on finishing a project. All risk on owner. Contractor receives all cost spent plus fixed percentage

Cost plus fixed fee contract: the contractor receives all the actual cost spent for the job plus an additional fixed fee. All risk on owner, but contractor may lose some profit

Target estimate contract: penalty or reward to a contractor is based on the actual cost with respect to estimated cost and completion date. Both contractor and owner share reward/penalty.

Guaranteed maximum cost contract: contractor takes all the risks, but the change orders must be minimal

Risk management

Risk: deviation of system results from expected outcomes. Although positive and negative results can ensue, it is typically looked at from the negative. Lowering risk lowers negative

There are 3 iterative steps:

Risk identification: studying the conditions in which a project can go wrong, or in which a system may experience an undesirable behaviour or outcome. This can be done by identifying classes of risks such as people, processes, systems, and nature. Sharing experience, lessons learned, post job analysis etc. are also methods

Risk analysis and evaluation: Risk=Probability×Impact\textrm{Risk}=\textrm{Probability}\times\textrm{Impact}Risk=Probability×Impact. RIP. Can be depicted with a probability-impact matrix, which has impact on vert axis and probability on x axis. High impact high probability (top right corner) needs to be addressed

Risk treatment: to address risk. Below are 4 steps in order or preference:

  1. Avoid: changing the project management plan so that the risk no longer exists
  2. Transfer: transferring risks and related responsibilities to a 3rd-party
  3. Mitigate: reducing the probability and/or impact of the risk to be within the acceptable limits
  4. Accept: accepting the risk and dealing with its potential negative consequences, since it is not possible to eliminate or reduce the risk

Internal risks: local and global. Local are risks that happen on the construction site. Global are errors in design, financial risks, location, environments, client (essentially impacts internal to the site but beyond the direct vicinity of the construction project)

External risks: broad impacts not internal to the site. Eg. climate change, political action

Popular project delivery methods include EPC (engineer, procure, construct), turnkey, BOT (build, operate, transfer), design/build, IPD (integrated project delivery)

Project Management Systems

3 main goals: Time, Quality, and Cost optimization

A controller is the medium between the desired and achieved states of a project. The desired state is first planned. Then, the sum of all activities that lead up to achieved state. The achieved state invokes the activities to occur, creating a control feedback loop.

The control loop is simplified to: Plan -> Schedule -> Execute -> Monitor -> Adjust -> Plan

Project management involves reporting and recording progress, comparing work accomplished with work planned, calculating productivity, estimating time and cost to completion, comparing this estimate with original objectives, analyzing the causes of the delays, taking appropriate action to correct, and updating the schedule. Frequency of process is daily for field progress, weekly for project management reports, and monthly for schedule updates and revisionss

Cost control considers the following:

Budgeted cost: the estimated cost of the project and is the basis for bidding for the project

Estimated total cost: reflects the total of all costs to date on a project

Earned value: dollar amount of the value of the work completed to date. Should be higher than estimated total cost

There are some other terms used in PM below:

Planned Value (PV): the authorized budget assigned to the work to be accomplished for an activity or project. Can also be referred to as budget at completion

Earned Value (EV): the value of the work performed expressed in terms of the approved budget assigned to the work for an activity. Project managers monitor the EV to determine long-term performance trends

Actual Cost (AC): the total cost actually incurred and recorde in accomplishing work performed for an activity. It has no upper limit

There are some variances. Schedule variance SV is a measure of schedule performance SV=EV−PVSV=EV-PVSV=EV−PV. Cost variance CV is a measure of cost performance CV=EV−ACCV=EV-ACCV=EV−AC. Negative values indicate being behind schule or over budget

Capital Costs: costs incurred or upfront costs. EG. Equipment, materials, labour, bid, acquisition, testing and inspection, insurance,

Operating and Maintenance Costs: utilities, insurance (also O&M if annual), rehibilitation, maintenance and repair

Decommissioning/Salvage Costs: demolition and disposal, residual environmental impacts, adaptive reuse and repurposing

Life Cycle Analysis LCA Costs: looking beyond just the life of the project. Planning horizon looks beyond just cost, but also social and environmental costs

There are a few approaches to estimating:

  1. Empirical cost inference: either linear or exponential
  2. Unit cost for bill of quantities: based on quantity take-offs from design models
  3. Allocation of joint costs: assignment or prorating of overhead costs such as site management, cranes, scaffolding, hoarding, general office overheads, materials management

Hendrickson (industrial) estimates progress from design estimates -> bid estimates -> control estimates. Faithful+Gould (building) estimates progress from conceptual phase -> schematic -> design development phase -> procurement phase -> cost control function. Project Planning

Planning includes designing the structure and sequence of activities as well as estimating the resource requirements to complete each activity. Planning requires estimating, analysis, and balance of trade-offs. The schedule provides a benchmark against which progress can be measured and therefore is an essential aspect of any planning task. In planning, begin with end result and identify steps required to yield that result (Sherlock Holmes).

Steps to plan:

  1. Define the work tasks or activities (Work Breakdown Structure WBS)
    • Types of activities: Production/Construction relate directly to the physical effort, Procurement: arranging for the aquisition of materials, equipment, labour, and money, Management: support or administrative tasks related to the project
    • Work breakdown structure are broken down by area, system, or trade.
  2. Choose the method and technology (drawings and specifications)
  3. Define precedence relationships between activities (contract time frames, budget, milestones)
    • identify the order in which the activities within a project need to take place. Precedence defined by physical logic, spatial constraints, resource constraints, and skill constraints
    • Draw with AON (Activity on Node) diagram.
      • Independent nodes don't have arrows, but dependent activities are depicted with consecutive arrows.
      • There will need to be a starting "dummy point" and an ending "dummy point"
      • Assumes no resource requirements
      • A merge and burst relationship might exist. This occurs when two nodes must both precede an activity (they merge to that activity). Then, that activity is required for several other activity (it burst to multiple)
  4. Estimate durations and resource requirements (based on previous projects)
    • D=AP⋅ND=\frac{A}{P\cdot{N}}D=P⋅NA​, with A being amount of work, P is average productivity/crew and N is number of crews, D is duration
    • Other factors that influence durations are learning curves, specific crew leaders, trade stacking, weather, and work hours per day
  5. Name activities (based on historical data)
  6. Assign responsibilities (materials, equipment, crews)

Project Scheduling

To create project schedules, there are some practical aspects. The first is rough diagramming on paper. This looks at history and may use post-it notes. It is likely that owners set milestones, the GC ask for schedules from SCs, and the GC merges schedules constrained by milestones. Feedback from subcontractors is given to the owner.

There are several methods to schedule: gantt, critical path method, short interval planning, and toolbox meetings

The Critical Path Plan is presented by GC to owner at the beginning of the project, and presented in greater detail later.

Short interval planning includes weekly meetings between project manager and key project participants. It involves reviewing recently completed tasks, analyzing delay causes, setting goals for next 5-15 days, momitoring progress, and allocating resources

Critical Path Method

D: duration of an activity, ES (early start): earliest time at which an activity can start, EF (early finish): the earliest time that the activity can finish, LF (late finish): latest time that activity can finish

Not only are there characteristics of activities, but also of relationships between them. Start-start indicates that B can't start until A is started, start-finish indicates that B can't finish until A has started, finish-start indicates that B can't start until A has finished, and finish-finish indicates that B can't finish until A has finished

  1. Forward Pass: to find the earliest possible start time and finish time for each activity
  2. Backward Pass: to find the latest possible time to start and end each activity

Total float (TF): amount of time by which start or finish of an activity may be delayed without causing the project to last longer. TF=LS−ES=LF−EFTF=LS-ES=LF-EFTF=LS−ES=LF−EF

Free float (FF): amount of time by which start or finish of an activity may be delayed without delaying early start of succeeding activities. FF=min ES of following activities−EF of activity in questionFF=\textrm{min ES of following activities}-\textrm{EF of activity in question}FF=min ES of following activities−EF of activity in question. TF≥FFTF\geq{FF}TF≥FF

Critical path: longest paths in the network. Delay of any activities on the critical path will delay the project finish time. FF=TF=0FF=TF=0FF=TF=0

Project Scheduling - Resource Levelling and Allocation

Levelling resources means to have a stable work condition without increasing the total project duration. Activities can only be moved within their float time. Resource allocation has limited resources and therefore one or more activities need to be delayed in order to make the schedule practical and feasible. Also increases project duration.

The advantages to the worker profile is that there isn't an excessive amount of workers on board, and thus costs are minimized. Peak work force is when there is the most people. Thus, hiring and firing doesn't need to take place in the middle of the project, but at the beginning and end.

The advantages to the equipment profile is that there are no additional costs. It is only on site when it is needed.

To design a worker resource profile:

  1. Schedule all critical activities first, so that you know what your absolute requirements will be
  2. During the upward sloping section of the resource profile, delay the non-critical activities towards their LS date
  3. Once the maximum of the sloping line is achieved, start as many non-critical activities to establish the zero-slope line
  4. Towards the end of the project, make sure the non-critical activities are finished towards their EF time and then start to decrease the number of workers to produce the downward sloping line to the end of the project.

A moment around the horizontal axis is used as a metric for levelling. Mx=∑1n(Resource Demand)2M_x=\sum_1^n{(\textrm{Resource Demand})^2}Mx​=∑1n​(Resource Demand)2. The lower moment indicates less fluctuations. A moment around y-axis ∑1n(Resource Demand)×Days\sum_1^n{(\textrm{Resource Demand})}\times{\textrm{Days}}∑1n​(Resource Demand)×Days indicates better utilization for time.

Other objectives include minimum idle times, minimum hires and fires, maximum productivity, minimum cost, minimum duration, and maximum quality. If one is going for shortest duration, costs may increase (crashed). This can be through overtime wages, quality compromise, accidents risk, and space limitations. If cost is to be minimized, duration can go long.

To design an equipment profile, try to minimize the days it is on site.

Good rule-of-thumb (heuristic) approach to time-cost trade off:

  • shorten critical path activities
  • note emerging additional critical paths that result and increasing numbers of activities that must be simultaneously shortened to shorten project duration
  • optimal schedule however requires examination of every possible combination

Linear Programming

A linear system is a model of a system based on some kind of linear operator. Linear systems satisfy the property of linear transformation. It is widely used because it states optimization problems in universal form:

  • Objective of a single goal
  • Constraints must be satisfied by the solution
  • Decision variables are to be identified to optimize the objective
  • Parameters are involved

To solve (Example):

  1. Identify decision variables, constraints, objective, and parameters
  2. Set up a table
  3. Notate the constraints and create an objective function
  4. Maximize graphically or analytically

To solve graphically (2 decision variables), the constraint lines are plotted and the objective function is graphed according to the constraint. Taking a ruler and moving it up or down shows it's maximum and minimum values.

The feasible region is convex (any point on the segment between 2 feasible points is feasible), compact (the boundary is considered feasible), and continuous. The optimum point is on the boundary.

Binding constraints: when the constraints limit the objective function

Non-binding constraints: when constrains have no effect on the optimal solutions

Unique solution: only one extreme point. Alternate Optima: solution contains at least 2 extreme points. Infeasible: no feasible region Unbounded: not enough constraints

Slack variables help find which solutions are feasible and which intersection to try next. If S is positive, the constraint has not been used it, but if it is negative, the constraint has been exceeded. To solve, the following sumplex algorithm can be used:

  1. Find initial solution
  2. Move to adjacent corner point that improves objective function
  3. Repeat until no more improvement possible

Sensitivity Analysis

There are two kinds of sensitivity analysis: right-hand side and objective function

RHS: change in one constraint with all other imput parameters unchanged

OF: change in the value of on objective function criterion with all other input parameters unchanged

The shadow price is the marginal cost increass/decrease when switching optimal solutions by OF sensitivity

Multiple-objective

When trying to maximize different objectives that are inflict, it is considered multi-objective. When they have different units, it is non-commensurate

Multi-objectives have the same formulation to classical programs. However, the objective has a different expression of objective function. The goal is not to identify the best solution, but the quantify the degree of conflict or trade-off amongst the objectives

Non-inferiority indicates that there are multiple points that are non-inferior to each other. At each corner, one can check if other points dominate or not

Weighting Method: Grand Objective Function is to be maximized. The number of different sets of weights (number of linear programs to be solved) depends on the complexity of the trade-off surface
