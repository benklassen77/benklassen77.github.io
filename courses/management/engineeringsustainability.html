Climate: long-term average of weather

Weather: short term variation in the long term climate

Climate Change: change in the statistical distribution of weather patterns when that change lasts for an extended period of time

  • caused by anthropogenic (human) processes, solar radiation, plate tectonics, and volcanic eruptions
  • the earth's climate is governed by the balance between absorption of solar radiation and emission of infrared emissions
  • any imposed alteration in eather term is called a radiative forcing
  • following a radiative forcing, the temperatures of the Earth's surface and atmosphere will naturally adjust to bring solar absooption and infrared emission back into balance

Greenhouse effect: co2, methane, vapor, and other gases absorb heat energy into the atmosphere. These gases are emitted from anthropogenic and natural sources. Known as greenhosue gases

  • greenhouse is a glass enclosed structure used to grow plants, warmer inside than outside
  • greenhouse allows sun to come in, but blocks it outward
    • greenhouse gases do the same
  • Greenhouse effect refers to the partial absorption by certain gases in the atmosphere of infrared ratiation emitted by the Earth's surface. The absorption has a warming effect, whether solar or infrared, and this makes the climate warmer than it would be otherwise. Some artificial GHG (halocarbons like CFCs, HFCs, and HCFC) developed as alternatives to ozone-depleting substances. Also SF6 and other hydrocarbons

Air pollutant: any chemical or substance that is present in the atmosphere in quantities that negatively impact human health, the environment, and the economy or unreasonably interfere w/ the enjoyment of life, property, or recreation

  • CO, NO2. O3, SO2, PM, Pb, and VOCs
    • Primary pollutant: emitted directly into the atmosphere. Eg. NOx
    • Secondary pollutant: created by various physical processes and chemical reactions that take place in the atmosphere. For example, Ozone is created by chemical reaction of VOCs and NOx
  • major source of air pollutants is fossil fuels. Fossil fuels are mainly hydrocarbons
  • most fuels have other elements like N,S,Pb,and Hg that are unburnable

Criteria pollutants

CO, NO2. O3, SO2, PM, and Pb are criteria pollutants. Only air pollutants with national air quality standards that define allowable concentrations of these substances in ambient air

What makes energy sustainable? It needs to be safe, affordable, and abundant

Energy Poverty: an economic condition under which families are unable to access or afford commercially provided energy. Families rely on charcoal, batteries etc.

Energy Security: ability of a nation to protect itself from the economic, political, and social disruptions of an interrupted supply of a critical energy resource

Energy Cycle

  • the path from extraction to consumption
  • the energy cycle considers the total path of energy from its extraction in raw form to its processing and distribution and finally to its point of consumption
  • at each step in the energy cycle, we both consume energy and create negative environmental impacts
  • the benefit of energy production depends on the net energy; the amount of usable energy at the final step, less the energy used in production and transport

Energy is extracted -> distributed for processing -> converted to consumption form -> distributed to customers -> requires infrastructure for distribution

  • main challenge for energy chain is the efficiency. If a power plant is 35% efficient, and a lightbulb is 5% efficient, the total efficiency is 1.75%

Mitigating Negative Environmental Impacts

Major negative environmental byproducts of electricity production from fossil fuels are PM (particulate matter), SO2SO_2SO2​ (sulfur dioxide) and NOxNO_xNOx​ (Nitrogen Oxides)

PM Mitigation

  • particulates are removed from power plant emissions through the use of electrostatic precipitators (ESP)
  • the emission gas is bombarded w/ negative ions as it flows between two positively charged plates. The PM becomes negatively charged and is attracted to the plates at a speed of w ms\frac{m}{s}sm​
  • in contemporary systems, efficiencies are reaching >99%

SO2SO_2SO2​ Mitigation

  • chemical methods known as scrubbers are used to remove the SO2SO_2SO2​ from FFPP emissions. A limestone slurry is sprayed into the output flow, creating a chemical reaction resulting in gypsum. This can be used for drywall

NOxNO_xNOx​ Mitigation

  • oxides of N from emission gases are converted to nitrogen through the introduction of ammonia NH3NH_3NH3​ and oxygen

Solid Waste Managements

Solid waste is either hazardous or non-hazardous

Hazardous: has toxic, infectious, radioactive, or flammable properties and poses an actual or potential hazard to:

  • humans or other organisms
  • environment
  • in Canada, wastes are defined by concentration, toxicity to humans, and to ecosystems
  • Basel ban took place

Solid Waste Management System Objectives

  • to minimize the production of solid waste
  • to prevent the production or enhance the treatment of hazardous waste products
  • minimize negative impacts of solid on the environment
  • minimize negative externalities associated with solid waste treatment, like odour, environment degradation, and land value impacts
  • minimize energy consumption associated with the production and treatment of waste
  • minimize the cost of treatment

Lansink's Ladder: one of the first waste hierarchies proposed by Ad Lansink in 1979

  • Prevention is about avoiding the initial use of resources

Leachate: mixture of water, other liquids, and solids entering natural waterways from the landfill area. It contains zinc, mercury, and other metals as well as hydrocarbons such as benzene and xylene

Leachate Treatment

  • the amount of leachate produced is reduced by covering (capping) to reduce precipitation entering landfill
  • liner (typically clay)

Impacts of landfill on Air Quality

  • emission of landfill gases (LFG)
  • (LFG) such as CO2CO_2CO2​, CH4CH_4CH4​, and NHxNH_xNHx​
  • 2 phases: aerobic and anaerobic for when landfill has and doesn't have oxygen
  • Methane dramatically increases during the anaerobic stage
  • CO2CO_2CO2​ is produced during aerobic processes

Treating LFG

  • landfill gases may be collected (actively or passively) and burned to produce electricity
  • LFG can be collected and burned or flared. Methane can be flared to produce CO2CO_2CO2​
  • reuse of landfill sites is limited to recreational activities
  • 50% of Canada's landfills have less than 10 years remaining

Incineration

  • solid waste is burned, typically producing energy. This reduces the V of solid waste to be treated by other means
  • in Canada 2000, 21 incinerators burned 5% of all waste
  • Mass burn system: least sophisticated system in terms of presorting. Just burns it
  • Starved air system: 2 stages. First chamber is stoichiometrically (Pressure, O content) controlled to slowly convert solid waste to non-compustible materials
  • Fluidized bed system: combustion occurs in a chamber beneath a thin bed of sand-like material; the flue gas produced passes through and raises the bed material causing it to act like a fluid. The bed material acts as a filter
  • Refuse derived fuel RFD: solid waste is converted to a fuel which is then used as a power source in other industry applications
  • Plasma System: solids are converted to gaseous state due to high temperatures

Incineration Impacts: Water Quality

  • incineration plants use water based technologies like scrubbers to reduce air pollutant entering the atmosphere. Runoff from this must be controlled

Incineration Impacts: Air Quality

  • impacts air quality through gases, but is lower than alternatives

Incineration Impacts: Solid Waste

  • Ash is produced. This can be used in concrete, road, or landfill
  • Potential for energy: energy derived is a function of heat value (HV in kJ/kg) of the chemical and its carbon content
  • Some components in the waste stream have high HV, like plastics
  • Economical comparison (life cycle analysis) between recycling and incinerating

Recycling

  • process by which goods/materials are removed from the disposal waste stream and reprocessing into a usable form
  • most common recycled materials are organics, metals, glasses, and plastics
  • prevents landfill disposal, CO2CO_2CO2​ emissions
  • Nyle Ludolph (1983) started Blue Box recycling from Kitchener, ON

Composting

  • breaking down the organic materials from the waste stream by aerobic or anaerobic microorganisms
  • usually done in 2 steps: stabilization period of 1-2 weeks, and a final curing period of 6-12 weeks

Composting Efficiency

  • temperature of the composting material (30ºC desirable)
  • oxygen content (15% desirable)
  • C-N 30:1 ratio
  • moisture content (above 50% desirable)

Negatives to composting

  • CO2CO_2CO2​ production
  • odour
  • greater energy consumption than landfills

Decision making for managing waste

  • goals for minimizing total cost of treatment, and maximizing the amount of diversion
  • optimization problems solved by operations research (OR) or linear programming

  1. Determine equations of constraints, as well as non-negativity constraints
  2. Solve for the feasible region
  3. Find the point of highest profit
    • This will always be at one of the extrema of the feasible region
