Lecture 1 - What is Economics & Engineering Decision Making

Defining Economics

Economics (1935): the science which studies human behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have alternative uses

Microeconomics: the study of how individual units (people or businesses) allocate their scarce resources, and the consequences of their decisions for markets and the entire economy

  • Welface economics is concerned w/ impacts of change on the well-being of various groups in the economy
  • Transportation economics is concerned w/ society's use of scarce resources for the movement of goods and people from point A -> B
  • Environmental economics is concerned w/ economic consequences of policies aimed at pricing pollution etc.
  • Ecological economics is concerned w/ placing value on nature over human needs
  • Urban economics is concerned w/ economic issues in cities like real estate markets, housing, poverty, and transportation. It has spatial dimension and agglomeration effects
  • Engineering economics is concerned w/ analyzes techniques to choose the best option from a bunch

Macroeconomics: the study of the economy as a whole, using simplified aggregate quantities to analyze the complete interactions of the economy

  • eg. GPA

Course objectives:

  1. Understand how to choose the most economically efficient project
  2. Understand when to make replacements
  3. Understand how to make public-sector choices

This understanding comes from:

  • cash flow analysis
  • comparison methods
  • project financing
  • sensitivity analysis

Engineering Decision Making

  • projects use materials, money, labour, and time
  • projects can be undertaken in different ways
  • most economical is important
    • awareness of financial, environmental, social, and polical constraints is also essential

  • facts are used by influential personalities and powerful individuals to develop beliefs about what is best, leading to a decision
  • in theory, facts should control politics, people and preferences. However, sometimes a decision is made and facts are searched for after the fact
  • engineering economics provide facts about a decision

Dealing w/ Abstractions

  • simplifying the real world is necessary for any engineering analysis

  • model is a simplification, and is only appropriate for a given purpose
  • think about whether model assumptions are appropriate for a given purpose
  • if we include everything in the model, it is real world and overcomplicated. By definition, models are simplifications of the real world
  • we don't have precise values for some quantities
    • Sensitivity Analysis involves assessing the effect of uncertainty on a decision
    • a range of values can be assessed w/ reasonable certainty
    • accessing robustness is important to determine how sensitive decisions are to different factors in order to build confidence

Lecture 2 - Time Value of Money

One dollar today is worth more than one dollar in the future. Why?

  1. It can be invested for productive use
    • Eg. livestock will naturally grow. Thus, if I were to lend 10 livestock, I might want 12 back
  2. People must be compensated for lending money due to lost opportunity and risk
    • Eg. Financiers risked their money on overseas trade
    • Loan shark: a person who charges a high rate of interest to loan people money
  3. Interest

Interest

Interest is the difference between money lent and money repaid

F=P+Pi(Future Amount)F=P+Pi\tag{Future Amount} F=P+Pi(Future Amount)

  • F = future amount
  • P = present amount
  • i = interest rate

Simple interest: interest earned in a period is not added to the principle amount in the next period. In other words, no interest on interest. Is=PiNI_s=PiNIs​=PiN, where N is the the number of periods. Grows linearly

Compound interest: interest is added to the principle amount after each period. Ic=P(1+i)n−PI_c=P(1+i)^n-PIc​=P(1+i)n−P. Grows exponentially

Interest rate may be stated for some period (eg. years) but based on shorter compounding periods (eg. months)

  • Nominal interest rate: conventional method of stating annual interest rate. Eg. brochure
    • calculated by multiplying the interest rate per sub-period isi_sis​ by the number of periods per year (m), r=is⋅mr=i_s\cdot{m}r=is​⋅m
    • Eg. 12% per year nominal is 1% per month. However, if it compounds, you are actually paying excess of 12% per year
  • Effective interest rate: actual, but not usually stated interest rate, for a one-year compounding period.

Depreciation

  • as time passes, normal assets (equipment, buildings) lose value or depreciate
  • some things appreciate
  • while buildings depreciate in quality, land price can appreciate strongly

Reasons for Depreciation (3 major causes)

  1. Use-related physical loss. Commonly referred to as wear and tear
  2. Time-related physical loss. Eg. Unused cars can rust
  3. Functional loss. Eg. Apple software updates

It is important to know the value of assets at any given time. Given by:

  • Market Value: actual value that asset can be sold for in an open market. Requires sale
  • Book Value: value of an asset for account purposes, as calculated with a depreciation model
  • Salvage Value: value of an asset at the end of useful life (sold after for further use)
  • Scrap value: value of an asset at the end of physical life (broken up afterwards)

Straight-line Depreciation: assumes rate of loss in value constant (linear) over useful life ($/year).

Engineers usually try to simplify cash flows:

  • Discrete models: all cash flows and compounding occur at the ends of periods (eg. months or years)
    • there will be some inaccuracy

Discrete Compounding

  • formulas that define mathematical equivalence for four common cash flow patterns
  1. Money received: single disbursement (money spent) or receipt
  2. Annuity: set of equal disbursement or receipts over a sequence of periods
    • when you draw annuity, no cash flow at point 0
  3. Arithmetic gradient series: set of receipts/disbursements that Δ\DeltaΔ by a constant amount from one period to the next
    • when you draw arithmetic gradient, no cash flow at point 0 or 1
  4. Geometric gradient series: set of receipts/disbursements that Δ\DeltaΔ by a constant proportion from one period to the next

Non-Standard Annuities and Gradients

When payment period and compounding period are not the same:

  1. Treat each cash flow individually
  2. Change the compounding period to match the payment period
    • use the effective interest rate, ie=(1+is)m−1i_e=(1+i_s)^m-1ie​=(1+is​)m−1
  3. Determine an equivalent annuity for the compounding period
    • use the sinking fund factor (A/F,i,N)
