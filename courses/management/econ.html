Lecture 1 - What is Economics & Engineering Decision Making

Defining Economics

Economics (1935): the science which studies human behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have alternative uses

Microeconomics: the study of how individual units (people or businesses) allocate their scarce resources, and the consequences of their decisions for markets and the entire economy

  • Welface economics is concerned w/ impacts of change on the well-being of various groups in the economy
  • Transportation economics is concerned w/ society's use of scarce resources for the movement of goods and people from point A -> B
  • Environmental economics is concerned w/ economic consequences of policies aimed at pricing pollution etc.
  • Ecological economics is concerned w/ placing value on nature over human needs
  • Urban economics is concerned w/ economic issues in cities like real estate markets, housing, poverty, and transportation. It has spatial dimension and agglomeration effects
  • Engineering economics is concerned w/ analyzes techniques to choose the best option from a bunch

Macroeconomics: the study of the economy as a whole, using simplified aggregate quantities to analyze the complete interactions of the economy

  • eg. GPA

Course objectives:

  1. Understand how to choose the most economically efficient project
  2. Understand when to make replacements
  3. Understand how to make public-sector choices

This understanding comes from:

  • cash flow analysis
  • comparison methods
  • project financing
  • sensitivity analysis

Engineering Decision Making

  • projects use materials, money, labour, and time
  • projects can be undertaken in different ways
  • most economical is important
    • awareness of financial, environmental, social, and polical constraints is also essential

  • facts are used by influential personalities and powerful individuals to develop beliefs about what is best, leading to a decision
  • in theory, facts should control politics, people and preferences. However, sometimes a decision is made and facts are searched for after the fact
  • engineering economics provide facts about a decision

Dealing w/ Abstractions

  • simplifying the real world is necessary for any engineering analysis

  • model is a simplification, and is only appropriate for a given purpose
  • think about whether model assumptions are appropriate for a given purpose
  • if we include everything in the model, it is real world and overcomplicated. By definition, models are simplifications of the real world
  • we don't have precise values for some quantities
    • Sensitivity Analysis involves assessing the effect of uncertainty on a decision
    • a range of values can be assessed w/ reasonable certainty
    • accessing robustness is important to determine how sensitive decisions are to different factors in order to build confidence

Lecture 2 - Time Value of Money

One dollar today is worth more than one dollar in the future. Why?

  1. It can be invested for productive use
    • Eg. livestock will naturally grow. Thus, if I were to lend 10 livestock, I might want 12 back
  2. People must be compensated for lending money due to lost opportunity and risk
    • Eg. Financiers risked their money on overseas trade
    • Loan shark: a person who charges a high rate of interest to loan people money
  3. Interest

Interest

Interest is the difference between money lent and money repaid

F=P+Pi(Future Amount)F=P+Pi\tag{Future Amount} F=P+Pi(Future Amount)

Simple interest: interest earned in a period is not added to the principle amount in the next period. In other words, no interest on interest. Is=PiNI_s=PiNIs​=PiN, where N is the the number of periods. Grows linearly

Compound interest: interest is added to the principle amount after each period. Ic=P(1+i)n−PI_c=P(1+i)^n-PIc​=P(1+i)n−P. Grows exponentially

Depreciation

  • as time passes, normal assets (equipment, buildings) lose value or depreciate
  • some things appreciate
  • while buildings depreciate in quality, land price can appreciate strongly

Reasons for Depreciation (3 major causes)

  1. Use-related physical loss. Commonly referred to as wear and tear
  2. Time-related physical loss. Eg. Unused cars can rust
  3. Functional loss. Eg. Apple software updates

It is important to know the value of assets at any given time. Given by:

  • Market Value: actual value that asset can be sold for in an open market. Requires sale
  • Book Value: value of an asset for account purposes, as calculated with a depreciation model
  • Salvage Value: value of an asset at the end of useful life (sold after for further use)
  • Scrap value: value of an asset at the end of physical life (broken up afterwards)

Comparison Methods

Project relations and the MARR

Investing: giving something up for something greater later

  • engineers often refer to investment opportunities as projects
  • we need to make these assumptions:
    • for now, it is assumed that sufficient funds are available
    • costs and benefits are measurable in terms of money
    • taxes are not applicable
    • cash flows are unaffected by inflation (constant prices assumed)
    • future cash flows are known with certainty

Relations Among Projects

Independent: expected costs and benefits of each project do not depend on whether the other one is chosen

Mutually exclusive: if one is chosen, all other alternatives are excluded

  • Evaluation: 1st step is to determine which projects are economically acceptable, 2nd step is to compare economically acceptable projects to each other to select best project

Related, but not mutually exclusive: expected costs and benefits of one project depend on whether the other one is chosen

  • for this case, all the possible combinations should be listed out. The best combination will be chosen and treated as mutually exclusive
  • if we have n related projects, they can be turned into a set of mutually exclusive projects with 2n2^n2n options

Contingent: a project cannot be taken unless another project is taken first

  • do nothing is an option unless specified

Minimum Acceptable Rate of Return (MARR): interest rate that must be earned for any project to be considered acceptable

Comparisons of Worth

Present Worth (PW): compares projects by computing the equivalent present value of the project's cashflows at the MARR

  • it is the profit of the project relative to their MARR. Thus, could make money but it would be considered a loss since it is less than the MARR
  • present worth of any money invested at the MARR is 0, since the present worth of future receipts would exactly offset the current disbursement
  • If PW>0, acceptable; PW=0 is marginally acceptable; PW<0 is unacceptable
  • There is usually a do nothing comparison

Annual Worth: compares projects by transforming all disbursements and receipts of the projects to a uniform series (annuity) at the MARR

  • comparison of 2 projects by the PW and AW methods will always indicate the same preferred alternative
  • AW comparisons can sometimes be more easily grasped mentally. When there is no clear justification, it is reasonable to use the one that requires less conversion

Future Worth (FW): desirable to use in cases where money is being saved since the actual dollar value in the future has a particular meaning

Comparison w/ Unequal Lives

When making PW comparisons, we must use the same time period in order to take into account the full costs and benefits of each alternative.

For unequal lives:

  1. Use repeated lives: repeat the service life of each alternative to arrive at the lowest common multiple of the lives for all alternatives
  2. Adopt a specified study period: necessitates an additional assumption about salvage value (may be difficult)

PW comparison can be done easier by first calculating AWs, and then converting AWs over the lowest common multiple of the service lives to PWs, such that PW=AW(PA,MARR,LCM)PW = AW(\frac{P}{A},MARR,LCM)PW=AW(AP​,MARR,LCM)

Payback Period: a rough measure of the time it takes for an investment to pay for itself (interest rate is assumed to be zero)

P=First costAnnual Savings(Payback Period)P = \frac{\textrm{First cost}}{\textrm{Annual Savings}}\tag{Payback Period} P=Annual SavingsFirst cost​(Payback Period)

  • investments with shorter payback periods are preferable
  • suitable as a preliminary filter before evaluating AW or PW. For example, one might only accept projects with a PP of 7 years or less

Advantages of payback period:

  • easy to understand and calculate
  • focuses on need to recover capital quickly (businesses can't tie up their money in long-term projects)
  • future is uncertain

Disadvantages of payback period:

  • naturally discriminates against long-term projects
  • disregards cash flows that occur after the payback period
  • ignores the time-value of money

In order to consider the time-value of money, use the discounted payback period.

P=First costDiscounted Annual Savings(Discounted Payback Period)P = \frac{\textrm{First cost}}{\textrm{Discounted Annual Savings}}\tag{Discounted Payback Period} P=Discounted Annual SavingsFirst cost​(Discounted Payback Period)

  • one can isolate for (P/A,i,N), and then solve for N

  • the projects with higher IRR is not necessarily the one which should be accepted
  • projects with a lower i∗i^{*}i∗ can still have a higher profit

The excel function IRR() will yield the IRR for a range of costs

Procedure for analysis:

  1. Rank projects from 1 to n in increasing order based on first cost. First one will be smallest first cost (likely no change)
  2. Next highest-ranked project considered the challenger. One needs to first ask if the IRR on the incremental investment is greater than the MARR.
    • if it isn't greater than the MARR, it can be discarded. The current best is the best overall alternative
    • if the IRR on the incremental investment is greater than the MARR, it becomes the current champion

Project Financing

Private Sector Financing

  • it is possible that sufficient funds are not available

2 ways companies finance projects: debt and equity

Debt: money contractually borrowed from a bank, or raised through issueing bonds

  • Loan: borrower usually liable to pay interest on the loan until the entire debt is repaid
  • Bond: issuer owes the holders a debt and is usually obliged to pay them interest or to repay the principal at a later date, the maturity date. Difference between bond and loan is that loan is ongoing. These were first introduced to fund WW1 and WW2

Equity: involves selling shares of ownership

  • is riskier to investors, since they are only paid if the company first makes its debt payments
  • hence, debt typically has a lower interest rate than equity. Debt is preferred by companies

Company size and sources of capital

Large companies can secure capital both by borrowing and by selling ownership shares

WACC: weighted average cost of capital. Weighted average between equity and debt

  • all companies are constrained: the more debt they take on, the riskier they become, and hence lenders require higher rates of return
  • a company's investments are determined by a trade-off between a rising WACC curve and a falling IRR curve for potential projects

ke(ED+E)+kd(DE+D)(WACC)k_e(\frac{E}{D+E})+k_d({D}{E+D})\tag{WACC} ke​(D+EE​)+kd​(DE+D)(WACC)

  • kek_eke​ is cost of equity. Basically percent return shareholders want (%)
  • kdk_dkd​ is after-tax cost of debt (%). %bankrate⋅1−%taxrate\%_{bankrate}\cdot{1-\%_{taxrate}}%bankrate​⋅1−%taxrate​
  • E is equity portion of funding in dollars
  • D is debt portion of funding in dollars
  • the WACC typically becomes a company's MARR

Opportunity cost: cost of best opportunity forgone. it is the best rate of return on a rejected project

Small companies raising capital may be difficult (high risk)

  • Bootstrapping: rely entirely on the capital of the foundders/owners, friends and family
  • Bank loans are usually the source of capital with the lowest cost. However, they will not finance without personal guarantees
  • Stock exchanges specialize in small, speculative companies
  • Venture capitalists require the expectation of at least a 35% rate of return after taxes
  • Angel investors are wealthy individuals that have expertise in your area
  • Government grants and credits (OCE, IRAP, SR&ED tax credits) are big for start-up success
  • Customer prepayments

Debt vs. Equity (Investor's perspective)

Debt

  • repayment required
  • regular payments
  • interest rate
  • can take security over assets
  • gain is limited to interest

Equity

  • no ongoing payments
  • no security
  • 'gain' is unlimited

Public Sector Financing

Government guidelines

  • Provincial and federal (14 000 000 and 35 000 000 ppl respectively)
    • Revenue has no meaningful limits
    • Expenses are defined in the constitution
  • Municipal
    • Revenue limited by province
    • Expenses limited by province

Federal Project funding

  • very simple
  • all revenue goes into one fund
  • all projects funded from this fund

Provincial Project funding

  • more nuanced than federal funding
  • all revenue goes into one fund
  • some transfers from Canada are intended for specific types of projects
  • unspoken that province needs to spend money from federal level on specified projects. However, they are not technically required

Municipal Project funding

  • revenue is raised for specific goals
  • revenue either goes into tax-supported or fee-supported budgets
    • tax-supported uses property taxes
    • fee-supported relies on user fees
  • budgets are subdivided into operating and capital projects
    • operating: everyday expenses
    • capital: reconstruction or new builds

  • User fee is direct fee on a good or service
    • bus fare, parking fee, water bill
  • Property taxes: tax collected from property owners
    • typically the only tax collected by municipal governments (Toronto an exception)
    • Applied to almost all municipal programs
  • Development Charge: fee collected from new development
    • only allowed to be collected on brownfield or greenfield builds. Greenfield: new property built where there wasn't something before. Brownfield: building on previously developed land
  • Debt (debentures): loans that must be repaid with interest
    • not a true source of revenue, since it must be repaid
    • cities have a debt repayment limit, or limit of amount of debt that can be pulled out. Limit is 25% of non-transfer revenues.
    • non-transfer revenue: is revenue that government earns by itself without getting money from other people
    • debt cannot be used for operating costs
  • Intergovernmental transfers: transfers from the fed/provincial government
    • common for large capital projects
    • sometimes 33-33-33, sometimes 50(Canada)-25-25. Eg. ION was 33-33-33

Common federal/provincial revenue

  • income tax (residents)
  • health tax (residents)
  • gas tax (drivers)
  • sales tax (consumers)

Types of goods

  • Public good
    • Non-rivalrous: when provided to one person, others can receive it at no additional cost. Eg. lighthouse
    • Non-excludable: unable to prevent people who don't pay
  • Private good
    • Rivalrous: consumption by one person prevents providing the same good to others
    • Excludable: able to prevent people who don't pay

User Pay Principle: theory in public economics to tie revenues to appropriate expenditures

  • Taxes -> public goods
  • User fees -> private goods

Replacement Decisions

Three scenarios:

  1. Defender and challenger are identical and repeat indefinitely
  2. Challenger is different from defender, and repeats indefinitely
  3. Challenger is different from defender, but does not repeat

Replacement scenario 1

Economic life: service life that minimizes the average cost of using an asset. This occurs wherever the derivative of the total cost curve is equal to zero. This is the amount of years until the cost is minimized

Equivalent Annual Cost EAC: annual worth in the context of a replacement study

Replacement

  • cheaper way to get same value (eg. deterioration, technological change)
  • existing asset no longer adequate (insufficient capacity/quality)

Retirement

  • asset is on longer necessary (changes in demands, technology etc.)

Capital Costs: difference between the purchase price and the current salvage value (loss in market value over time)

Installation Costs: includes disruption of production, worker training, reorganization (not reversible, not recovered at a later time)

Operating and Maintenance O&M Costs: includes energy, fuel, periodic servicing, and repairs

  1. Solve Equivalent Annual Cost at different N periods
  2. Choose the smallest value

Replacement scenario 2

Procedure

  1. Determine the economic life of the challenger and its associated EAC
  2. Determine the remaining economic life of the defender and its associated EAC
    • One year principle: can calculate the remaining economic life using one year only if both capital costs are low (true if O&M greater than capital) and if O&M costs are monotonically increasing (continually increasing)
  3. If the EAC of the defender > EAC challenger, replace the defender now. Otherwise, wait

Scenario 3: Irrelevance of Sunk Costs

Sunk Cost: cost that is spent but cannot be recovered. Eg. installation cost

  • should not impact future decisions. This is a sunk cost fallacy Thus, instead of averaging costs over time, use the next year or the next time period's costs as a one-year annuity

Lecture 9 - Public Sector Decision Making

Market economy causes supply and demand fluctuations. Companies owned by private owners

Planned economy, supply and demand doesn't affect. Government controls supply

Market Price is the price defined by the market. Agreed upon by the consumer and the producer

  • Problem: projects may have effects on individuals or groups external to the firm. These are known as externalities, or a cost or benefit that affects a party who did not choose to incur that cost or benefit. These are not included in the market price
  • Problem: must assign value to social benefits and costs (safety, reduced travel time, loss of habitat, noise, etc)

Market Failure occurs when a market, left on its own, fails to make decisions in which resources are allocated efficiently.

  • Market prices do not reflect all social benefits and costs
  • Aggregate benefits to decision makers and other beneficiaries are less than aggregate costs imposed on all others
  • There is an externality

Reasons for market failure:

  • No market for those affected (acid rain, deforestation)
  • Inadequate information about costs or benefits at the time (emissions, asbestos)
  • Monopolists influence price or quantity
  • Regulations: standards, bans, permits, quotas. Usually backed by penalties for non-compliance
  • Monetary incentives or deterrents: subsides or special tax treatments
  • Legal regulations and litigation: sellers of a product implicitly guarantee that the product is fit for reasonable use
    • Consumer Code (Canada)
    • Class action lawsuit
  • Informal methods: lobbying, demonstrations, boycotting, civil disobedience
  • Education for informed decision making
    • Sin tax increases cost, lowers demand
  • Government provision: police services, health care, infrastructure etc.
    • Public sector analysts take into account all parties affected by a decision through a comprehensive assessment of total costs and benefits of a decision

Government Value of Costs and Benefits

  • Benefit-Cost Analysis BCA widely used in public sector

Eg. costs and benefits with a highway project

Costs

  • construction (P)
  • operations and maintenance (A,G)
  • administrative (P)

Savings

  • disruption of the local environment (habitat fragmentation)

How to asign value to costs and benefits

  • Time-travel disruptions: based on earnings per hour of passengers or the hourly cost of running trucks, value of time (mode choice model)
    • Eg. a person who can earn 35 dollars/hour working should be willing to pay 35/hour for time saved travelling to work
  • Health and Safety: reduction in the number and type of injuries expected, and associated wages, treatment costs, or insurance claims, value of a statistical life VSL.
    • VSL is defined as the additional cost that individuals would be willing to bear for improvements in safety that, in the aggregate, reduce the expected number of fatalities by one.
    • if an individual will pay $ 1000 to reduce the annual risk of death by 1/10000, the VSL is 10M
  • Value of noise/environment
    • Contingent Valuation: ask members of the public what they would be willing to pay
      • Eg. asking people how much they would pay for each minute of reduced travel time
    • Hedonic Prices: deducing valuations based on individuals behaviour in other markets
      • Eg. value of noise due to a busy road may be estimated by examining the selling prices of houses near busy roads and comparing these to prices away from busy roads
  • Consumer surplus: difference between what consumers pay vs. what they are willing to pay
  • Demand Curve: curve or line between cost difference and demand

Inflation

Inflation: increase over time in average prices of goods and services

  • Implication: prices likely to change over the lives of most engineering projects

Cost-push inflation: an equilibrium market price and quantity of production is agreed upon by the supply and demand curves intersection. Inflation occurs when the price increases, and thus that the quantity produced decreases. Supply curve moves left.

  • as quantity produced increases, price increases
  • as price decreases, customer quantity consumed increases

Demand-pull inflation: demand curve shifts right, thus price increases

Built-in inflation: from expectations about future inflation

  • as wage increases, prices increase

Money supply: relative to economic growth in the long term

  • if money supply increases at the same rate as real output, then prices will stay the same

Measuring Inflation

There are several methods to measure inflation rate

  • Laspeyres Index: cost of a fixed basket of commodities from base year at current prices, compared to the cost of basket at base-year prices. This can overestimate inflation, as customers might stop purchasing products that have inflated very highly. Compares to a year ahead in time
  • Paasche Index: cost of a fixed basket of commodities from current year at current prices, compared to the cost of basket at base-year prices. Compares to a year behind in time. This underestimates inflation
  • Fisher Index: geometric mean of both of these indexes
  • In Canada, Consumer Price Index CPI is the average price of a fixed basket of goods in the given period relative to the average price in a base period (Laspeyres-type)
    • basket's goods are subject to change

  • lower inflation provides certainty to worth of money
  • no inflation gives companies an incentive to just hoard money. This can't happen

Dealing w/ Uncertainty

  • important to know how sensitive decisions are to variations in important parameters (eg. prices, interest rates, etc)
  • Sensitivity analysis: determining how different parameter estimates affect decisions/outcomes

Sensitivity Graphs

  1. Begin w/ base case that has the best possible parameter estimates
  2. Vary one parameter above and below the base case, holding all others fixed
  3. Summarize impacts due to those one-at-a-time parameter changes
  4. Graph illustrates which parameters have a significant impact on the decision/outcome

Break-Even Analysis: similar to sensitivity analysis, but identifies the parameter value that causes an outcome to reach a threshold or "break-even" value

  • gives an indication of how much a parameter can change from its original estimate before a threshold is reached
