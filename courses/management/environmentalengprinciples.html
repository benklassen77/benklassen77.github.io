The first step is identification, which includes discovering contaminants threating our health and nature. The next step, quantification, involves creating thresholds of contaminants over which human health can be seriously threatened, called maximum acceptable concentrations (MACs). Quantificaiton is an important process to ensure we are not disclosed to chemicals and pathogens over MACs to ensure protection of humans and nature. The next step, control, involves removing contaminants below MAC to protect humans and nature. Pollutant control means transformation of noxious forms of contaminants into stable, healthy forms. This indicates re-location of contaminants, but we should ensure that relocated contaminants are safe in solid and gas phases. Physical, chemical, and biological methods are used.

Contaminants and concentration

There are three types of contaminants: microbiological, chemical and physical, and radiological contaminants.

Pathogenic microorganimss and viruses are the two types of microbiological contaminants. There are 1400 known species of human pathogens, including viruses, bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and helminths. We cannot measure all of these because it is very expensive and technically challenging. Drinking water is tested for Escherichia coli as an indicator of faecal contamination and is required to have none per 100mL. Enteric viruses require 99.99% reduction (4 log) and protozoa require 99.9% reduction (99.9%).

Hazardous substances that physically and chemically affect human health must be kept below MAC. There are more than 20-80k chemicals being produced, but less than 5-10% are monitored. Drinking water has MAC of 0.2μgL0.2 \frac{\mu{g}}{L}0.2Lμg​ and 0.6μgL0.6 \frac{\mu{g}}{L}0.6Lμg​ for PFOA and PFOS, respectively. There are more than 50 types of PFAS compounds. While there are indicators for microbiological parameters, there are no indicator concepts for physical and chemical parameters

Natural and artificial radionuclides have MACs for drinking water as well. Radioactivity is measured in becquerel (Bq), with 1 Bq=1 event of radiation emission or disintegration per second.

Basic Water Chemistry and Water Quality Parameters

Oxygen Demand (OD): total amount of organic compounds expressed with O2O_2O2​. This is a theoretical amount of organics also called theoretical oxygen demand ThOD. Unit: mg/l O2. As hardness and alkalinity used CaCO3CaCO_3CaCO3​ equivalent, organics use O2O_2O2​ equivalent.

  • A high OD indicates high concentration of organics in water. This indicates organic contaminants in water, such as proliferation of microorganisms. This leads to lack of dissolved oxygen, pathogen issue, and impacts of aquatic animals. Impact is on water resource contamination, drinking water, ecoststems, and recreaetional activities
  • A low OD indicates low concentration of organics in water. This means less organic contaminants in water and less proliferation of microorganisms. This leads to less opportunities for lack of dissolved oxygen, pathogen, or aquatic animal impact. Water resource is protected, drinking water is great, and recreational activities are safe.

Procedure for OD calculation:

  1. Express the mole of organic compound as mole of electrons using half reactions
  2. Express mole of electrons with gram O2O_2O2​ using the O2O_2O2​ half reaction (0.25O2+H++e−=0.5H2O0.25O_2+H^++e^-=0.5H_2O0.25O2​+H++e−=0.5H2​O)
  3. Convert electrons to oxygen knowing 8g O2=1 mol e−8g\textrm{ }O_2=1\textrm{ mol }e^-8g O2​=1 mol e−

To build half reactions for organics:

  1. Balance an organic compound with CO2CO_2CO2​
  2. Balance OOO with H2OH_2OH2​O (or nitrogen with nitrogen source first)
  3. Balance HHH with H+H^+H+
  4. Balance charge with electron
  5. Express the reaction per mole of electrons as reduction form

Major non-point pollution sources include manure, road salt, and sediment. To clean up pollutants after generation, we need to understand contaminant transport. Ideal cases are to control contaminant transport such as domestic wastewater in sewer systems (point source). There are many other cases that aren't controlled (non-point sources like fertilizers, pesticides, manure). NPS create acidification of lake. It is thus essential to understand contaminant transport to protect human and nature from pollution.

Equilibrium

In equilibrium, the forward reaction rate equals the backward reaction rate, with A symbolizing reactions and B products. Thus, kf[A]=kb[B]k_f[A]=k_b[B]kf​[A]=kb​[B]. This is temperature dependent. We then have:

[B][A]=kfkb=Keq(Equilibrium Constant)\frac{[B]}{[A]}=\frac{k_f}{k_b}=K_{eq}\tag{Equilibrium Constant} [A][B]​=kb​kf​​=Keq​(Equilibrium Constant)

Homogeneous chemical equilibria: all reactions of reactants and products occur in the same phase (eg. liquid phase, gas phase). Eg. ionization of acids/bases

Heterogeneous chemical equilibria: reactions occur in two or more phases, such as the solubility of gas in water, chemical precipitation in water, etc. Bubly :)

When reaction rates are very fast, the concentration change of chemical species is negligible. −dCdt=0-\frac{dC}{dt}=0−dtdC​=0. Compounds with a constant concentration over time are said to be at steady state. In this case, we are able to estimate the compound concentration of interest using measured concentration of compounds (reactant or product) and equilibrium constant

There are 3 sizes of contaminants: suspended, colloidal, and dissolved. Before, filtered meant dissolved or soluble. Now, dissolved means less than 3μm3\mu{m}3μm. Suspended means greater than 1.2μm1.2\mu{m}1.2μm. Between that range is colloidal solids, which is mostly filterable.

Clean water is pumped through a screen and goes to the coagulation/flocculation process. This includes rapid mixing and slow mixing together. Here, particle sizes go from 10−3→1μm10^{-3}\to{1\mu{m}}10−3→1μm Then, water goes to sedimentation. The particle sizes become heavier and heavier, and eventually settle. The water then goes through a filter to catch last particles, and then it is disinfected.

Main functions:

  • Screen prevents large substances
  • Coagulation/flocculation/sedimentation catches colloidal particles
  • Filtration catches small particles not being settled down in sedimentation
  • Disinfection catches pathogens

Some processes are done aside from the conventional methods.

  • Adsorption removes contaminants, heavy metals, hardness, emerging contaminants etc.
  • Advanced oxidation removes dissolved contaminants like emerging contaminants
  • Ion exchange removes heavy metals, hardness
  • Membrane filtration removes small particles and microplastics

Suspended contaminents are removed with filtration and sedimentation. Filtration always causes clogging and high operating costs, while sedimentation is economically viable and incomplete. Colloidal contaminants are removed with coagulation/sedimentation (widely used), coagulation/filtration (less economical but high removal), adsorption (effective for eliminating small colloids, expensive), membrane (effective, but relatively expensive). Dissolved contaminants are removed with oxidation-reduction (efficient removal, but expensive), adsorption (selective removal and expensive), and ion exchange (ion removal and expensive).

Microplastics are smaller than 5mm in size. These are found in 93% of bottled water tested in global study. Microplastics include polypropylene, polystyrene, nylon, and polyethylene terephthalate (PET). Average number of plastic particles for every litre of water sold. The colloid is negatively charged, surrounded by ions, very stable in water, and not settled down. This is why colloids are troublemakers in drinking water processes.

Coagulation works to destabilize stable colloids. Stable colloids flocculate with coaggulants to create larger particles. Thus, rapid mixing and slow mixing, followed by sedimentation, is necessary.

The colloid structure has an electrical double layer, EDL. The first layer is the starn layer (Helmholtz layer), and the diffuse layer. Negatively charged particles in water form the two layers. Charge attraction does not dominatately influence movement of anion and cation in diffuse layer - influenced by concentration gradient

The stern layer is a fixed cation layer that binds tightly to the surface of a negatively charged particle. The stern layer behaves as a part of the particle - thickness is 5Å

The diffuse layer is beyond the stern layer in which excessive cations and anions exist together. These cations and anions move under the influence of diffusion. The excessive cations extend out into solution till all the surface charge and electric potential are zero to meet electroneutrality.

EDL = stern layer + diffuse layer. Approximately 300Å, or 0.3μm0.3\mu{m}0.3μm. Since the goal of coagulation is to destabilize stable colloids protected by EDl using positively charged salts, understnding net surface charge of colloids is important for optimization of coagulation

Coagulation and flocculation processes

Particle-particle interactions (DLVO theory) demonstrates what happens. Coagulation is to destabilize negatively charged particles in water. The theory of particle-particle interaction is based on the repulsive and attractive forces on two charged particles.

Electrostatic repulsion: colloidal particles in water have a net negative surface charge, leading to electrostatic repulsion. This repulsion is the principal mechanism for stable particles in water. As a result, net charge is negative. Van der Walls force: attractions to occur between the positively charged nuclei and the electrons of nearly atoms. This attractive force operates only over a very limited distance (0.3-0.6 nm). The question is how to mave van der walls force dominant and repulsion small (negligible)

We know Van der Waal's force starts to govern particle interaction if acting at 0.3-0.6 nm. The stern layer is about 0.5 nm and EDL about 30 nm. Thus, if diffuse layer is removed, the Van der Waal's force can govern. Based on DLVO theory, coagulation is to decrease repulsion and stimulate attraction between particles (particles getting closer together).

The destabilizing mechanism has 4 steps:

  1. Double layer compression
  2. Specific adsorption and charge neutralization
  3. Adsorption and interparticle bridging
  4. Sweeping coagulation

Specific adsorption and charge neutralization: positively charged metals, salts, etc. added. These attach the surface of the solid and neutralize the particle charge. Floccs are formed. If these are added in an overdose amount, net charge becomes positive, which has the same repulsive problems as negative-negative charge

Adsorption and interparticle bridging: reaction occurs when polymeric coagulants (MW:100000-10000000) are used. Very large.

In this reaction, polymers make the bridge bewteen stable particles. These then altogether form floccs

Sweep coagulation (enmeshment): formation of insoluble chemical precipitate with metal salts or polymers. A conceptual model of sweep coagulant describes that particles are incorporated into formation of insoluble precipitates, and removed together with settlement of the insoluble precipitates. When the precipitated molecules settle down, other particles are enmeshed and settle with them. Efficient when number of particles are very small. Has both entrapment and adsorption. Dependent on pH of water and on coagulant dose rate

Coagulation is optimized with four destabilization mechanisms, which do not occur separately but in combined manners. Floc size should be settable (at least 100−200μm100-200\mu{m}100−200μm). Density of flocs should be higher than 1 (eg. alum floc is 1.01 mg/cm3mg/cm^3mg/cm3)

  2. Scenario 2: moderate concentration of particles
  3. Scenario 3: high concentration of particles
  4. Scenario 4: too high concentration of particles

Scenario 1: Destabilization by adsorption and charge neutralization occurs with small dosage of coagulants due to low concentration of particles. Scenario 3: this case is normally called "polluted water". Destabilization by adsorption and charge neutralization occurs and most of the particles are removed.
